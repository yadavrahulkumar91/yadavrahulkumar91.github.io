Describe the pathways followed by corticospinal tract. Enumerate the clinical features due to its lesion.

The corticospinal tract is the primary motor pathway for voluntary movement. It originates in the motor cortex of the brain, descends through the internal capsule, and then travels through the brainstem and spinal cord. The corticospinal tract is divided into the lateral corticospinal tract and the ventral corticospinal tract. The lateral corticospinal tract crosses over to the opposite side of the body at the level of the medulla oblongata, while the ventral corticospinal tract remains ipsilateral. Clinical features of a corticospinal tract lesion include spastic paralysis, hyperreflexia, and Babinski sign. Spastic paralysis is characterized by increased muscle tone and difficulty initiating movement, while hyperreflexia refers to an overactive reflex response. The Babinski sign is a reflex in which the big toe extends and the other toes fan out when the sole of the foot is stimulated. In a corticospinal tract lesion, this reflex becomes pathological and is present even in adults.

5) Write short note on Auditory pathway.

The auditory pathway is responsible for processing sound information and transmitting it from the ear to the brain. It starts with the outer ear, which captures sound waves and directs them into the ear canal. The sound waves then travel to the middle ear, where they vibrate the eardrum and the ossicles, three small bones that amplify the vibrations. These vibrations are then transmitted to the inner ear, where they stimulate hair cells in the cochlea, a snail-shaped structure. The hair cells convert the mechanical vibrations into electrical signals, which are then transmitted to the brain via the auditory nerve. The auditory nerve synapses in the cochlear nucleus of the brainstem, and from there, the auditory information is relayed to various brain regions, including the inferior colliculus, the medial geniculate nucleus, and the auditory cortex in the temporal lobe.

6) Trace the visual pathway and correlate with different types of Anopias.

The visual pathway is responsible for processing visual information and transmitting it from the eyes to the brain. It starts with the retina, where light is converted into electrical signals by photoreceptor cells. These signals are transmitted to the bipolar cells, which in turn transmit them to the ganglion cells. The axons of the ganglion cells converge at the optic disc, where they form the optic nerve. The optic nerve carries the visual information from the eye to the brain. The optic nerve fibers from both eyes then join at the optic chiasm, where they partially cross over to the opposite side of the brain. The fibers that originate from the nasal retina cross over, while the fibers that originate from the temporal retina do not cross over. The fibers then continue on as the optic tract and terminate in the lateral geniculate nucleus in the thalamus. From there, the visual information is transmitted to the primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe. Different types of anopias can occur depending on where along the visual pathway the damage occurs. For example, damage to the optic nerve can result in monocular blindness, while damage to the optic chiasm can result in bitemporal hemianopia. Lesions in the optic tract or lateral geniculate nucleus can result in homonymous hemianopia, where the loss of vision affects one side of the visual field in both eyes.

Axonal transport

Axonal transport is the process by which materials are transported along the length of an axon, the long extension of a neuron. There are two main types of axonal transport: anterograde transport, which moves materials from the cell body toward the axon terminal, and retrograde transport, which moves materials from the axon terminal toward the cell body. This transport occurs along microtubules and is mediated by motor proteins, such as kinesin and dynein, which carry cargo along the microtubules. Axonal transport is essential for the maintenance and function of neurons, as it allows for the transport of important molecules such as proteins, lipids, and organelles, to and from the cell body and axon terminal. IPSP stands for inhibitory postsynaptic potential, which is a type of synaptic potential that makes it harder for the neuron to generate an action potential. It occurs when inhibitory neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, causing an influx of negatively charged ions (such as Cl-) or an efflux of positively charged ions (such as K+). This makes the inside of the neuron more negative or the outside more positive, reducing the likelihood of an action potential being generated.

Differences between local potential and action potential.

Local potentials are graded changes in membrane potential that occur in response to neurotransmitter binding or sensory stimuli. They can be either depolarizing (EPSP) or hyperpolarizing (IPSP) and are conducted decrementally, meaning they lose strength as they travel along the neuron. They can also summate, meaning multiple inputs can add together to reach the threshold for an action potential. In contrast, action potentials are all-or-nothing events that occur when the membrane potential reaches a certain threshold. They are conducted without decrement and have a fixed amplitude and duration. They also involve the opening and closing of voltage-gated ion channels and require a large influx of Na+ followed by an efflux of K+ to repolarize the membrane.

Define a synapse. List the stages in synaptic transmission. Describe briefly synaptic inhibition.

A synapse is a specialized junction between two nerve cells or between a nerve cell and its target, where electrical or chemical signals are transmitted. There are four stages in synaptic transmission:

  1. Presynaptic events: Action potentials arrive at the presynaptic terminal, resulting in the opening of voltage-gated calcium channels and the influx of calcium ions into the terminal.
  2. Neurotransmitter release: The calcium influx triggers the fusion of synaptic vesicles with the presynaptic membrane, releasing neurotransmitter molecules into the synaptic cleft.
  3. Postsynaptic events: The neurotransmitter molecules diffuse across the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, leading to the opening or closing of ion channels and the generation of a postsynaptic potential.
  4. Termination: The neurotransmitter is either taken back up into the presynaptic terminal or broken down by enzymes in the synaptic cleft.

Synaptic inhibition is a process by which the transmission of signals across a synapse is decreased. It can occur through various mechanisms, such as presynaptic inhibition, where the release of neurotransmitter from the presynaptic terminal is reduced, or postsynaptic inhibition, where the responsiveness of the postsynaptic cell to the neurotransmitter is decreased.

Polio vaccine is a vaccine used to prevent poliomyelitis, a viral disease that affects the nervous system. The pathogenesis of rabies involves the replication of the virus in the peripheral nervous system and its subsequent spread to the central nervous system (CNS).
