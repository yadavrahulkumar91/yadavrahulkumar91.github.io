    • Give the anatomical/ embryological basis of shoulder joint dislocation. [2]

    The shoulder joint is a ball and socket joint where the head of the humerus articulates with the glenoid fossa of the scapula. The joint is surrounded by a loose capsule and is stabilized by ligaments, tendons, and muscles. Shoulder joint dislocation can occur due to trauma or injury that causes the humeral head to dislocate from the glenoid fossa.

    List the movements that occur at shoulder joint. Name the muscles that bring about those movements. [2+3 = 5]

    The movements that occur at the shoulder joint are:
    1. Flexion and extension - The muscles responsible for flexion are the anterior fibers of deltoid, pectoralis major, and coracobrachialis, while the muscles responsible for extension are the posterior fibers of deltoid and latissimus dorsi.
    2. Abduction and adduction - The muscles responsible for abduction are the middle fibers of deltoid, supraspinatus, and serratus anterior, while the muscles responsible for adduction are the pectoralis major, latissimus dorsi, and teres major.
    3. Internal and external rotation - The muscles responsible for internal rotation are the subscapularis, teres major, and latissimus dorsi, while the muscles responsible for external rotation are the infraspinatus and teres minor.

    Mention the hip joint under the following heading:

    1. a) Capsule and important ligament

      The hip joint is a ball-and-socket joint formed by the articulation between the femur and the acetabulum of the hip bone. The joint is enclosed by a fibrous joint capsule that is reinforced by three strong ligaments: the iliofemoral, pubofemoral, and ischiofemoral ligaments.

    2. b) Movement of muscles causing those movements.

      The movements at the hip joint include flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, medial and lateral rotation. The flexion is mainly caused by the iliopsoas, rectus femoris, and sartorius muscles, while the extension is caused by the gluteus maximus and hamstrings. The adduction is caused by the adductor magnus, longus, and brevis muscles, and the abduction is caused by the gluteus medius and minimus muscles. The medial and lateral rotation are mainly caused by the gluteus medius and piriformis muscles, respectively.

    3. c) Blood supply and its clinical significance

      The blood supply to the hip joint is mainly provided by the medial and lateral circumflex femoral arteries, which are branches of the deep femoral artery. The clinical significance of this blood supply is that the femoral head is at risk of avascular necrosis when there is a disruption of the blood supply to the femoral head.

    Give the anatomical/embryological basis of Foot drop. [2]

    Foot drop is a condition where the foot is unable to dorsiflex due to damage to the common fibular nerve. The common fibular nerve is derived from the posterior division of the sacral plexus, which originates from the spinal cord segments L4-S3. The nerve passes through the popliteal fossa and wraps around the fibular neck before dividing into the superficial and deep fibular nerves. Damage to this nerve can result from various causes such as trauma, compression, or injury, leading to paralysis of the muscles that dorsiflex the foot.

    Differentiate skeletal and cardiac muscle.

    Skeletal muscle is striated and voluntary in nature, while cardiac muscle is striated and involuntary. Skeletal muscle is attached to the bones of the skeleton and is responsible for body movements, while cardiac muscle forms the walls of the heart and is responsible for pumping blood. Skeletal muscle is under conscious control, while cardiac muscle is controlled by the autonomic nervous system. Skeletal muscle fibers are long and cylindrical, while cardiac muscle fibers are short and branched.

    Explain the microscopic features of skeletal muscle. List the constituents of light and dark bands. [3+2=5]

    Skeletal muscle fibers are made up of myofibrils, which are composed of repeating units called sarcomeres. Each sarcomere contains two types of protein filaments: thick myosin filaments and thin actin filaments. The myosin filaments are located in the center of the sarcomere, while the actin filaments are located on either side of the myosin filaments. The light bands are composed of actin filaments, while the dark bands are composed of myosin filaments. The region where the light and dark bands meet is called the Z-line. The sarcoplasmic reticulum, a specialized type of endoplasmic reticulum, surrounds the myofibrils and stores calcium ions that are required for muscle contraction.

    Explain the sequence of events which causes transmission of impulse across neuromuscular junction.

    The transmission of an impulse across the neuromuscular junction involves a series of events. When a nerve impulse reaches the end of a motor neuron, it triggers the release of a neurotransmitter called acetylcholine. The acetylcholine diffuses across the neuromuscular junction and binds to receptors on the motor end plate of the muscle fiber. This binding causes a change in the permeability of the muscle fiber membrane, which results in the influx of sodium ions and the generation of an action potential. The action potential spreads along the muscle fiber membrane and triggers the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, which initiates muscle contraction.

    Write short notes on: Synpatic properties of habituation and sensitization.

    Habituation and sensitization are two types of synaptic plasticity that allow organisms to adjust their behavioral responses to repeated stimuli. Habituation is the process by which an organism becomes less responsive to a stimulus after repeated exposure. This occurs when synaptic connections between sensory neurons and interneurons in the central nervous system become weaker over time. Sensitization, on the other hand, is the process by which an organism becomes more responsive to a stimulus after repeated exposure. This occurs when synaptic connections between sensory neurons and interneurons become stronger over time. Both habituation and sensitization are important adaptive mechanisms that allow organisms to respond appropriately to environmental stimuli.

    Write short notes on: Synaptic transmission.

    Synaptic transmission is the process by which neurons communicate with each other through specialized junctions called synapses. When an action potential reaches the presynaptic terminal, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft. These neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane and cause a change in the membrane potential, either by depolarization or hyperpolarization. The strength of the synaptic connection between two neurons can be modified by a variety of factors, including changes in the number or sensitivity of receptors, as well as changes in the amount or release of neurotransmitters. Synaptic transmission is a highly dynamic process that underlies many important functions of the nervous system, including learning and memory.

    Explain the changes occurring at the neuromuscular junction in response to an adequate stimulus to the motor nerve. Mention the physiological basis of myasthenia gravis.

    In response to an adequate stimulus to the motor nerve, the nerve impulse triggers the release of acetylcholine from the presynaptic terminal of the motor neuron. Acetylcholine then binds to receptors on the motor end plate of the muscle fiber, which leads to the opening of ion channels and the influx of sodium ions into the muscle fiber. This results in depolarization of the muscle fiber membrane and the generation of an action potential that triggers muscle contraction.

    Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder that affects the neuromuscular junction. The physiological basis of myasthenia gravis is the production of autoantibodies against the acetylcholine receptors located on the muscle endplate. These antibodies block or destroy the receptors, which leads to a reduction in the number of available receptors for acetylcholine to bind to. As a result, the muscle fiber cannot generate enough force to contract, leading to muscle weakness and fatigue. The treatment for myasthenia gravis involves the use of anticholinesterase drugs that increase the amount of acetylcholine available at the neuromuscular junction.

    Define synapse and explain how synaptic transmission occurs. Name any four properties of a synapse.

    A synapse is a specialized junction between two nerve cells, where one neuron (the presynaptic neuron) releases a chemical neurotransmitter that binds to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, transmitting the signal from one neuron to the next. Synaptic transmission occurs when an action potential reaches the axon terminal of the presynaptic neuron, causing the release of neurotransmitter molecules into the synaptic cleft. These neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron, leading to changes in the membrane potential of the postsynaptic neuron that may generate an action potential. Four properties of a synapse are: Plasticity: the ability to change and adapt in response to changes in neuronal activity or experience. Spatial summation: the integration of signals from multiple presynaptic neurons at a single postsynaptic neuron. Temporal summation: the integration of signals from a single presynaptic neuron over time. Synaptic efficacy: the strength of the connection between two neurons and the ability of the synapse to transmit signals.

    Describe the mechanism of skeletal muscle contraction. Explain motor unit and rigor mortis. [3+2=5]

    Skeletal muscle contraction occurs when myosin and actin filaments slide over each other. This sliding is driven by the interaction between myosin heads and actin binding sites. The process is initiated by the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum into the sarcoplasm, which binds to troponin and causes a conformational change that exposes the actin binding sites. This allows myosin heads to bind to the actin and undergo a power stroke, pulling the actin filaments towards the center of the sarcomere. Motor unit refers to a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates. When a motor neuron fires, all the muscle fibers it innervates contract simultaneously. Rigor mortis is the stiffening of muscles that occurs after death due to the depletion of ATP, which is necessary for muscle relaxation. Without ATP, myosin heads cannot detach from actin, leading to a state of sustained muscle contraction.

    List the steps of skeletal muscle contraction. Explain isometric and isotonic contraction. [2+2=4]

    The steps of skeletal muscle contraction include excitation, excitation-contraction coupling, contraction, and relaxation. During excitation, an action potential propagates along the sarcolemma and down the T-tubules, leading to the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. During excitation-contraction coupling, calcium ions bind to troponin, leading to the exposure of actin binding sites and the initiation of the cross-bridge cycle. During contraction, myosin heads bind to actin, undergo a power stroke, and slide the actin filaments towards the center of the sarcomere. During relaxation, calcium ions are pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum, causing troponin to release actin and allowing the muscle to relax. Isometric contraction refers to muscle contraction without a change in muscle length, such as pushing against an immovable object. Isotonic contraction refers to muscle contraction with a change in muscle length, such as lifting a weight.

    Write short notes on: Cross-bridge cycle of muscle contraction.

    The cross-bridge cycle of muscle contraction refers to the series of events that occur when myosin heads bind to actin and undergo a power stroke, sliding the actin filaments towards the center of the sarcomere. The cycle begins with the binding of myosin heads to actin, followed by the release of ADP and inorganic phosphate, which allows the myosin head to undergo a conformational change and slide the actin filament. ATP then binds to the myosin head, causing it to detach from actin. The ATP is hydrolyzed, causing the myosin head to return to its original conformation and be repositioned for another cycle. This process continues as long as calcium ions are bound to troponin, exposing actin binding sites.

    Describe the sequences of event occuring during excitation contraction coupling in skeletal muscle.

    Excitation-contraction coupling is the process that connects the action potential generated in the sarcolemma of a muscle fiber to the start of muscle contraction. The following is the sequence of events that occur during excitation-contraction coupling in skeletal muscle: Action potential arrives at the axon terminal of the motor neuron, causing voltage-gated calcium channels to open. Calcium ions enter the axon terminal, which triggers the release of acetylcholine into the synaptic cleft. Acetylcholine binds to receptors on the sarcolemma, causing ion channels to open and allowing sodium ions to enter the muscle fiber. This causes the sarcolemma to depolarize, generating an action potential that spreads along the T-tubules. The action potential triggers the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum into the sarcoplasm. Calcium ions bind to troponin, causing a conformational change that exposes the myosin-binding sites on actin. Myosin heads bind to actin, forming cross-bridges. ATP is hydrolyzed by myosin, causing the myosin head to change conformation and pull the actin filament toward the center of the sarcomere. ATP binds to myosin, causing it to release from actin and return to its original conformation. The cycle of myosin binding, power stroke, and detachment continues as long as calcium ions are present and ATP is available. The excitation-contraction coupling process results in the contraction of the muscle fiber, which is necessary for movement and other physiological processes in the body.

    Explain the mechanism of contraction of skeletal muscle in the body.

    Skeletal muscle contraction is initiated by the release of acetylcholine from motor neurons at the neuromuscular junction. Acetylcholine binds to receptors on the muscle fiber membrane, which causes depolarization of the membrane and the generation of an action potential. This action potential travels along the muscle fiber and triggers the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Calcium ions bind to troponin, which allows myosin heads to bind to actin filaments and form cross-bridges. The hydrolysis of ATP by the myosin head provides the energy for the cross-bridge to pull the actin filament towards the center of the sarcomere, shortening the muscle fiber and generating force.

    Explain sequences of events during contraction and relaxation of skeletal muscle. What do you mean by excitation contraction coupling?

    During skeletal muscle contraction, the action potential travels along the muscle fiber and triggers the release of calcium ions from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Calcium ions bind to troponin, which allows myosin heads to bind to actin filaments and form cross-bridges. The hydrolysis of ATP by the myosin head provides the energy for the cross-bridge to pull the actin filament towards the center of the sarcomere, shortening the muscle fiber and generating force. During relaxation, the calcium ions are reabsorbed back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum, the troponin-tropomyosin complex re-establishes its position on the actin filaments, and the cross-bridges detach from the actin filaments. Excitation-contraction coupling is the process by which the action potential in the muscle fiber membrane leads to the contraction of the muscle fiber.

    A motor unit consists of a motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates. Actin and myosin are the two main proteins responsible for the contraction of skeletal muscle. Actin is a thin filament made up of actin, tropomyosin, and troponin. Myosin is a thick filament made up of myosin heads and tails. During muscular contraction, the myosin heads bind to the actin filaments and form cross-bridges, which pull the actin filaments towards the center of the sarcomere. The hydrolysis of ATP by the myosin head provides the energy for the cross-bridge to cycle and generate force. A flow chart diagram showing the interaction between actin and myosin during muscular contraction in skeletal muscle would include the steps of calcium release, troponin-tropomyosin complex shift, cross-bridge formation, power stroke, and cross-bridge detachment.

    Enumerate the role of calcium ion in excitation contraction coupling.

    Calcium ions play a crucial role in the process of excitation-contraction coupling, which is the process by which an action potential triggers muscle contraction. When an action potential travels down a motor neuron and reaches the neuromuscular junction, it causes the release of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine. Acetylcholine binds to receptors on the muscle fiber, causing depolarization of the muscle cell membrane and the opening of voltage-gated calcium channels in the sarcoplasmic reticulum. This leads to a release of calcium ions into the cytoplasm of the muscle fiber. The calcium ions then bind to the protein complex troponin, which is part of the thin filament of the sarcomere.
