10.2 Alkali Metals

10.2.1 General Characteristics of Alkali Metals

Alkali metals are a group of elements found in Group 1 of the periodic table, which includes lithium (Li), sodium (Na), potassium (K), rubidium (Rb), cesium (Cs), and francium (Fr). They share several general characteristics:

  • They are highly reactive metals.
  • They have low density and are relatively soft.
  • They have low melting and boiling points.
  • They are excellent conductors of heat and electricity.
  • They have a single valence electron, making them prone to losing that electron and forming a univalent cation with a +1 charge.

10.2.2 Sodium

Extraction from Down's Process:

Sodium is commonly extracted from its chief ore, sodium chloride (NaCl), through an electrolytic process known as Down's process. In this process, a brine solution (concentrated NaCl solution) is electrolyzed using a Downs cell, which consists of a molten mixture of sodium chloride, calcium chloride, and a steel cathode.

During electrolysis, sodium ions migrate towards the cathode, where they are reduced to sodium metal:

\[2Na^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow 2Na\]

Properties of Sodium

Sodium exhibits various properties, as described below:

Action with Oxygen:

Sodium readily reacts with oxygen (O2) to form sodium oxide (Na2O) or sodium peroxide (Na2O2) depending on the conditions:

\[4Na + O_2 \rightarrow 2Na_2O\] \[2Na + O_2 \rightarrow Na_2O_2\]

Action with Water:

Sodium vigorously reacts with water (H2O) to produce sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and hydrogen gas (H2):

\[2Na + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH + H_2\]

Action with Acids:

Sodium reacts with acids to form the respective salts and release hydrogen gas. For example, with hydrochloric acid (HCl):

\[2Na + 2HCl \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2\]

Action with Nonmetals:

Sodium reacts with nonmetals, such as sulfur (S) and phosphorus (P), to form sodium sulfide (Na2S) and sodium phosphide (Na3P), respectively:

\[2Na + S \rightarrow Na_2S\] \[3Na + P \rightarrow Na_3P\]

Action with Ammonia:

Sodium reacts with ammonia (NH3) to produce sodamide (NaNH2) and hydrogen gas (H2):

\[2Na + 2NH_3 \rightarrow 2NaNH_2 + H _2\]

Uses of Sodium

Sodium has several important uses, including:

  • Manufacturing of sodium compounds: Sodium compounds, such as sodium hydroxide, sodium carbonate, and sodium bicarbonate, have numerous industrial applications.
  • Production of sodium-vapor lamps: Sodium-vapor lamps are widely used in street lighting and industrial lighting due to their high efficiency.
  • Alloying agent: Sodium is used as an alloying agent in certain metals to improve their properties.
  • Reducing agent: Sodium can act as a reducing agent in various chemical reactions.
  • Chemical synthesis: Sodium is used in the production of various organic compounds and pharmaceuticals.

10.2.3 Sodium Hydroxide

Properties of Sodium Hydroxide

Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), also known as caustic soda, possesses the following properties:

Precipitation Reaction:

Sodium hydroxide can cause precipitation reactions when it reacts with certain metal ions. For example, when sodium hydroxide reacts with copper(II) sulfate (CuSO4), a blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide (Cu(OH)2) forms:

\[NaOH + CuSO_4 \rightarrow Cu(OH)_2 + Na_2SO_4\]

Action with Carbon Monoxide:

Sodium hydroxide reacts with carbon monoxide (CO) to form sodium formate (HCOONa), a salt of formic acid:

\[NaOH + CO \rightarrow HCOONa\]

Uses of Sodium Hydroxide

Sodium hydroxide finds various applications, including:

  • Industrial manufacturing: Sodium hydroxide is used in the production of pulp and paper, textiles, soaps, and detergents.
  • Drain cleaner: It is commonly used as a powerful drain cleaner due to its ability to dissolve organic matter.
  • pH regulation: Sodium hydroxide is used in water treatment plants to adjust the pH of water.
  • Food industry: It is employed in food processing, such as in the preparation of certain food products and the cleaning of equipment.

10.2.4 Sodium Carbonate

Properties of Sodium Carbonate

Sodium carbonate (Na2CO3), also known as soda ash or washing soda, exhibits the following properties:

Action with Carbon Dioxide:

Sodium carbonate reacts with carbon dioxide (CO2) to form sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3):

\[Na_2CO_3 + CO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow 2NaHCO_3\]

Action with Sulfur Dioxide:

Sodium carbonate reacts with sulfur dioxide (SO2) to produce sodium sulfite (Na2SO3):

\[Na_2CO_3 + SO_2 \rightarrow Na_2SO_3 + CO_2\]

Action with Water:

Sodium carbonate readily dissolves in water, forming an alkaline solution due to the hydrolysis of carbonate ions:

\[Na_2CO_3 + H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH + CO_2\]

Precipitation Reactions:

Sodium carbonate can cause precipitation reactions with various metal ions. For example, when sodium carbonate reacts with calcium chloride (CaCl2), calcium carbonate (CaCO3) precipitates out:

\[Na_2CO_3 + CaCl_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + 2NaCl\]

Uses of Sodium Carbonate

Sodium carbonate is utilized in numerous applications, including:

  • Manufacturing of glass: Sodium carbonate is a vital ingredient in the production of glass, providing stability and clarity.
  • Water treatment: It is used in water treatment processes to adjust the pH and remove impurities.
  • Textile industry: Sodium carbonate is employed in the textile industry for dyeing and bleaching processes.
  • Domestic cleaning products: It can be found in various cleaning products, such as laundry detergents and dishwasher detergents.
  • pH regulation: Sodium carbonate is used to maintain the pH in swimming pools and spas.
