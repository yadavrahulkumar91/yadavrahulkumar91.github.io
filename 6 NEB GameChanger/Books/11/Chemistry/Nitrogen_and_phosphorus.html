9.4 Nitrogen

9.4.1 Reason for Inertness of Nitrogen and Active Nitrogen

Nitrogen is an element found abundantly in the Earth's atmosphere, constituting about 78% of the air we breathe. It exists as a diatomic molecule, N2, where two nitrogen atoms are triple-bonded together. The inertness of nitrogen is primarily attributed to the strength of this triple bond.

The triple bond in N2 is exceptionally strong, requiring a significant amount of energy to break it. This makes nitrogen unreactive under normal conditions. The inertness of nitrogen allows it to act as a diluent gas, providing stability and reducing the reactivity of other substances.

However, despite being generally unreactive, nitrogen can form compounds under specific conditions. Active nitrogen refers to the highly reactive forms of nitrogen, such as nitrogen radicals or ions, which are capable of participating in chemical reactions. These reactive forms of nitrogen can be generated through processes like lightning, combustion, or certain industrial reactions.

9.4.2 Chemical Properties of Ammonia

Ammonia, represented by the chemical formula NH3, is a compound composed of one nitrogen atom bonded to three hydrogen atoms. It exhibits several chemical properties, as described below:

Action with CuSO4 Solution:

When ammonia reacts with copper sulfate (CuSO4) solution, it forms a deep blue complex known as tetraamminecopper(II) sulfate:

\[CuSO_4 + 4NH_3 \rightarrow [Cu(NH_3)_4]SO_4\]

Action with Water:

Ammonia can act as a weak base in the presence of water. It undergoes a reversible reaction with water, forming ammonium ions (NH4+) and hydroxide ions (OH-):

\[NH_3 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons NH_4^+ + OH^-\]

Action with FeCl3 Solution:

Ammonia reacts with ferric chloride (FeCl3) solution, forming a deep red-brown complex called hexaammineferric chloride:

\[FeCl_3 + 6NH_3 \rightarrow [Fe(NH_3)_6]Cl_3\]

Action with Concentrated HCl:

Ammonia reacts with concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl) to form a white precipitate of ammonium chloride (NH4Cl):

\[NH_3 + HCl \rightarrow NH_4Cl\]

Action with Mercurous Nitrate Paper:

Ammonia gas, when brought in contact with mercurous nitrate paper, causes the paper to turn black due to the formation of a gray-black compound called mercurous ammonium nitrate:

\[Hg(NO_3)_2 + 2NH_3 \rightarrow Hg + NH_4NO_3 + H_2O\]

Action with Oxygen (Combustion):

Ammonia can undergo combustion in the presence of oxygen to produce nitrogen gas (N2) and water (H2O):

\[4NH_3 + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2N_2 + 6H_2O\]

9.4.3 Applications of Ammonia

Ammonia finds numerous applications in various fields. Some of the significant applications include:

  • Production of fertilizers: Ammonia is a key ingredient in the manufacture of nitrogen-based fertilizers, such as ammonium nitrate and urea. These fertilizers provide essential nutrients to plants.
  • Refrigeration and cooling systems: Ammonia has excellent refrigerant properties and is widely used in industrial refrigeration systems and air conditioning units.
  • Cleaning agents: Ammonia is an effective cleaning agent and is often used in household cleaners for removing stains, grease, and dirt.
  • Production of dyes and textiles: Ammonia is utilized in the production of various dyes and as a pH adjuster in the textile industry.

9.4.4 Harmful Effects of Ammonia

While ammonia has several useful applications, it can also pose risks to human health and the environment. Some of the harmful effects of ammonia include:

  • Respiratory irritant: Ammonia vapor can irritate the respiratory system, causing coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath.
  • Corrosive to tissues: Concentrated ammonia can cause severe burns and corrosion if it comes into contact with the skin, eyes, or mucous membranes.
  • Toxicity: In high concentrations, ammonia can be toxic if inhaled or ingested, leading to systemic effects on the body.
  • Environmental impact: Ammonia, when released into water bodies or soil, can contribute to eutrophication and harm aquatic life.

9.4.5 Oxy-acids of Nitrogen (Name and Formula)

Nitrogen forms several oxy-acids, each with a different number of oxygen atoms. The commonly known oxy-acids of nitrogen include:

  • Nitric Acid (HNO3)
  • Nitrous Acid (HNO2)
  • Perchloric Acid (HClO4)
  • Hypochlorous Acid (HClO)
  • Chlorous Acid (HClO2)
  • Chloric Acid (HClO3)

9.4.6 Chemical Properties of Nitric Acid

HNO3 as an Acid:

Nitric acid (HNO3) is a strong acid that dissociates completely in water, releasing hydrogen ions (H+). It reacts with bases to form salts and water.

Oxidizing Agent:

Nitric acid also acts as a powerful oxidizing agent, capable of oxidizing various substances. Here are some examples of its reactions:

  • Action with Zinc (Zn): Nitric acid oxidizes zinc to form zinc nitrate (Zn(NO3)2) and liberates nitrogen dioxide (NO2) gas:

    • \[Zn + 2HNO_3 \rightarrow Zn(NO_3)_2 + H_2O + 2NO_2\]

  • Action with Magnesium (Mg): Nitric acid oxidizes magnesium to form magnesium nitrate (Mg(NO3)2) and liberates nitrogen dioxide (NO2) gas:

    • \[2Mg + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 2Mg(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + N_2O_4\]

  • Action with Iron (Fe): Nitric acid oxidizes iron to form ferric nitrate (Fe(NO3)3) and liberates nitrogen dioxide (NO2) gas:

    • \[4Fe + 10HNO_3 \rightarrow 4Fe(NO_3)_3 + 5H_2O + 2NO_2\]

  • Action with Copper (Cu): Nitric acid oxidizes copper to form copper nitrate (Cu(NO3)2) and liberates nitrogen dioxide (NO2) gas:

    • \[3Cu + 8HNO_3 \rightarrow 3Cu(NO_3)_2 + 4H_2O + 2NO_2\]

  • Action with Sulphur (S): Nitric acid oxidizes sulphur to form sulphuric acid (H2SO4) and liberates various nitrogen oxides (NOx):

    • \[S + 6HNO_3 \rightarrow H_2SO_4 + 6NO_x\]

  • Action with Carbon (C): Nitric acid oxidizes carbon to form carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O):

    • \[C + 4HNO_3 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O + 4NO_2\]

  • Action with Sulphur Dioxide (SO2): Nitric acid oxidizes sulphur dioxide to form sulphuric acid (H2SO4) and liberates nitrogen dioxide (NO2) gas:

    • \[SO_2 + 2HNO_3 \rightarrow H_2SO_4 + 2NO_2\]

  • Action with Hydrogen Sulphide (H2S): Nitric acid oxidizes hydrogen sulphide to form sulphuric acid (H2SO4) and liberates various nitrogen oxides (NOx):

    • \[H_2S + 4HNO_3 \rightarrow H_2SO_4 + 4NO_x\]

9.4.7 Ring Test for Nitrate Ion

The ring test is a simple qualitative test used to detect the presence of the nitrate ion (NO3-). Here's how the test is conducted:

  1. A small amount of the test substance is mixed with a few drops of concentrated sulphuric acid (H2SO4) in a test tube.
  2. After mixing, a ferrous sulphate (FeSO4) crystal is gently added to the mixture.
  3. If the nitrate ion is present, a brown ring or coloration forms at the junction of the two layers, indicating the formation of nitric oxide (NO) gas.

The reaction can be represented as:

\[2NO_3^- + 4Fe^{2+} + 10H_2SO_4 \rightarrow 2NO + 4FeSO_4 + 8H_2O + 5O_2\]

The formation of the brown ring confirms the presence of the nitrate ion.

9.7 Phosphorus

9.7.1 Allotropes of Phosphorus

Phosphorus exhibits several allotropes, which are different structural forms of the same element. The allotropes of phosphorus include:

  • White phosphorus
  • Red phosphorus
  • Black phosphorus

9.7.2 Preparation, Properties, and Uses of Phosphine (PH3)

Preparation:

Phosphine (PH3) is prepared by the reaction of a metal phosphide with a strong acid, usually hydrochloric acid (HCl). One common method involves the reaction between calcium phosphide (Ca3P2) and hydrochloric acid:

$$\text{Ca}_3\text{P}_2 + 6\text{HCl} \rightarrow 2\text{PH}_3 + 3\text{CaCl}_2$$

Other metal phosphides, such as aluminum phosphide (AlP) and zinc phosphide (Zn3P2), can also be used in the preparation of phosphine.

Properties:

Basic Nature: Phosphine (PH3) is a weak base. It reacts with acids to form salts known as phosphonium salts.

$$\text{PH}_3 + \text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{PH}_4^+\text{Cl}^-$$

Reducing Nature: Phosphine is a reducing agent and can reduce metal ions to their elemental form. For example, it can reduce silver ions (Ag+) to metallic silver (Ag).

$$3\text{Ag}^+ + \text{PH}_3 + 3\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 3\text{Ag} + \text{PH}_4^+ + 3\text{OH}^-$$

Action with Halogens: Phosphine reacts with halogens, such as chlorine (Cl2), to form phosphorus halides. For example, with chlorine:

$$\text{PH}_3 + \text{Cl}_2 \rightarrow \text{PCl}_3 + \text{HCl}$$

Action with Oxygen: Phosphine is spontaneously flammable in the presence of oxygen. It undergoes combustion to form phosphorus pentoxide (P2O5) and water (H2O).

$$\text{4PH}_3 + \text{8O}_2 \rightarrow \text{2P}_2\text{O}_5 + \text{6H}_2\text{O}$$

Uses:

Phosphine (PH3) has various applications in different fields:

  • It is used as a fumigant to control pests in stored grain and other agricultural products.
  • Phosphine is used in the semiconductor industry for the deposition of thin films.
  • It is used as a reducing agent in organic synthesis and pharmaceutical manufacturing.
  • Phosphine gas is employed in the production of flame retardants and in the manufacture of certain chemicals.
