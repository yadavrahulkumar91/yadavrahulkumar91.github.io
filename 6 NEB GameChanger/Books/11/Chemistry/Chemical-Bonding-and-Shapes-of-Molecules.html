5.1 Valence Shell, Valence Electron, and Octet Theory

The valence shell of an atom is the outermost shell that contains electrons. Valence electrons are the electrons present in the valence shell. The valence electrons play a crucial role in determining the chemical properties of an element.

The octet theory states that atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration with eight electrons in their valence shell, resembling the electron configuration of noble gases. This is known as the octet rule.

5.2 Ionic Bond and its Properties

An ionic bond is formed between two atoms when there is a transfer of electrons from one atom to another. This results in the formation of ions, which are charged particles.

Properties of ionic compounds include:

  • High Melting and Boiling Points: Ionic compounds have strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the positively and negatively charged ions, requiring a large amount of energy to break these bonds.
  • Crystal Lattice Structure: Ionic compounds typically form a regular repeating pattern of alternating positive and negative ions in a three-dimensional lattice.
  • Conductivity: Ionic compounds conduct electricity when dissolved in water or in a molten state due to the presence of mobile ions.

5.3 Covalent Bond and Coordinate Covalent Bond

A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. Covalent bonding occurs between nonmetallic elements.

A coordinate covalent bond is a special type of covalent bond in which both shared electrons come from the same atom.

5.4 Properties of Covalent Compounds

Properties of covalent compounds include:

  • Lower Melting and Boiling Points: Covalent compounds have weaker intermolecular forces compared to ionic compounds, resulting in lower melting and boiling points.
  • Soft and Brittle: Covalent compounds are typically soft and brittle in nature.
  • Low Electrical Conductivity: Covalent compounds do not conduct electricity in their pure state, as they lack free-moving ions or charged particles.

5.5 Lewis Dot Structure of Some Common Compounds of s and p Block Elements

Lewis dot structures are diagrams that represent the valence electrons of atoms and their arrangement in a molecule. Here are the Lewis dot structures of some common compounds of s and p block elements:

  • Sodium Chloride (NaCl): Na · Cl
  • Water (H2O): H · O · H
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO2): O = C = O
  • Ammonia (NH3): H · N · H · H
  • Methane (CH4): H · C · H · H · H

Note: The dots represent valence electrons, and the lines represent covalent bonds.

5.6 Resonance

Resonance is a phenomenon that occurs when there are multiple valid Lewis structures possible for a molecule or an ion. It indicates the delocalization of electrons and the stability of the molecule or ion.

5.7 VSEPR Theory and Shapes of Some Simple Molecules

The VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) theory predicts the shapes of molecules based on the repulsion between electron pairs around the central atom.

The shapes of some simple molecules according to the VSEPR theory are:

  • BeF2: Linear
  • BF3: Trigonal Planar
  • CH4: Tetrahedral
  • CH3Cl: Tetrahedral
  • PCl5: Trigonal Bipyramidal
  • SF6: Octahedral
  • H2O: Bent
  • NH3: Trigonal Pyramidal
  • CO2: Linear
  • H2S: Bent
  • PH3: Trigonal Pyramidal

5.8 Elementary Idea of Valence Bond Theory

Valence Bond Theory explains chemical bonding by considering overlapping atomic orbitals of the participating atoms.

5.9 Hybridization Involving s and p Orbitals Only

Hybridization is the mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals with different shapes and energies. Hybridization involving s and p orbitals only includes sp, sp2, and sp3 hybridizations.

5.10 Bond Characteristics

5.10.1 Bond Length

Bond length is the average distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms. It is influenced by factors such as the size of the atoms and the number of bonds between them.

5.10.2 Ionic Character

Ionic character refers to the degree of ionic character in a bond. It is influenced by the electronegativity difference between the bonded atoms. A higher electronegativity difference indicates a more polar or ionic bond.

5.10.3 Dipole Moment

Dipole moment is a measure of the polarity of a bond or a molecule. It arises when there is an unequal distribution of electron density. The dipole moment is influenced by the electronegativity difference between the bonded atoms and the bond length.
