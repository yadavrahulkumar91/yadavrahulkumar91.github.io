8.1 Physical and Chemical Equilibrium

Equilibrium is a state in which the forward and reverse processes occur at equal rates, resulting in no net change in the concentrations of reactants and products.

Physical equilibrium occurs in physical processes, such as phase changes (e.g., solid to liquid, liquid to gas), where the equilibrium is established between two different phases.

Chemical equilibrium occurs in chemical reactions, where the equilibrium is established between the reactants and products.

8.2 Dynamic Nature of Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium is a dynamic process, meaning that the forward and reverse reactions continue to occur even in the equilibrium state.

At equilibrium, the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, but the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant.

The equilibrium can be shifted by changing the concentration, temperature, or pressure of the system, according to Le Chatelier's principle.

8.3 Law of Mass Action

The law of mass action states that the rate of a chemical reaction is directly proportional to the product of the molar concentrations (or activities) of the reactants. For a generic chemical reaction: \[aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD\] The law of mass action can be expressed as: \[r = k [A]^a [B]^b\] Where: - \(r\) represents the rate of the reaction - \(k\) is the rate constant or specific rate constant - \([A]\) and \([B]\) are the molar concentrations (or activities) of reactants A and B, respectively - \(a\) and \(b\) are the stoichiometric coefficients of reactants A and B in the balanced chemical equation ---

8.4 Expression for Equilibrium Constant and its Importance

The equilibrium constant (\(K\)) is a fundamental concept in chemical equilibrium. It quantifies the extent to which a chemical reaction reaches equilibrium and provides information about the concentrations of reactants and products at equilibrium. For the generic reaction: \[aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD\] The equilibrium constant expression is given by: \[K = \frac{{[C]^c[D]^d}}{{[A]^a[B]^b}}\] Where: - \([A]\), \([B]\), \([C]\), and \([D]\) represent the molar concentrations (or activities) of the respective species at equilibrium. - \(a\), \(b\), \(c\), and \(d\) are the stoichiometric coefficients of the balanced chemical equation. The importance of the equilibrium constant lies in its ability to determine the position of equilibrium. If \(K\) is large, it indicates that the reaction favors the products at equilibrium, whereas a small \(K\) value signifies a preference for the reactants. Additionally, the equilibrium constant provides insights into reaction spontaneity and the direction of chemical reactions. ---

8.5 Relationship between Kp and Kc

The relationship between the equilibrium constants expressed in terms of partial pressures (\(K_p\)) and molar concentrations (\(K_c\)) can be obtained using the ideal gas law. Let's consider the generic reaction: \[aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD\] The relationship is given by: \[K_p = K_c \left(\frac{{RT}}{P}\right)^{\Delta n}\] Where: - \(K_p\) represents the equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures. - \(K_c\) denotes the equilibrium constant expressed in terms of molar concentrations. - \(R\) is the ideal gas constant. - \(T\) represents the temperature in Kelvin. - \(P\) is the total pressure of the system. - \(\Delta n\) represents the difference between the total moles of gaseous products (\(c + d\)) and the total moles of gaseous reactants (\(a + b\)). The relationship between \(K_p\) and \(K_c\) allows us to convert between the two representations of the equilibrium constant. ---

8.6 Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle states that when a system at equilibrium is subjected to an external influence or stress, the system responds by readjusting itself to minimize the effect of the disturbance and regain equilibrium. This principle helps us understand the behavior of chemical reactions when changes are made to factors such as concentration, pressure, temperature, or the presence of a catalyst. Le Chatelier's Principle can be summarized as follows:

  1. 1. Concentration changes: If the concentration of a reactant or product is increased, the equilibrium will shift in the direction that reduces the concentration of that species. Conversely, if the concentration is decreased, the equilibrium will shift to increase the concentration of the species affected.
  2. 2. Pressure changes: For reactions involving gases, if the pressure is increased, the equilibrium will shift in the direction that reduces the total number of moles of gas. If the pressure is decreased, the equilibrium will shift to increase the total number of moles of gas.
  3. 3. Temperature changes: Changes in temperature affect the equilibrium constant. For exothermic reactions (reactions that release heat), increasing the temperature shifts the equilibrium in the direction that consumes heat, while decreasing the temperature shifts the equilibrium in the direction that releases heat. The opposite is true for endothermic reactions.
  4. 4. Catalysts: Catalysts do not affect the position of equilibrium. They increase the rate of the forward and reverse reactions equally, allowing the system to reach equilibrium faster.
