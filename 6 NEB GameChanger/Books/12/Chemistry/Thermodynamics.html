4.1 Introduction

Thermodynamics is the branch of science that deals with the study of energy and its interconversion in various processes, including chemical reactions. It provides a framework to understand and predict the behavior of systems based on their energy changes and the laws that govern them.

4.2 Energy in Chemical Reactions

Chemical reactions involve the breaking and forming of chemical bonds, which results in the transfer or transformation of energy. The energy changes associated with chemical reactions can be classified as:

  • Exothermic Reactions: These reactions release energy to the surroundings in the form of heat or light. The products have lower energy than the reactants.
  • Endothermic Reactions: These reactions absorb energy from the surroundings, resulting in an increase in the energy of the products compared to the reactants.

4.3 Internal Energy

Internal energy (\(U\)) is the sum of the kinetic and potential energies of the particles within a system. It includes the energy associated with the motion and interactions of atoms, molecules, and ions. Changes in internal energy are denoted as \(\Delta U\).

4.4 First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics, also known as the law of energy conservation, states that energy cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system. It can only be converted from one form to another or transferred between the system and its surroundings.

Mathematically, the first law of thermodynamics is expressed as:

\[ \Delta U = q + w \]

Where:

  • \(\Delta U\) is the change in internal energy of the system
  • \(q\) is the heat transferred to or from the system
  • \(w\) is the work done on or by the system

This equation indicates that the change in internal energy of a system is equal to the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system or vice versa.

4.5 Enthalpy and Enthalpy Changes

Enthalpy (\(H\)) is a thermodynamic property that accounts for the heat flow in a system at constant pressure. It includes the internal energy of the system as well as the product of pressure and volume.

The enthalpy change (\(\Delta H\)) is the difference in enthalpy between the products and reactants in a chemical reaction. It represents the heat absorbed or released during the reaction.

The enthalpy change can be expressed as:

\[ \Delta H = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}} \]

If \(\Delta H\) is negative, the reaction is exothermic, and if \(\Delta H\) is positive, the reaction is endothermic.

The enthalpy change can be measured experimentally using calorimetry or calculated using thermodynamic data (such as standard enthalpies of formation).

4.6 Enthalpy of Reaction, Enthalpy of Solution, Enthalpy of Formation, Enthalpy of Combustion

The enthalpy of reaction (\(\Delta H_{\text{rxn}}\)) is the change in enthalpy that occurs during a chemical reaction. It is determined by the difference in enthalpies between the products and reactants. If the reaction is exothermic, the enthalpy change is negative; if it is endothermic, the enthalpy change is positive.

The enthalpy of solution (\(\Delta H_{\text{sol}}\)) is the enthalpy change that occurs when a solute dissolves in a solvent to form a solution. It can be exothermic or endothermic, depending on whether heat is released or absorbed during the dissolution process.

The enthalpy of formation (\(\Delta H_{\text{f}}\)) is the enthalpy change that occurs when one mole of a compound is formed from its constituent elements in their standard states. The enthalpy of formation is defined as zero for elements in their standard states.

The enthalpy of combustion (\(\Delta H_{\text{c}}\)) is the enthalpy change that occurs when one mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion with oxygen. It represents the heat released during the combustion process.

4.7 Laws of Thermochemistry: Laplace Law and Hess's Law

Laplace's Law of Thermochemistry states that the enthalpy change of a reaction is directly proportional to the amount of substance that undergoes the reaction. It means that if a reaction is multiplied by a certain factor, the enthalpy change is also multiplied by the same factor.

Hess's Law states that the enthalpy change of a reaction is the same whether it occurs in one step or multiple steps. In other words, the overall enthalpy change of a reaction is the sum of the enthalpy changes of the individual steps or reactions that make up the overall process. Hess's Law allows for the calculation of unknown enthalpy changes by using known enthalpy changes of other reactions.

4.8 Entropy and Spontaneity

Entropy (\(S\)) is a thermodynamic property that measures the degree of randomness or disorder in a system. It quantifies the number of microstates (possible arrangements) that a system can have at a given macrostate (observed condition).

The second law of thermodynamics is closely related to entropy and states that the entropy of an isolated system tends to increase over time. In spontaneous processes, the entropy of the universe (system + surroundings) always increases.

4.9 Second Law of Thermodynamics

The second law of thermodynamics states that the total entropy of an isolated system always increases or remains constant in a spontaneous process. It provides a directionality to thermodynamic processes, distinguishing between those that occur spontaneously (with an increase in entropy) and those that do not.

The second law also states that it is impossible to convert heat completely into work in a cyclic process without the assistance of an external entity, such as a heat engine. This principle is known as the Kelvin-Planck statement of the second law.

4.10 Gibbs' Free Energy and Prediction of Spontaneity

Gibbs' free energy (\(\Delta G\)) is a thermodynamic potential that combines both enthalpy and entropy changes in a system. It determines the maximum amount of non-expansion work that can be extracted from a system at constant temperature and pressure.

The sign of \(\Delta G\) determines the spontaneity of a process:

  • If \(\Delta G\) is negative (\(\Delta G < 0\)), the process is spontaneous in the forward direction.
  • If \(\Delta G\) is positive (\(\Delta G > 0\)), the process is non-spontaneous in the forward direction.
  • If \(\Delta G\) is zero (\(\Delta G = 0\)), the system is at equilibrium.

4.11 Relationship between \(\Delta G\) and Equilibrium Constant

The relationship between \(\Delta G\) and the equilibrium constant (\(K\)) is given by the equation:

\[ \Delta G = -RT\ln(K) \]

Where:

  • \(R\) is the gas constant
  • \(T\) is the temperature in Kelvin
  • \(K\) is the equilibrium constant

This equation allows us to relate the standard free energy change (\(\Delta G^\circ\)) to the equilibrium constant at a given temperature.
