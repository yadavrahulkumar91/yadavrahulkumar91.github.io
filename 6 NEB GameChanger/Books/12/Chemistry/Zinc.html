7.2 Zinc

7.2.1 Occurrence and Extraction of Zinc from Zinc Blende

Zinc is a chemical element that is commonly found in the Earth's crust. It occurs in the form of zinc blende (ZnS), which is a major zinc ore. The extraction of zinc from zinc blende involves the following steps:

  1. Roasting: Zinc blende is heated in the presence of oxygen to convert it into zinc oxide and sulfur dioxide.

    \[\text{ZnS(s)} + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{ZnO(s)} + \text{SO}_2(g)\]

  2. Reduction: Zinc oxide is then reduced using carbon or coke to obtain metallic zinc.

    \[\text{ZnO(s)} + \text{C(s)} \rightarrow \text{Zn(g)} + \text{CO}(g)\]

  3. Purification: The obtained crude zinc is further purified by distillation or electrolysis.

7.2.2 Properties and Uses of Zinc

Zinc possesses several important properties and finds various practical applications:

With Air:

Zinc is a relatively reactive metal and reacts with air to form a protective layer of zinc oxide (ZnO) on its surface, which prevents further corrosion.

With Acid:

Zinc reacts with dilute acids, such as hydrochloric acid (HCl) or sulfuric acid (H2SO4), to produce zinc salts and release hydrogen gas (H2).

\[\text{Zn(s)} + \text{2HCl(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_2(aq) + \text{H}_2(g)\]

With Alkali:

Zinc does not react with alkalis under normal conditions.

Displacement Reaction:

Zinc is more reactive than copper and can displace copper from its salt solution through a displacement reaction.

\[\text{Zn(s)} + \text{CuSO}_4(aq) \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4(aq) + \text{Cu(s)}\]

Uses:

  • Zinc is widely used as a protective coating for iron and steel in a process called galvanization.
  • It is used in the production of various alloys, such as brass and bronze.
  • Zinc oxide is used in the manufacture of rubber, paint, ceramics, and other products.
  • Zinc is an essential trace element for humans and is used in dietary supplements and medicinal products.

7.2.3 Chemistry of White Vitriol

White vitriol, also known as zinc sulfate heptahydrate (ZnSO4·7H2O), is a compound derived from zinc. It has several chemical properties, preparation methods, and practical uses:

Preparation:

White vitriol can be prepared by dissolving zinc oxide (ZnO) or zinc metal in sulfuric acid (H2SO4) and then crystallizing the solution. The resulting compound is a hydrated form of zinc sulfate.

\[\text{ZnO(s) + H}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4(aq) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l)\]

Properties:

  • White vitriol forms colorless crystals with a heptahydrate structure (ZnSO4·7H2O).
  • It is highly soluble in water.
  • Upon heating, the water molecules of hydration can be removed, resulting in the formation of anhydrous zinc sulfate (ZnSO4).

Uses:

  • White vitriol is used in the production of pigments, dyes, and paints.
  • It is utilized as a mordant in the textile industry to enhance dye fixation.
  • It is employed as a fertilizer additive to provide zinc to plants.
  • White vitriol has applications in the field of medicine and is used as an astringent and emetic.
