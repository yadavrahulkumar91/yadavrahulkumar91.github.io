5.1 Electrode Potential and Standard Electrode Potential

Electrode potential is the measure of the tendency of an electrode to gain or lose electrons when it is connected to a half-cell in an electrochemical cell. It is a measure of the driving force of a redox reaction at the electrode. The electrode potential is expressed in volts (V).

Standard electrode potential (\(E^\circ\)) is the electrode potential measured under standard conditions, which include a standard concentration of 1 M, a temperature of 25Â°C, and a pressure of 1 atm. Standard electrode potentials are used to compare the relative strengths of different redox couples and to determine the spontaneity of redox reactions.

5.2 Types of Electrodes: Standard Hydrogen Electrode and Calomel Electrodes

The Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) is used as the reference electrode to measure the electrode potentials of other half-cells. It consists of a platinum electrode immersed in a solution of 1 M HCl, with hydrogen gas bubbled over the platinum surface. The SHE has a standard electrode potential of 0 V at all temperatures.

Calomel electrodes are widely used as reference electrodes in electrochemical measurements. They consist of a platinum electrode in contact with a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) containing a solution of potassium chloride and mercurous chloride (Hg2Cl2). The potential of a calomel electrode is constant and can be adjusted by changing the concentration of the KCl solution.

5.3 Electrochemical Series and its Applications

The electrochemical series is a list of elements or compounds arranged in order of their standard electrode potentials. It allows us to compare the relative strengths of oxidizing and reducing agents. Substances higher in the electrochemical series have a greater tendency to be reduced (act as oxidizing agents), while substances lower in the series have a greater tendency to be oxidized (act as reducing agents).

The electrochemical series finds various applications, including:

  • Predicting the feasibility of redox reactions
  • Determining the direction of electron flow in electrochemical cells
  • Selecting suitable materials for electrodes in batteries and electrolytic processes
  • Understanding corrosion processes

5.4 Voltaic Cell: Zn-Cu Cell, Ag-Cu Cell

A voltaic cell, also known as a galvanic cell, is an electrochemical cell that converts chemical energy into electrical energy through a spontaneous redox reaction. It consists of two half-cells connected by a salt bridge or a porous barrier.

A common example of a voltaic cell is the Zn-Cu cell, which consists of a zinc electrode (anode) and a copper electrode (cathode) immersed in their respective electrolyte solutions. The zinc electrode undergoes oxidation, losing electrons, while the copper electrode undergoes reduction, gaining electrons. The flow of electrons through an external circuit generates an electric current.

Another example is the Ag-Cu cell, which uses a silver electrode (anode) and a copper electrode (cathode). The silver electrode undergoes oxidation, while the copper electrode undergoes reduction.

5.5 Cell Potential and Standard Cell Potential

Cell potential (\(E_{\text{cell}}\)) is the measure of the potential difference between the two electrodes in an electrochemical cell. It is a measure of the electrical potential energy per unit charge. Cell potential is influenced by the standard electrode potentials of the half-cells and the concentrations of the species involved.

Standard cell potential (\(E_{\text{cell}}^\circ\)) is the cell potential measured under standard conditions. It is obtained by subtracting the standard electrode potential of the anode from the standard electrode potential of the cathode. The standard cell potential is a useful measure of the spontaneity of a redox reaction.

5.6 Relationship between Cell Potential and Free Energy

The relationship between cell potential (\(E_{\text{cell}}\)) and the change in Gibbs free energy (\(\Delta G^\circ\)) is given by the equation:

\[ \Delta G^\circ = -nF \cdot E_{\text{cell}}^\circ \]

Where:

  • \(\Delta G^\circ\) is the change in Gibbs free energy under standard conditions
  • \(n\) is the number of moles of electrons transferred in the balanced redox equation
  • \(F\) is the Faraday constant (96,485 C/mol)
  • \(E_{\text{cell}}^\circ\) is the standard cell potential

This equation relates the spontaneity of a redox reaction to the cell potential and the free energy change.

5.7 Commercial Batteries and Fuel Cells (Hydrogen/Oxygen)

Commercial batteries are devices that convert chemical energy into electrical energy through electrochemical reactions. They consist of one or more voltaic cells connected in series or parallel. Common examples include alkaline batteries, lithium-ion batteries, and lead-acid batteries.

Fuel cells are electrochemical devices that convert the chemical energy of a fuel, such as hydrogen, directly into electrical energy. They operate through the continuous supply of reactants to the electrodes. In a hydrogen/oxygen fuel cell, hydrogen gas is supplied to the anode and reacts with oxygen gas from the air supplied to the cathode, producing water as the byproduct.

Both commercial batteries and fuel cells play important roles in various applications, including portable electronics, electric vehicles, and stationary power systems.
