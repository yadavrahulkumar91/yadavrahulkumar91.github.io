Equation of Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

In physics, Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) refers to the oscillatory motion exhibited by certain systems under the influence of a restoring force that is directly proportional to the displacement from an equilibrium position.

The equation that describes the displacement of an object undergoing SHM as a function of time is:

x(t) = A \cdot \cos(\omega t + \varphi) $------(1)$

Where:

  • x(t) is the displacement of the object from its equilibrium position at time t.
  • A is the amplitude of the motion, representing the maximum displacement from the equilibrium position.
  • \omega is the angular frequency, given by \omega = 2\pi f, where f is the frequency of the motion in hertz (Hz).
  • \varphi is the phase constant, representing the initial phase of the motion.

By differentiating equation $1$ with respect to time, we can obtain the velocity and acceleration equations for SHM:

v(t) = -A \omega \sin(\omega t + \varphi) $------(2)$

a(t) = -A \omega^2 \cos(\omega t + \varphi) $------(3)$

Where:

  • v(t) is the velocity of the object at time t.
  • a(t) is the acceleration of the object at time t.

These equations demonstrate the sinusoidal nature of the motion, with the displacement, velocity, and acceleration varying sinusoidally as a function of time.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

In simple harmonic motion (SHM), the total mechanical energy of a system undergoing oscillatory motion remains constant. This energy is divided between two forms: potential energy and kinetic energy.

Potential Energy (PE)

The potential energy in SHM is associated with the displacement of the object from its equilibrium position. The equation for potential energy in SHM can be expressed as:

$$PE = \frac{1}{2} kx^2$$

Where:

  • $$PE$$ is the potential energy
  • $$k$$ is the spring constant, which represents the stiffness of the spring
  • $$x$$ is the displacement from the equilibrium position

Kinetic Energy (KE)

The kinetic energy in SHM is associated with the velocity of the object. The equation for kinetic energy in SHM can be expressed as:

$$KE = \frac{1}{2} m v^2$$

Where:

  • $$KE$$ is the kinetic energy
  • $$m$$ is the mass of the object
  • $$v$$ is the velocity of the object

Total Mechanical Energy

The total mechanical energy, represented by $$E$$, in SHM is the sum of the potential energy and kinetic energy:

$$E = PE + KE$$

Since the total mechanical energy remains constant, the equation can be simplified as:

$$E = \frac{1}{2} kx^2 + \frac{1}{2} mv^2$$

This equation shows the relationship between the potential energy and kinetic energy of an object undergoing simple harmonic motion.

Application of Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM): Vertical Oscillation of Mass Suspended from Coiled Spring

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) finds numerous applications in physics and engineering. One such application involves the vertical oscillation of a mass suspended from a coiled spring. This system exhibits SHM characteristics and is widely utilized in various practical scenarios.

System Description

In this setup, a mass is attached to the lower end of a vertical coiled spring. The upper end of the spring is fixed to a support, allowing the mass to move up and down along the vertical axis. The system is assumed to be ideal, neglecting any external factors like air resistance or damping effects.

Equilibrium Position

The equilibrium position of the mass-spring system is when the spring is neither compressed nor extended, resulting in zero net force acting on the mass. At this position, the spring is in its natural length, and the mass experiences no gravitational force.

Hooke's Law

The behavior of the coiled spring follows Hooke's Law, which states that the force exerted by an ideal spring is directly proportional to the displacement from its equilibrium position. Mathematically, Hooke's Law can be expressed as:

$$F = -kx$$

Where:

  • $$F$$ is the restoring force exerted by the spring
  • $$k$$ is the spring constant, which represents the stiffness of the spring
  • $$x$$ is the displacement of the mass from the equilibrium position

The negative sign indicates that the restoring force acts opposite to the direction of the displacement, always attempting to restore the mass to its equilibrium position.

Equation of Motion

Using Newton's second law of motion, we can establish the equation of motion for the vertical oscillation of the mass-spring system. The sum of forces acting on the mass is given by:

$$-mg + F = -ma$$

Where:

  • $$m$$ is the mass of the object
  • $$g$$ is the acceleration due to gravity
  • $$a$$ is the acceleration of the mass

Substituting $$F$$ from Hooke's Law:

$$-mg - kx = -ma$$

Dividing the equation by the mass $$m$$:

$$a + \left(\frac{k}{m}\right)x = g$$

The term $$\frac{k}{m}$$ is the angular frequency squared ($$\omega^2$$) of the system.

Finally, the equation of motion can be written as:

$$a + \omega^2x = g$$

This is a differential equation that governs the motion of the mass-spring system.

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

When the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement, the mass-spring system exhibits SHM. In this case, the equation of motion simplifies to:

$$\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \omega^2x = 0$$

This is a second-order linear differential equation that represents SHM. The solution to this equation yields the displacement $$x$$ as a function of time $$t$$.

Period and Frequency

In SHM, the mass-spring system oscillates with a periodic motion. The period $$T$$ is the time taken to complete one full cycle of oscillation, while the frequency $$f$$ represents the number of cycles per unit time. The relationship between period and frequency is given by:

$$T = \frac{1}{f}$$

Angular Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM): Simple Pendulum

Angular Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a type of oscillatory motion exhibited by a simple pendulum. A simple pendulum consists of a mass (called the bob) suspended from a fixed point by a massless and inextensible string or rod. The motion of the pendulum bob follows a regular pattern, characterized by SHM.

System Description

A simple pendulum consists of the following elements:

  • A bob: A point mass located at the lower end of the pendulum.
  • A string or rod: A massless and inextensible element that connects the bob to a fixed point (pivot) above.
  • A pivot: A fixed point that supports the upper end of the string or rod.

Equilibrium Position

The equilibrium position of a simple pendulum is when the bob hangs vertically downward without any displacement. In this position, the pendulum is at rest, and the string or rod is perpendicular to the vertical axis.

Restoring Force

The motion of the simple pendulum is governed by the restoring force, which acts to bring the pendulum back towards its equilibrium position. The restoring force is provided by the component of the gravitational force acting along the direction of displacement. For small angles of oscillation, this force can be approximated by:

$$F = -mg\sin(\theta)$$

Where:

  • $$F$$ is the restoring force
  • $$m$$ is the mass of the bob
  • $$g$$ is the acceleration due to gravity
  • $$\theta$$ is the angular displacement of the pendulum from its equilibrium position

The negative sign indicates that the restoring force is opposite to the direction of displacement, always trying to bring the pendulum back towards the equilibrium position.

Equation of Motion

Using Newton's second law of motion, the equation of motion for a simple pendulum can be derived. The sum of forces acting on the bob is given by:

$$-mg\sin(\theta) = -ml\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2}$$

Where:

  • $$l$$ is the length of the string or rod
  • $$\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2}$$ is the angular acceleration of the bob

Simplifying the equation:

$$\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \frac{g}{l}\sin(\theta) = 0$$

This is a second-order nonlinear differential equation that represents the angular motion of a simple pendulum.

Small Angle Approximation

For small angles of oscillation (typically less than about 10 degrees), the sin function can be approximated by the angle itself in radians. This simplifies the equation of motion to:

$$\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \frac{g}{l}\theta = 0$$

This is a linear differential equation that governs the motion of a simple pendulum under small angle approximation.

Period of Oscillation

The period of oscillation, denoted by $$T$$, is the time taken for the pendulum to complete one full cycle of motion. The period can be calculated using the formula:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}}$$

Where:

  • $$l$$ is the length of the string or rod
  • $$g$$ is the acceleration due to gravity

The period is independent of the amplitude (angular displacement) of the pendulum. It only depends on the length of the string or rod and the acceleration due to gravity.

Oscillatory Motion: Damped Oscillation, Forced Oscillation, and Resonance

Oscillatory motion is a repetitive back-and-forth motion exhibited by various physical systems. In addition to simple harmonic motion (SHM), which we discussed earlier, there are three important types of oscillatory motion: damped oscillation, forced oscillation, and resonance.

Damped Oscillation

In damped oscillation, the amplitude of the oscillating system gradually decreases over time due to the presence of damping forces. Damping can arise from various sources, such as friction, air resistance, or internal energy dissipation. The damping force opposes the motion of the system, resulting in a gradual loss of mechanical energy.

The equation of motion for damped oscillation can be written as:

$$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = 0$$

Where:

  • $$m$$ is the mass of the oscillating object
  • $$x$$ is the displacement of the object from its equilibrium position
  • $$c$$ is the damping coefficient
  • $$k$$ is the force constant or stiffness of the system

The presence of the damping term $$c\frac{dx}{dt}$$ causes the amplitude of the oscillation to decrease over time. The system eventually reaches a point where the damping force balances the applied force, resulting in a steady-state motion with constant amplitude.

Forced Oscillation

Forced oscillation occurs when an external periodic force is applied to an oscillating system. The external force drives the system, causing it to oscillate with a frequency that matches the frequency of the applied force. The equation of motion for forced oscillation can be expressed as:

$$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = F_0\cos(\omega t)$$

Where:

  • $$m$$ is the mass of the oscillating object
  • $$x$$ is the displacement of the object from its equilibrium position
  • $$c$$ is the damping coefficient
  • $$k$$ is the force constant or stiffness of the system
  • $$F_0$$ is the amplitude of the applied force
  • $$\omega$$ is the angular frequency of the applied force

The external force term $$F_0\cos(\omega t)$$ represents a sinusoidal force with amplitude $$F_0$$ and angular frequency $$\omega$$. The forced oscillation results in an oscillatory response of the system at the same frequency as the applied force.

Resonance

Resonance occurs when a forced oscillation drives a system at its natural frequency, leading to a significant increase in the amplitude of the oscillations. The phenomenon of resonance can be observed when the driving frequency closely matches the natural frequency of the system.

In resonance, the amplitude of the system's response can become much larger than the amplitude of the applied force. This amplification of the response is due to the constructive interference between the applied force and the natural oscillations of the system.

Resonance can have both beneficial and detrimental effects depending on the context. It is exploited in various applications, such as musical instruments, radio receivers, and vibrating systems. However, resonance can also lead to structural damage and failures if not properly accounted for in engineering designs.

Summary

  • Damped oscillation involves the gradual decrease in amplitude due to damping forces.
  • Forced oscillation occurs when an external periodic force drives an oscillating system.
  • Resonance is the phenomenon where a system oscillates with a large amplitude when driven at its natural frequency.

Oscillatory motion with its various forms plays a crucial role in many physical phenomena, engineering applications, and everyday life.
