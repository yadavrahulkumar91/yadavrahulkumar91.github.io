1. Volumetric Analysis

1.1 Introduction to Gravimetric Analysis, Volumetric Analysis, and Equivalent Weight

Volumetric analysis, also known as titration, is a quantitative analytical method used to determine the concentration of a substance in a solution. It involves the reaction between the analyte (substance to be analyzed) and a reagent of known concentration (titrant). The volume of titrant required to react completely with the analyte is measured, and from this information, the concentration of the analyte can be calculated.

Gravimetric analysis, on the other hand, is a quantitative analytical method based on the measurement of mass. In this method, the analyte is converted into a solid precipitate of known composition, which is then separated, dried, and weighed. The mass of the precipitate is used to calculate the concentration or amount of the analyte in the original sample.

The equivalent weight of a substance is the mass of the substance that combines with or replaces one mole of hydrogen ions (H+) or one mole of electrons in a redox reaction. It is related to the concept of chemical equivalents and is used in stoichiometric calculations.

1.2 Relationship between Equivalent Weight, Atomic Weight, and Valency

The relationship between the equivalent weight (\(E\)), atomic weight (\(A\)), and valency (\(V\)) can be described by the following equation:

\[E = \frac{A}{V}\]

The equivalent weight is inversely proportional to the valency and directly proportional to the atomic weight of a substance. It represents the mass of a substance that reacts with or is equivalent to one mole of a specific reactant or undergoes a specific chemical change.

1.3 Equivalent Weight of Compounds

The equivalent weight of a compound depends on its function in a reaction. Here are the equivalent weights of different types of compounds:

  • Acids: The equivalent weight of an acid is equal to its molecular weight divided by the number of replaceable hydrogen ions (H+) it can donate.
  • Bases: The equivalent weight of a base is equal to its molecular weight divided by the number of hydroxide ions (OH-) it can donate or the number of moles of protons it can accept.
  • Salts: The equivalent weight of a salt is equal to its molecular weight divided by the total positive or negative charge it carries.
  • Oxidizing and Reducing Agents: The equivalent weight of an oxidizing or reducing agent is equal to its molecular weight divided by the number of moles of electrons it can accept or donate.

1.4 Concentration of Solution and its Units

The concentration of a solution is a measure of the amount of solute dissolved in a given amount of solvent or solution. It can be expressed in various units:

  • Percentage (%): The percentage concentration represents the mass or volume of solute as a percentage of the total mass or volume of the solution.
  • Grams per Liter (g/L): This unit expresses the mass of solute dissolved in one liter of the solution.
  • Molarity (M): Molarity is defined as the number of moles of solute dissolved in one liter of the solution.
  • Molality (m): Molality is defined as the number of moles of solute dissolved in one kilogram of solvent.
  • Normality (N): Normality is a unit of concentration that takes into account the equivalent weight of the solute. It is defined as the number of equivalents of solute per liter of solution.
  • Formality: Formality is similar to molarity and represents the number of formula units of solute per liter of solution. It is commonly used in discussions of acid-base reactions.
  • Parts Per Million (ppm) and Parts Per Billion (ppb): These units represent the concentration of a solute in terms of the number of parts of solute per million or billion parts of the solution.

These concentration units are used in different contexts depending on the nature of the solute, solvent, and the specific application of the solution.

1.5 Primary and Secondary Standard Substances

In volumetric analysis, it is crucial to use standard substances for accurate and precise measurements. These standard substances are known as primary and secondary standards.

Primary Standard: A primary standard is a highly pure compound that can be obtained in a stable and reproducible form. It has a high molar mass and undergoes minimal degradation or decomposition over time. Examples of primary standards include potassium hydrogen phthalate (KHP) for acid-base titrations and potassium dichromate (K2Cr2O7) for redox titrations.

Secondary Standard: A secondary standard is a substance that is standardized against a primary standard. It may not be as pure or stable as a primary standard but can be used to determine the concentration of a solution accurately. Examples of secondary standards include sodium carbonate (Na2CO3) and oxalic acid (C2H2O4).

1.6 Law of Equivalence and Normality Equation

The law of equivalence states that in a chemical reaction, substances react in stoichiometric ratios based on their equivalent weights. It can be represented by the equation:

\[n_1E_1 = n_2E_2\]

Where:

  • \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) represent the number of equivalents of the reactants or products
  • \(E_1\) and \(E_2\) represent the equivalent weights of the reactants or products

The normality equation is derived from the law of equivalence and relates the normality (\(N\)) of a solution to its equivalent weight (\(E\)) and concentration (\(C\)). The equation is:

\[N = \frac{E}{V}\times C\]

Where:

  • \(N\) represents the normality of the solution
  • \(E\) represents the equivalent weight of the solute
  • \(V\) represents the volume of the solution in liters
  • \(C\) represents the concentration of the solution in moles per liter

1.7 Titration and its Types: Acid-Base Titration, Redox Titration

Titration: Titration is a technique used in volumetric analysis to determine the concentration of a substance (analyte) in a solution by reacting it with a standardized solution of another substance (titrant) of known concentration. The point at which the reaction is complete is called the equivalence point, and it is often indicated by a color change or other observable change.

Acid-Base Titration: Acid-base titration is a type of titration that involves the reaction between an acid and a base. The goal is to determine the concentration of one of the substances by using a standardized solution of the other. The equivalence point is reached when the moles of acid are equal to the moles of base, indicating neutralization.

Redox Titration: Redox titration is a type of titration that involves a redox reaction between the analyte and titrant. It is used to determine the concentration of a substance by measuring the volume of the titrant required to completely oxidize or reduce the analyte. Common redox titrations include the use of potassium permanganate (KMnO4) as a titrant in the presence of an acid or cerium sulfate (Ce(SO4)2) as a titrant in the presence of iron(II) ions.

Numerical problems related to acid-base and redox titrations often involve calculations of the molarity or normality of the solution, stoichiometric ratios, and volume measurements.
