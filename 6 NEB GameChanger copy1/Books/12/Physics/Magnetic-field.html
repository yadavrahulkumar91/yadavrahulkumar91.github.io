Magnetic Field Lines and Magnetic Flux; Oersted’s Experiment

Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines used to visualize and represent the magnetic field around a magnet or a current-carrying conductor. These lines provide a graphical representation of the direction and strength of the magnetic field at different points in space.

Key characteristics of magnetic field lines include:

  • Magnetic field lines are continuous and closed loops.
  • The direction of the magnetic field lines indicates the direction of the magnetic field at a given point.
  • The density of magnetic field lines represents the strength of the magnetic field, with closer lines indicating a stronger field.
  • Magnetic field lines never intersect, as the direction of the magnetic field at a point can only have one value.
  • Magnetic field lines tend to curve around the magnet or current-carrying conductor, forming concentric circles or other shapes depending on the geometry.

Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux is a measure of the total magnetic field passing through a given surface. It is represented by the symbol \(\Phi\) and is defined as the product of the magnetic field strength (\(B\)) and the area (\(A\)) perpendicular to the magnetic field.

The mathematical expression for magnetic flux is:

\(\Phi = B \cdot A \cdot \cos(\theta)\)

where:

  • \(\Phi\) is the magnetic flux
  • \(B\) is the magnetic field strength
  • \(A\) is the area
  • \(\theta\) is the angle between the magnetic field and the normal to the surface

Magnetic flux is a useful concept in understanding the interaction of magnetic fields with conductors, as it is related to phenomena such as electromagnetic induction.

Oersted’s Experiment

Oersted’s experiment, conducted by Hans Christian Oersted in 1820, demonstrated the connection between electricity and magnetism. In the experiment, Oersted observed the deflection of a compass needle when placed near a current-carrying wire.

The key observations and conclusions of Oersted’s experiment are:

  • When a current flows through a wire, a magnetic field is produced around the wire.
  • The magnetic field produced by a current-carrying wire is circular, with the wire at the center of the circles.
  • The direction of the magnetic field can be determined using the right-hand rule, where the thumb points in the direction of the current and the curled fingers indicate the direction of the magnetic field.
  • The deflection of the compass needle near the wire indicated the presence and direction of the magnetic field.
  • Oersted’s experiment provided experimental evidence for the fundamental relationship between electric currents and magnetic fields, leading to the development of the field of electromagnetism.

Force on Moving Charge; Force on a Conductor

Force on Moving Charge

When a charged particle, such as an electron or a proton, moves in the presence of an electric or magnetic field, it experiences a force known as the Lorentz force. The Lorentz force acting on a moving charge ($q$) is given by the equation:

$\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B})$

where:

  • $\vec{F}$ is the force experienced by the charge
  • $\vec{E}$ is the electric field
  • $\vec{v}$ is the velocity of the charge
  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field

The electric field component ($\vec{E}$) exerts a force on the charge due to its charge interaction, while the magnetic field component ($\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$) exerts a force due to the charge's motion in the magnetic field. The magnitude and direction of the force depend on the charge's velocity, the strength of the electric and magnetic fields, and the charge's charge.

Force on a Conductor

When a conductor carrying an electric current is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force called the magnetic force. The force acting on a conductor carrying current ($I$) in a magnetic field ($\vec{B}$) is given by the equation:

$\vec{F} = I(\vec{L} \times \vec{B})$

where:

  • $\vec{F}$ is the force experienced by the conductor
  • $\vec{L}$ is the length of the conductor in the direction of the current
  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field

The force on the conductor is perpendicular to both the length of the conductor and the magnetic field. The magnitude of the force depends on the current flowing through the conductor, the length of the conductor, and the strength of the magnetic field.

Applications

The force on moving charges and conductors has various applications in physics and engineering:

  • Electric Motors and Generators: The interaction between the magnetic field and the current-carrying conductors is the basis of electric motors and generators, which convert electrical energy to mechanical energy (in motors) or vice versa (in generators).
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): The force on moving charges is utilized in MRI machines to create detailed images of the body's internal structures. The magnetic fields and the motion of protons in the body produce signals that are detected and processed to generate the images.
  • Particle Accelerators: Powerful magnetic fields are used in particle accelerators to control and guide charged particles, allowing scientists to study the fundamental properties of matter and conduct experiments in high-energy physics.
  • Electromagnetic Induction: The force on a conductor in a magnetic field plays a crucial role in electromagnetic induction, which is the basis of electric power generation, transformers, and various electrical devices.
  • Magnetic Levitation: By utilizing the force on a conductor, magnetic levitation systems can suspend objects, such as trains or Maglev trains, above magnetic tracks, reducing friction and allowing for high-speed transportation.

Force and Torque on Rectangular Coil, Moving Coil Galvanometer

Force on a Rectangular Coil

When a rectangular coil carrying current is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force due to the interaction between the magnetic field and the current. The force on a rectangular coil can be calculated using the following equation:

$\vec{F} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{I} \cdot \vec{A}$

where:

  • $\vec{F}$ is the force on the coil
  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\vec{I}$ is the current flowing through the coil
  • $\vec{A}$ is the area of the coil

The force is perpendicular to both the magnetic field and the current direction. The magnitude of the force depends on the strength of the magnetic field, the current flowing through the coil, and the area of the coil.

Torque on a Rectangular Coil

When a rectangular coil is placed in a magnetic field and current flows through it, it experiences a torque or turning moment. The torque on a rectangular coil can be calculated using the following equation:

$\vec{\tau} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{I} \cdot \vec{A} \cdot \vec{n}$

where:

  • $\vec{\tau}$ is the torque on the coil
  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\vec{I}$ is the current flowing through the coil
  • $\vec{A}$ is the area of the coil
  • $\vec{n}$ is the perpendicular distance between the coil and the axis of rotation

The torque causes the coil to rotate, and the direction of rotation depends on the direction of the current and the magnetic field.

Moving Coil Galvanometer

A moving coil galvanometer is a device used to measure small electric currents. It consists of a rectangular coil suspended between the poles of a permanent magnet. When a current flows through the coil, it experiences a torque, causing it to rotate.

The deflection of the coil is proportional to the current passing through it. By calibrating the galvanometer, it can be used as an ammeter or voltmeter to measure current or voltage, respectively.

The torque on the moving coil can be given by the equation:

$\vec{\tau} = \vec{N} \cdot \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A} \cdot \sin(\theta)$

where:

  • $\vec{\tau}$ is the torque on the coil
  • $\vec{N}$ is the number of turns of the coil
  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\vec{A}$ is the area of the coil
  • $\theta$ is the angle between the normal to the coil and the magnetic field

The deflection of the coil can be measured using a pointer attached to the coil, which moves over a scale calibrated in units of current or voltage.

Hall Effect

The Hall Effect is a phenomenon in physics discovered by Edwin Hall in 1879. It describes the generation of a voltage across a conductor or semiconductor when it is subjected to a magnetic field perpendicular to the direction of the electric current flowing through it.

Principle of Hall Effect

When a current-carrying conductor or semiconductor is placed in a magnetic field perpendicular to the direction of the current, a force acts on the moving charges due to the interaction between the magnetic field and the charges. This force, known as the Lorentz force, causes the charges to be deflected to one side of the conductor.

The accumulation of charges on one side of the conductor creates an electric field that opposes the further flow of charges. Eventually, the electric field becomes strong enough to balance the Lorentz force, resulting in a steady-state condition known as the Hall equilibrium. In this equilibrium, the force due to the magnetic field is exactly counteracted by the electric field, leading to a transverse potential difference, or Hall voltage, across the conductor.

Hall Voltage and Hall Coefficient

The Hall voltage ($V_H$) is directly proportional to the product of the magnetic field strength ($B$), the current ($I$), and a constant known as the Hall coefficient ($R_H$). The Hall coefficient depends on the material and geometry of the conductor or semiconductor.

$V_H = R_H \cdot \frac{IB}{t}$

where:

  • $V_H$ is the Hall voltage
  • $R_H$ is the Hall coefficient
  • $I$ is the current
  • $B$ is the magnetic field strength
  • $t$ is the thickness of the conductor or semiconductor

The Hall coefficient can provide information about the charge carriers in the material, such as their density and mobility.

Applications of Hall Effect

The Hall Effect has various applications in physics and engineering:

  • Magnetic Field Measurement: The Hall Effect can be used to measure the strength and polarity of magnetic fields. Hall effect sensors are widely used in applications such as current sensors, position sensors, and magnetic field measurements.
  • Velocity and Displacement Measurements: By applying the Hall Effect in conjunction with the Lorentz force, it is possible to measure the velocity and displacement of moving objects.
  • Semiconductor Characterization: The Hall Effect is used to study the electrical properties of semiconductors, including their carrier concentration, mobility, and type (n-type or p-type).
  • Electrical Switches and Controls: Hall effect switches are employed in various electronic devices, such as proximity switches, automotive sensors, and brushless DC motors, due to their non-contact operation and reliability.

Magnetic Field of a Moving Charge

When a charged particle moves through space, it creates a magnetic field around it. This phenomenon is described by Ampere's law and the Biot-Savart law.

Ampere's Law

Ampere's law states that the magnetic field ($\vec{B}$) produced by a moving charge is directly proportional to the product of the charge ($q$), the velocity of the charge ($\vec{v}$), and the sine of the angle ($\theta$) between the velocity vector and the direction of observation.

$\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{q \cdot \vec{v} \times \vec{r}}{r^3}$

where:

  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $q$ is the charge of the particle
  • $\vec{v}$ is the velocity vector of the particle
  • $\vec{r}$ is the vector from the charge to the point of observation
  • $r$ is the magnitude of the vector $\vec{r}$

The direction of the magnetic field can be determined using the right-hand rule, where the thumb points in the direction of the velocity vector and the curled fingers indicate the direction of the magnetic field.

Biot-Savart Law

The Biot-Savart law is a fundamental law in electromagnetism that relates the magnetic field produced by a small current element to the current and its distance from the observation point. It can be used to calculate the magnetic field of a moving charge by treating the charge as a current element.

$\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{I \cdot \vec{dl} \times \vec{r}}{r^3}$

where:

  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $I$ is the current
  • $\vec{dl}$ is the differential length element along the current path
  • $\vec{r}$ is the vector from the current element to the point of observation
  • $r$ is the magnitude of the vector $\vec{r}$

The Biot-Savart law allows for the calculation of the magnetic field at any point in space due to the motion of a charged particle.

Biot-Savart Law and Its Applications

The Biot-Savart law is a fundamental principle in electromagnetism that allows us to calculate the magnetic field produced by a current-carrying conductor. It states that the magnetic field ($\vec{B}$) at a point in space is directly proportional to the product of the current ($I$) and a differential element of the current path ($d\vec{l}$), and inversely proportional to the square of the distance ($r$) between the current element and the point of observation.

Biot-Savart Law for a Circular Coil

When applying the Biot-Savart law to a circular coil with radius $R$ and carrying a current $I$, the magnetic field at a point on the axis of the coil can be calculated as:

$\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{2\pi R^2 I}{(R^2 + z^2)^{3/2}} \cdot \hat{z}$

where:

  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $I$ is the current in the coil
  • $R$ is the radius of the coil
  • $z$ is the distance along the axis of the coil from the center of the coil to the point of observation
  • $\hat{z}$ is the unit vector in the direction of the axis

Biot-Savart Law for a Long Straight Conductor

For a long straight conductor carrying a current $I$, the magnetic field at a point located at a perpendicular distance $r$ from the conductor can be calculated as:

$\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{I}{r} \cdot \hat{\phi}$

where:

  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $I$ is the current in the conductor
  • $r$ is the perpendicular distance from the conductor to the point of observation
  • $\hat{\phi}$ is the unit vector in the azimuthal direction

Biot-Savart Law for a Long Solenoid

For a long solenoid with $N$ turns per unit length, carrying a current $I$, the magnetic field inside the solenoid can be calculated as:

$\vec{B} = \mu_0 \cdot N \cdot I \cdot \hat{z}$

where:

  • $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $N$ is the number of turns per unit length of the solenoid
  • $I$ is the current in the solenoid
  • $\hat{z}$ is the unit vector in the direction of the axis of the solenoid

The Biot-Savart law provides a mathematical framework to calculate the magnetic field produced by various current configurations and is widely used in the study of electromagnetism.

Ampere's Law and Its Applications

Ampere's law is a fundamental principle in electromagnetism that relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the electric current passing through the loop. It states that the line integral of the magnetic field ($\vec{B}$) around a closed path is equal to the product of the permeability of free space ($\mu_0$) and the total enclosed current ($I_{\text{enc}}$).

Ampere's Law for a Long Straight Conductor

When applying Ampere's law to a long straight conductor carrying a current $I$, the magnetic field along a circular path of radius $r$ around the conductor can be calculated as:

$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 \cdot I_{\text{enc}}$

where:

  • $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l}$ is the line integral of the magnetic field along the closed path
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the total enclosed current by the closed path

Ampere's Law for a Straight Solenoid

For a long straight solenoid with $N$ turns per unit length and carrying a current $I$, the magnetic field inside the solenoid can be calculated using Ampere's law as:

$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 \cdot N \cdot I_{\text{enc}}$

where:

  • $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l}$ is the line integral of the magnetic field along the closed path
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $N$ is the number of turns per unit length of the solenoid
  • $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the total enclosed current by the closed path

Ampere's Law for a Toroidal Solenoid

For a toroidal solenoid with $N$ turns and carrying a current $I$, the magnetic field inside the toroid can be calculated using Ampere's law as:

$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 \cdot N \cdot I_{\text{enc}}$

where:

  • $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l}$ is the line integral of the magnetic field along the closed path
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $N$ is the number of turns of the toroid
  • $I_{\text{enc}}$ is the total enclosed current by the closed path

Ampere's law provides a powerful tool to calculate the magnetic field produced by current-carrying conductors, solenoids, and toroids. It allows us to understand and analyze the magnetic fields in various configurations.

Force between Two Parallel Conductors and Definition of Ampere

Force between Two Parallel Conductors

The force between two parallel conductors carrying electric currents is governed by Ampere's law. According to Ampere's law, the force per unit length between the two conductors is proportional to the product of their currents and inversely proportional to the distance between them.

The formula to calculate the force per unit length ($F$) between two parallel conductors carrying currents $I_1$ and $I_2$ separated by a distance $d$ is given by:

$F = \frac{{\mu_0 \cdot I_1 \cdot I_2}}{{2\pi \cdot d}}$

where:

  • $F$ is the force per unit length between the conductors
  • $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A}$)
  • $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the currents in the two conductors
  • $d$ is the distance between the conductors

Definition of Ampere

The unit of electric current, the Ampere (A), is defined in terms of the force between two parallel conductors carrying current. One Ampere is defined as the current that, when flowing through two infinitely long parallel conductors placed one meter apart in a vacuum, produces a force per unit length of $2 \times 10^{-7}$ N/m between the conductors.

Therefore, the force per unit length formula can be rearranged to define the Ampere:

$1 \, \text{A} = \frac{{2 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{N/m}}}{{\mu_0}}$

This definition establishes a relationship between electric current and the force between parallel conductors, allowing for the measurement and standardization of current using the Ampere unit.
