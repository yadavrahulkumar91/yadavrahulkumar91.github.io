Introduction

  • Members of Platyhelminthes are called Flat worms because they are dorsiventrally flattened.
  • Study of Platyhelminthes and Nemathelminthes is called Helminthology.
  • Diseases caused by Platyhelminthes and Aschelminthes are called Helminthic diseases.
  • Habitat:
    • Most members are Parasitic.

Planaria (Dugesia)

General Characteristics

  • Planaria can evert its pharynx.
  • Planaria is gregarious (i.e., they live in groups) and omnivorous.
  • Epidermis:
    • Presence of Rhabdites.
    • Syncytial epidermis
  • Genital pore lies posterior to Pharynx.
  • Planarians have remarkable power of regeneration: Neoblasts from mesoderm help in regeneration.
    • If an individual is cut transversely into two parts, the anterior fragment will regenerate a new tail and the posterior piece will develop a new head. It involves two processes:
      1. Epimorphosis
      2. Morphollaxis
    • Planaria has lateral polarity. If the head is cut longitudinally into 2 or several parts along the middle line, each part grows into a complete head, thus forming a several-headed Planaria. This is heteromorphosis.
    • Pieces of one planaria can also be grafted into another.

Reproduction

  • Sexual
    • Planaria are Hermaphrodite.
      1. Eggs are ectolecithal (i.e., yolk is deposited outside).
      2. Fertilization = Internal
      3. Cleavage = Spiral
  • Asexual
    • Binary fission.

Fasciola hepatica (Liver Fluke)

Residence in Sheep

  • Resides in Bile passages in the liver of sheep.

Epidermis of Fasciola hepatica

  • Syncytial

Excretory organ: Flame cells (Solenocytes)

  • Flame cells are connected to excretory canals by their canaliculi.
  • Excretory products: CO2, NH3, and fatty acids.
  • Excretory pore opens at the extreme posterior end of the body.

Reproduction

  • Reproductive systems are extremely well developed and quite complex.
  • Mid-dorsal part contains a minute pore for copulation.
  • Bisexual (Hermaphrodite)

Life Cycle of Fasciola hepatica

  • Fasciola hepatica is Digenetic
    • Primary host: Sheep
      1. Disease causing: 'Liver rot'
      2. Fasciolopsis is caused by Fasciola busci
      3. Fasciola infection can be treated with the veterinary Fasciolide triclabendazole.
    • Secondary host: Snail
      1. Fasciola is harmless to snail but is pathogenic in vertebrate hosts including man (rarely).
  • Larval stages complete into five stages:
    1. Sporocyst gives rise to Redia, Redia to Cercaria, and Cercaria is transformed into Metacercaria. This phenomenon is Polyembryony.
  • Fasciola exhibits both alternation of generation and alternation of host.
  • Fasciola cannot complete its lifecycle in the absence of water.
    • It is because of the lack of Snails.

Schistosoma (Blood/ Lymphatic Fluke of Man)

Schistosoma infecting man are:

  • Schistosoma haematobium
  • Schistosoma mansoni
  • Schistosoma japonicum

The old name of Schistosoma is Bilharzia.

  • The German parasitologist Theodor Bilhartz discovered S. haematobium responsible for urinary schistosomiasis.
  • Schistosoma haematobium causes kidney damage.

Location of Schistosoma in Human Body

  • Small branches of urinary bladder
  • Urinary tract
  • Mesenteric veins
  • Portal veins
  • Lymph nodes

Reproduction

Dioecious with well-defined sexual dimorphism:

  • Male is Smaller and Thicker.
  • Female is Longer, Slender, and Cylindrical.
  • Female is lodged in the Gynaecophoral canal of male.

Life Cycle

Digenetic:

  • Primary host = Man
  • Secondary host = Balinus or other Snails

The eggs pass out from man with urine or faeces and hatch when they come in contact with water.

Disease

  • Infection is caused either by penetration of Cercaria through the skin or by contaminated water.
  • Disease called Schistosomiasis or Bilharziasis.
  • Blood flukes sometimes produce a severe cercarial dermatitis called "Swimmer's itch".

Symptoms

  • Skin rash
  • Bronchial cough
  • Anaemia
  • Haematuria
  • Bloody stool

Treatment

Prazi-quantel

Taenia solium

1. Taenia infecting man

  • Taenia solium (Pork tapeworm)
  • Taenia saginata (Beef tapeworm)

2. Life cycle

  • Digenetic
    • Primary host = Man (Adult tapeworm lives in the Small intestine of Man)
    • Secondary host = Pig or Cattle (Larval stages live in them)

3. Body structure

Tapeworm possesses an elongated ribbon or tape-like segmented body (pseudometamerism).

Head (Scolex)

  • Apical part of Scolex is Rostellum.
  • Rostellum bears two circles of chitinous hooks (28-32)
  • Four suckers or Acetabula (for attachment)

Neck

  • Unsegmented part
  • New segments are formed in this region (Budding zone or Proliferation zone)
  • Narrowest part of the body

Strobila

  • Main body made of 800-1000 similar rectangular segments called Proglottids
  • Three types of proglottids: Young, Mature, and Gravid

Body walls

  • Body wall lacks a cellular epidermis
  • Consists of Cuticle, Musculature, and Parenchyma (Mesodermal tissue)
  • Body cavity is absent in tapeworm (acoelomate).

    • 4. Nutrition

    • Taenia has no digestive system, relies on predigested nutrients absorbed through the cuticle
    • Absorbs nutrients through general body surface (Osmotrophy)
    • Complete absence of an alimentary canal is a unique parasite adaptation
    • The cuticle protects tapeworm from the host's digestive enzymes

    9. Taenia saginata

    • Taenia saginata is more prevalent in man than Taenia solium
    • Taenia saginata is longer than Taenia solium
