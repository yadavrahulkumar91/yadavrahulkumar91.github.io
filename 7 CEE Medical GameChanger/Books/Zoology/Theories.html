Theories of Evolution

A. Lamarckism/ Theory of Inheritance of Acquired Characters/ Theory of Use and Disuse of Organs

  1. I. Jean Baptiste de Lamarck

    1. French naturalist
    2. First to suggest a complete theory of evolution (organic evolution)
    3. Lamarck's theory was published in his book "Philosophie Zoologique"
    4. Lamarck's theory was most severely criticized by Cuvier who greeted it by calling ????

  2. II. Postulates

    1. Internal forces tend to increase the size of the body
    2. Formation of new organs is the result of the need or want continuously felt by organisms. It is also known as the Doctrine of Desires or Appetency
    3. Development and power of action of an organ are directly proportional to its use
    4. All changes acquired by the organism during its life are transmitted to offspring by the process of inheritance
      • This is most controversial
      • Known as Lamarckian Doctrine

  3. III. Lamarck believed in the direct influence of the environment on the individual

  4. IV. The greatest blow to Lamarckism came from the work of the German scientist August Weismann

  5. V. Weismann cut the tails of mice for 80 generations, but no single mouse was born without a tail

  6. VI. Weismann formulated his famous theory "Theory of Continuity of Germplasm"

  7. VII. Neo-Lamarckians believe that only those characters, whatever their origin and nature, that could affect germplasm are heritable, and others are not

  8. VIII. Examples

    1. Formation of web üï∏ between toes in aquatic birds
    2. Formation of long-necked giraffe
    3. Loss of limbs in snakes

B. Darwinism

  1. I. Charles Darwin

    1. British naturalist
    2. In 1831, at the age of 22, he was appointed upon a world survey ship of the British government, H.M.S. Beagle.
    3. Russel Wallace also traveled widely. Evolutionary ideas similar to those of Darwin developed independently in Wallace's mind.
    4. Both Darwin and Wallace jointly propounded the "Theory of Natural Selection".
    5. The thinking of both Darwin and Wallace was greatly influenced by essays on "Principle of Population" written by T.R. Malthus.
    6. Darwin and Wallace published a joint paper titled "Origin of Species".
    7. Later, Darwin published his detailed theory in his book titled "On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection".

  2. II. Postulates

    1. Overproduction
    2. Struggle for existence
      1. Intraspecific
        • High competition
      2. Interspecific
      3. With Environment
    3. Heredity and evolution
    4. Natural selection (Darwin) / Survival of the fittest (Spencer)
    5. Changing environment needs structural modifications
    6. Origin of species

    2. Didn't distinguish between somatic and germinal variations.

    4. Darwinism doesn't take into account for variation with heredity.

D. Mutation theory

  1. I. Hugo de Vries

    Dutch Botanist

  2. II. Experimental plant of Vries = Oenothera lamarckiana (Evening primrose)

    Mutation observed = Chromosomal number variant

  3. III. Mutations are discontinuous variations, called "Sports" by Darwin and "Saltatory variations" by Bateson.

  4. IV. Mutations are generally harmful and recessive.

  5. V. Role of mutations in evolution is genetic variations.

  6. VI. Mutations are the changes which may or may not be inherited.

  7. VII. Mutation theory successfully explains

    1. progressive and retrogressive evolution
    2. Continuation of Vestigial and Overspecialized organs
    3. Struggle for existence and
    4. Survival of the fittest along with inheritance of useful mutations and
    5. Formation of new species

  8. VIII. Germinal mutation is a change that is inherited.

  9. IX. Hereditary variations in plants have been produced by the use of X-rays.

  10. X. Frequency of a mutated gene in a population is expected to increase if that gene is selected by nature.

  11. XI. The possibilities of hereditary and evolutionary changes are greater in species that reproduce by sexual means.

  12. XII. Organic evolution would not have taken place if individuals in a population did not show gene variations.
