Taxes

Klinotaxis

  • Zig-zag movement.
  • Undulating movement.
  • Movement by comparing the intensities of stimulation on two sides of the body.
  • By bending right and left in response to stimulus until the receptors of two sides are equally stimulated.
  • Examples:
    • Organisms that bear receptors but no sense organs (e.g., Euglena, Planaria, Earthworm).

Tropotaxis

  • Behavioral response of bilaterally symmetrical animals.
  • Orientation of the body straight towards the stimulus.
  • Examples:
    • Argulus (Fish louse)
    • Planaria
    • Grayling butterflies
  • Grayling butterflies always orient towards the sun by turning to the position in which both their eyes are equally stimulated.
  • If one of their eyes is made blind, they move in a circle.

Telotaxis

  • If two sources of stimuli of the same intensity operate at the same time, bilaterally symmetrical animals orient at any one of the stimuli but not intermediate or both. Known as Telotaxis.
  • Examples:
    • Honey bees going for foraging in the morning.
    • Orientation of Dragonfly towards its prey.

Menotaxis

  • Orientation of the body at a constant angle to the source of stimulus.
  • Also known as "Light Compass" response.
  • Exhibited by insects like ants and honeybees.

Mnemotaxis

  • Orientation of animals depending on complex stimuli like direction, landmarks, trail of chemicals, etc.
  • Removal of any landmarks may change the path while returning to the nest.
  • Example: Hunting wasps.

Animal Adaptations

Aquatic Adaptation (Water Inhabitants)

Primary Aquatic Adaptation / Gill Breathers

  • Adaptative features:
    • Body contour:
      • Body shape: streamlined, laterally compressed
      • Head: subconical
      • Neck: absent
    • Locomotion:
      • Primary locomotory organ: lateral undulation of flexible body
      • Secondary locomotory organ: fins
    • Swim bladder/air bladder:
      • Hydrostatic function
      • Also functions in breathing
    • Gills:
      • Primary respiratory organs
    • Lateral line sense organs:
      • Detect changes caused by mechanical disturbance in surrounding water
    • Scales:
      • Form protective covering / exoskeleton
    • Nictitating membrane / 3rd eyelid:
      • Eye protection and for clear vision

Secondary Aquatic Adaptation / Lung Breathers

  • Adaptative features:
    • Body contour:
      • Body: streamlined
      • Head: subconical
      • Neck: shortens
      • Tail: enlarges
    • Locomotive mechanism:
      • Oar propulsion (equally sized limbs)
      • Tail propulsion (hind limbs tend to disappear)
    • Limbs:
      • Webbed feet, limbs developed into flipper or paddles
      • Hyperdactyly and hyperphalangy for water displacement
    • Integument:
      • Reduced hair and skin glands; subcutaneous fat layer 'Blubber'

Aerial / Volant Adaptation (Flying)

  • Adaptative features:
    • Body shape: boat or spindle shaped
    • Body covering: feathers
    • Strong flight muscles: pectoralis major and pectoralis minor
    • Modified forelimbs into wings, strong hind limbs for perching
    • Endoskeleton, inelastic lungs with air sacs
    • Well-developed nervous system, single functional ovary

Desert Adaptation

  • Adaptative features:
    • Water conservation mechanisms:
      • Tough, thick, and rough skin
      • Reduced sweat glands
      • Concentrated urine
      • Presence of water cells in stomach
    • Well-developed senses protected from sand
    • Hygroscopic skin in desert lizard absorbs water from atmosphere

Fossorial / Burrowing Adaptation

  • Adaptative features:
    • Body contour:
      • Body shape: cylindrical (snakes, ichthyphis), fusiform (moles)
      • Head tapers to form a snout
      • Powerful forelimbs with digging claws

Arboreal / Scansorial Adaptation (Climbing)

  • Adaptative features:
    • Body contour:
      • Long, narrow, elongated slender body
      • Elongation of dorsolumbar vertebrae
      • Limbs girdles: strongly built
      • Limbs: usually plantigrade
      • Feet: prehensile or nonprehensile
      • Tail: prehensile, balancing organ

Cursorial / Running Adaptation

  • Adaptative features:
    • Body shape: spindle-shaped or streamlined
    • Neck shortens
    • Hind limbs more stout, muscular, and longer

Fish Migration

Types of Fish Migration based on Sea and Freshwater

Diadromous

Migrate between Freshwater and Sea.

Catadromous

  • Lives in Freshwater and migrates to the sea for breeding
  • Examples:
    • American eel (Anguilla rostrata)
    • European eel (Anguilla anguilla)

Anadromous

  • Lives in the Sea and migrates to Freshwater for breeding
  • Examples:
    • Salmon
    • Sea lamprey
    • Hilsa

Amphidromous

  • Migrates between freshwater and the sea, not for breeding but for feeding
  • Occurs regularly at specific stages of their life cycle
  • Examples:
    • Gobies fish

Potamodromous

  • Migrates within freshwater for breeding
  • Examples:
    • Carp
    • Trout

Oceanodromous

  • Migrates within the sea for breeding
  • Examples:
    • Herrings
    • Mackerels
    • Tunas
