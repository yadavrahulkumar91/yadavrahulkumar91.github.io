C. Habit and Habitat

  1. Distribution = Cosmopolitan
  2. Amphibious or Sternohygrous.
  3. During breeding season frog must descend into pond water for copulation or pairing and for the deposition of eggs.
  4. Feeding behaviour: Tadpole is herbivorous; Adult frog carnivorous, feeding on spiders, insects, worms, insects larvae etc.
    1. It is also cannibal, feeding on its tadpoles and small frogs.
    2. Prey is generally caught alive and when in motion small prey is captured by the sticky tongue.
  5. Locomotion: It swims (in water), leaping, jumping and walking (over land).
  6. Breeding behaviour: Breeding in frogs involves croaking, copulation along with oviposition and life cycle in sequence.
  7. Croaking: Done by male frogs only.
    1. Produced by vibrations of vocal cords.
    2. "Is an advertisement of home and husband".
    3. Male frog
      • Narrow abdomen
      • Presence of Vocal sacs
      • Amplexusary pads/Copulatory pads
  8. Poikilothermic - Cold-blooded animal.
  9. Two steps: Suspended animation
    1. Hibernation: Winter sleep
    2. Aestivation: Summer sleep

D. External Features of Frog

Shape and size:

  1. Body bilateral symmetry, spindle-shaped and somewhat dorsoventrally flattened and streamlined with bluntly pointed ends but it lacks neck.
  2. No exoskeleton, skin surface smooth and slimy.

Body division:

  • Body divisible into Head and Trunk.

Head

  1. Is large, triangular, flattened and forms snout; not completely marked off from the trunk.
  2. Bears mouth, external nostrils, eyes and ear-drums.
    • Mouth is large, terminal, semicircular wide opening bounded by hard immovable lips.
    • External nares or Nostrils: Two in number, one on either side of the snout, small circular openings, only respiratory in nature.
    • Eyes: Two, large bulging, dorsolateral, thick pigmented and immovable upper eyelid, rudimentary and movable lower eyelid.
    • Nictitating membrane, an outgrowth of the lower eyelid is transparent, movable and functional.
    • No external ears, but behind and below the eye on either side, is an obliquely placed elongated patch of dark colour, in the middle of which is a circular area, the tympanic membrane or the tympanum which is supported internally by a firm cartilaginous ring.
    • Head also bears a small lightly coloured spot just in front of the eyes. This spot, called the brown spot, is said to mark the third eye possessed by remote ancestors of the frog.
    • Neck: Absent

Trunk

  1. Short, broad and somewhat flattened with a characteristic dorsal hump.
  2. It possesses two pairs of limbs.
  3. A pair of forelimbs are short and consists of three parts.
    • Upper arm,
    • Forearm, and
    • Hand or Manus
  4. The hand consists of a palm and four nailless fingers; there is no thumb or pollex.
  5. The inner finger of the male develops at its base a swelling during the breeding season. It is called nuptial or amplexusory pad.
  6. A pair of hind limbs are longer and possess
    • Thigh or Femur,
    • Shank or Crus, and
    • Foot or Pes
  7. In the resting stage, hind limbs are arranged in a Z-shaped.
  8. The feet have remarkable elongated ankles and five webbed but nailless toes or digits, which are admirably adapted for swimming and jumping.
  9. On the ventral side of toes are a number of round pads, one under each joint. These are called subarticular pads.
  10. A cloacal aperture lies at the extreme posterior end. It is a common opening for faecal matter, urine, sperms, and eggs.
  11. Tail: Absent

Skin

  1. Thin, smooth (naked without scales), and slippery due to the presence of mucus.
  2. Loosely attached to body wall due to subcutaneous lymph spaces below the dermis.
  3. Skin color, however, changes to match with the surroundings; darker in damp cool places and lighter brown in sunshine. This phenomenon is called Metachrosis. It is protective coloration.
    • Synchronous: "Synchronous" tumors refer to cases in which the second primary cancer is diagnosed within 6 months of the primary cancer;
    • Metachronous: "Metachronous" tumors refer to cases in which the second primary cancer is diagnosed more than 6 months after the diagnosis of the first primary cancer.
  4. Skin consists of two distinct regions: Epidermis and Dermis.

Epidermis

  • Outer layer of the skin and which is thin, non-vascularized, and stratified epithelium.

Stratum corneum

  • Dead, Non-nucleated, Flat, Dry
  • Outermost layer of epidermis
  • Consists of flattened squamous epithelial cells arranged ……
  • Its outermost layer consists of completely keratinized cells (dead cells) which are cast off periodically. This phenomenon is called Moulting or Ecdysis.

Stratum germinativum or Malpighian layer:

  • Innermost layer of the epidermis
  • This layer is just one cell thick and composed of active columnar cells, resting on a thin basement membrane.
  • Its cells continually divided and add cells to the outer surface which undergoes keratinization.
  • During this, the cells acquire proteinous substance keratohyaline which dissolves into the protoplasm to form shiny, semitransparent, waterproof Eleidin which hardens forming Keratin.

Dermis:

  • Inner layer of skin which is vascular and thicker than the epidermis.
  • Consists of connective tissue, muscle fibers, blood vessels, capillaries, cutaneous glands, and chromatophores.
  • Mesodermal in origin.
  • Divisible into:

Stratum spongiosum

  • Loose and spongy, lying just below the basement membrane.
  • Composed of connective tissue fibers, blood vessels, blood capillaries, lymph spaces, nerve fibers, cutaneous glands, and chromatophores.
  • Cutaneous glands are simple alveolar glands of two types:
    • Mucous glands
    • Poison glands
  • Chromatophores provide coloration to the skin. These are of three types:
    • Melanophores: Brownish black
    • Xanthophores: Reddish and Yellowish
    • Guanophores: Whitish
    • The distribution of the pigment within the chromatophore is controlled by hormones:
      • Intermedin (from the anterior pituitary)
      • Epinephrine (from the supra-renal or adrenal gland)

Stratum compactum

  • Largely made of muscle fibers and connective tissue whose dense collagen fibers run in a wavy course parallel to the surface of the skin.

Functions of skin:

  1. Protective, sensory, gives texture to the body.
  2. Respiratory structure because permeable to water.
  3. Nutritive.

Body cavity/ Coelom

  • Whole trunk contains a fluid-filled space called a body cavity or coelom.
  • It is covered on the outside by parietal peritoneum and visceral peritoneum.
  • Pericardial cavity surrounds the heart;
  • Pleural cavity surrounds the lungs and
  • Abdominal cavity is present in the abdominal region.

Alimentary Canal

Alimentary canal is not very long because the adult animal is carnivorous.

1. Mouth:

  • Wide, terminal, horizontal, bounded by upper and lower jaws.
  • Lower jaw is movable.

2. Buccopharyngeal cavity:

  • Dorsoventrally flattened wide space between upper and lower jaws.
  • Teeth
    • Lower jaw is devoid of teeth.
    • Upper jaw with a semicircular row of submarginal maxillary teeth and two patches of vomerine teeth between internal nares and bulging of eyes.
    • Types of teeth
      1. Incisor
      2. Premolar
      3. Molar
        • Canines are absent in Frog and Rabbit.
    • Dentition
      • Homodont (all alike)
      • Acrodont (attached to the jaw bone)
      • Polyphyodont (many sets)
    • Teeth are small, sharp and backwardly directed, meant for preventing the escape of prey, and not for mastication.
    • Dental formula absent.
  • Tongue
    • Large, muscular, sticky, bifid at its tip
    • Protusible, retractile
    • Attached anteriorly and free posteriorly
    • Taste buds are absent.
    • Taste papillae are present.
    • Darting of the tongue is due to
      1. Presence of protractor + retractor hypoglossal muscles.
      2. Presence of lymph in the sublingual lymph sinus.
  • Terminal Region of Upper Jaw
    • Possesses three depressions or sub-rostral fossae.
    • Corresponding region of the lower jaw has three prelingual elevations for tight closing of the mouth.
  • Pharynx
    • Contains:
      1. Openings of eustachian tubes
      2. Gullet (for esophagus)
      3. Glottis (for respiratory passage)
  • In males, openings of the vocal sacs may be seen.
  • Epiglottis is absent.
  • No digestion takes place due to the absence of salivary glands.

3. Oesophagus

  • Short broad tube, less developed.
  • Its wall having pepsinogen, an inactive enzyme, secreting glands but the function of pepsinogen takes place in the stomach.

4. Stomach

  • Large, broad, 5-6 cm long curved muscular sac on the left side of the body cavity.
    • Anterior: Cardiac part
    • Posterior: Pyloric part, with pyloric sphincter.
  • Wall of the stomach posses a large number of gastric glands which are evenly distributed in the cardiac and pyloric parts.
    • Gastric glands are of 3 types:
      1. Chief/ Peptic or Zymogen gland = Secretes Pepsinogen
        • Pepsinogen ----HCl----> Pepsin
        • Proteins ----Pepsin----> Peptones + Peptides
      2. Oxyntic cell = Secretes HCl
      3. Mucous gland = Secretes mucin.
  • Stomach performs at least three functions:
    1. Storage
    2. Mechanical mixing
    3. Chemical modification of food

5. Small intestine

  • Absence of Brunner's gland and Crypts of Lieberkuhn.
  • Differentiated into anterior duodenum and posterior ileum.
    • Duodenum
      1. U-shaped
      2. Receives Hepatopancreatic duct in the proximal region, which brings bile juice and pancreatic juice.
      3. Absence of Ampulla of Vater and Sphincter of Oddi.
    • Ileum
      1. Its internal surface has longitudinal folds called villi for increasing absorptive surface area.
      2. Its wall (mucosa) contains a large number of intestinal glands secreting intestinal juice/ succus entericus.

6. Rectum (Large intestine)

  • Ileum dilates posteriorly to form Rectum.
  • Main function of Rectum is absorption of water, ions/salts.

7. Cloaca (Cloacal chamber)

  • It is a common chamber where
    1. Rectum
    2. Uterers
    3. Oviducts
    4. Urinary bladder open.
  • It leads to the outside through the cloacal aperture.

8. Anus

  • Openings of rectum into the cloaca are called Anus.
  • It is normally kept closed with the help of the anal sphincter.

Associated Digestive Glands

1. Liver

  • Largest gland as well as the largest organ of the body.
  • Divided into three lobes
    1. Right lobe
    2. Middle lobe
      • The middle lobe bears a greenish bag-like structure called the Gall bladder.
      • Gall bladder
        • Stores and concentrates bile secreted by the liver.
        • Cystic duct from the gall bladder and Hepatic duct from the liver join to form the Bile duct that opens into the pancreatic duct to form the hepatopancreatic duct.
    3. Left lobe
  • Histologically, the liver is a much-branched tubular gland. In this gland, each secretory unit is a lobule or tubule.
  • Liver receives blood from two sources:
    1. Hepatic artery = supplies oxygenated blood
    2. Hepatic portal vein = which is freshly laden with digested food from the intestine.
  • Hepatic vein drains the blood from the liver into the posterior vena cava.
  • Liver secretes:
    1. Bile salts
      • Sodium taurocholate
      • Sodium glycolate
    2. Bile pigments
      • Bilirubin
      • Biliverdin
  • Bile doesn't contain any digestive enzymes.
  • Functions of the liver:
    1. Emulsification of fat
      • Secretes bile juice which is alkaline in nature. Bile helps in emulsification of fat.
    2. Glycogenesis
      • Stores excess glucose in the form of glycogen by the process of glycogenesis.
      • Converts excess proteins into urea and other nitrogenous wastes.
    3. During the embryonic stage, it produces RBCs.
    4. Stores copper and iron and produces Vitamin A.
    5. Produces prothrombin and fibrinogen, essential for blood coagulation.
    6. Produces heparin that checks the coagulation of the blood within the blood vessels.

2. Pancreas

  • Second-largest gland of the body.
  • It is a mixed gland.
  • Histologically, it is a much-branched grape-like gland, each lobule of which consists of a cluster of secretory cells, acinus, which encloses a very narrow central space.
  • Pancreas is a compact gland.
  • It produces an alkaline watery digestive juice called pancreatic juice which contains 3 special enzymes:
    1. Trypsinogen
    2. Amylopsin or Amylase
    3. Steapsin or Lipase
  • Endocrine function of the pancreas is due to the Islets of Langerhans. They secrete 2 hormones:
    1. Insulin: Causes conversion of the excess glucose into glycogen which is stored in the liver and muscle.
    2. Glucagon: Converts the glycogen into glucose.

3. Frog has Intestinal Spleen.

A. BLOOD VASCULAR SYSTEM

a. Blood

  • Mobile fluid connective tissue of reddish colour.
  • Blood has two parts:
    • Plasma:
      • Colourless fluid.
      • Non-living constituent.
      • Contains 90% water.
    • Blood corpuscles:
      • Are of three types
      • Erythrocytes or RBCs:
        • Oval, Nucleated, Biconvex cells having reddish respiratory pigment haemoglobin.
      • Leucocytes or WBCs:
        • Colourless nucleated cells of five types as in mammals.
        • Some are phagocytic.
        • Others produce antibodies.
      • Thrombocytes:
        • Spindle-shaped nucleated cells.
        • Take part in blood coagulation.

b. Heart

Heart is triangular, thick-walled muscular, reddish pulsatile sac.

  • Heart lies midventrally in thorax in between two lungs.
  • Covered by a fluid-filled double membranous thin bag called pericardium which is protective and allows frictionless heart movements.
  • Heart of frog is 3 chambered. (2 auricles and 1 ventricle)
    • The two auricles are separated by an interauricular septum.
    • The two auricles are clearly separated from ventricle by a coronary sulcus.
    • The two auricles open jointly into ventricle through an auriculoventricular aperture guarded by four flapped auriculoventricular valve.
      • The valve is supported by strong thread-like chordae tendineae against being pushed into auricles.
    • Ventricle has a number of fissures and ridges called columnae corneae.
  • Sinus Venosus
    • On the dorsal side of heart is present a triangular sac named sinus venosus.
    • Sinus venosus receives three vena cava:
      • 2 pre cavals
      • 1 post caval
    • Sinus venosus opens into right auricle by sinu-auricular aperture guarded by two sinu-auricular valves.
    • In frog, blood flows to right auricle from sinus venosus.
  • Truncus arteriosus:
    • Ventral side of heart possesses tubular structure called truncus arteriosus which arises from right anterior corner of ventricle.
    • The truncus arteriosus consists of:
      • A thick-walled proximal portion, the Conus arteriosus/Pylangium:
        • Pylangium possesses a large twisted longitudinal spiral valve, which incompletely divides the cavity of conus arteriosus into:
          • Right-ventral: Cavum aorticum
          • Left-dorsal: Cavum pulmocutaneum
      • A thin-walled distal portion, the Synangium/Ventral aorta/Bulbous arteriosus:
        • Synangium is completely divided by a horizontal partition, the principal septum, into:
          • A dorsal chamber
          • A ventral chamber
        • Synangium is anteriorly divided into:
          • Left aortic trunk
          • Right aortic trunk
        • Each of the aortic trunks further divides into three arches:
          • Carotid artery
          • Systemic artery
          • Pulmocutaneous artery
      • Three semilunar valves occur between ventricle and pylangium and between pylangium and synangium.
  • Working/Action of Heart
    • Cardiac cycle completes in frog in 0.94 sec.
    • Frog's heart beats 64 times/minute.
    • Blood from all parts of the body except lungs is brought by three venae cava into sinus venosus which pours the same into right auricle.
    • Blood from the two lungs is poured into left auricle through pulmonary vein.
    • Contraction of both Auricles:
      • Closure of sino-auricular valve and aperture of pulmonary veins.
      • Opening of auriculoventricular valve.
      • Deoxygenated blood from right auricle and oxygenated blood from left auricle passes into the ventricle.
      • Due to the presence of columnae corneae, pure blood is collected in the fissures on the left side and impure blood in the fissures on the right side, with slight mixing of blood in the middle fissures.
    • Contraction of Ventricle:
      • Opening of
        • Sino-auricular valve (SA)
        • Aperture of pulmonary vein
      • Closure of Auriculo-Ventricular (AV) valve.
      • Opening of Semilunar valve in truncus arteriosus.
      • Blood passes into pylangium, impure and mixed in cavum pulmocutaneum; mixed and pure in cavum aorticum. From here, the blood passes into different arches: impure into pulmocutaneous, mixed into systemic, and nearly pure in carotid arches.
      • According to recent research, the carotid labyrinth is purely a sensory organ and surely helps in detecting pressure changes and the presence of CO2.

c. Blood vessels

Blood vessels are the channels/routes/paths of blood circulation.

i. Arterial System

  • Arteries --> Arterioles -->
  • Arteries carry blood away from the heart to different organs.
  • Carry oxygenated blood except pulmonary arteries.
  • Devoid of valves.
  • Truncus arteriosus:
    • Truncus arteriosus divides into two large right and left branches, each of which divides into three arches:
    • Carotid arch:
      • Each carotid arch gives off the following:
      • The External Carotid Artery/The Lingual Artery:
        • Supplies blood to the tongue and muscles of the hyoid.
      • The Internal Carotid Artery:
        • Divides into three branches:
        • Ophthalmic = Supply to the eye
        • Cerebral = Supply to the brain
        • Palatine = Supply to the roof of the buccopharyngeal cavity
        • Near its origin, the internal carotid bears a swelling called the carotid labyrinth, which detects the partial pressure of oxygen in the blood.
    • Systemic arch:
      • Systemic arch is the largest of all three.
      • Branches from systemic arch before the formation of the dorsal aorta are:
      • Oesophageo - Occipitovertebral artery:
        • Delicate oesophageal branch supplies blood to the oesophagus.
        • Occipitovertebral artery supplies blood to the vertebral column.
      • Subclavian or Brachial artery:
        • Supplies blood to the shoulder and forelimb.
      • At the point where the dorsal aorta is formed by the union of the right and left systemic arches, a large coeliaco-mesenteric artery is given out. It divides into two main branches:
      • Coeliac artery:
        • Gastric artery = Supplies blood to the stomach
        • Hepatic artery = Supplies blood to the liver
      • Anterior mesenteric artery:
        • Duodeno-pancreatic artery = Supplies blood to the duodenum and pancreas
        • Splenic artery = Supplies blood to the intestine and spleen
        • Intestinal artery = Supplies blood to the intestine
      • The dorsal aorta passes backward between and beneath the kidneys along the mid-dorsal line and gives off the following:
        • A pair of gonadal arteries (spermatic in males and ovarian in females)
        • 4-5 pairs of renal arteries to the kidneys
        • A number of small lumbar arteries to the dorsal body wall
        • A posterior mesenteric artery to the rectum
      • The dorsal aorta then divides into two iliac arteries.
      • Each iliac artery gives off the following branches:
        • Epigastric artery = Supplies blood to the muscles of the thigh and the urinary bladder
        • Femoral artery = Supplies blood to the skin and muscles of the anterior part of the thigh
        • Sciatic artery = Supplies blood to the shank and foot
    • Pulmocutaneous arch:
      • Hindermost of all three aortic arches.
      • Divides into the following two arteries:
      • Pulmonary artery = Supplies blood to the lungs
      • Cutaneous artery = Supplies blood to the skin

ii. Capillaries

Fine hair-like blood vessels that connect arteries and veins.

iii. Venous system

  • Veins --> Venules -->
  • Collect and carry blood from different parts of the body to the heart.
  • Provided with a series of semilunar valves.
  • Consists of:
    • Pulmonary veins:
      • Brings oxygenated blood from the lungs.
      • Left and right pulmonary veins unite to form a common pulmonary vein that opens into the left auricle through a single opening.
    • Systemic venous system (2 precavals, a post caval):
      • Consists of 2 precavals (Anterior venae cavae) and 1 post caval (Posterior vena cava)
      • External jugular:
        • Formed by the union of:
        • Lingual = from the tongue and mouth
        • Mandibular = from the lower jaw
      • Innominate:
        • Internal jugular = from the brain, eye, and other structures inside the skull
        • Subscapular = from the back of the arm and shoulder
      • Subclavian:
        • Brachial = from the forelimbs
        • Musculo-cutaneous = from the skin and sides of the body
      • The post caval begins between the kidneys and after passing dorsally to the liver, it ends at the posterior end of the triangular sinus venosus.
      • The component veins or veins draining to the postcaval are:
        • 4-5 pairs of renal veins
        • A pair of gonadal vein (spermatic or ovarian veins)
        • A pair of hepatic veins
    • Portal systems:
      • Veins which are formed by the union of capillaries in an organ, from which it collects blood, break up before reaching the heart into a second set of capillaries in some organ are called portal veins.
      • It is a short circuit for disposing of important biochemicals without delay.
      • Two types of Portal systems in frog:
      • Renal Portal System:
        • A branch from the femoral (outer side of the hind limb) called femoro-renal combines with the sciatic vein (inner side of the hind limb) to form the Renal Portal Vein.
        • It proceeds towards the kidney of its side, receives a dorso-lumbar (from dorsal lateral parts of the body) and the oviduct and then enters the kidney, breaking up into capillaries for quicker extraction of nitrogenous waste products and salts.
        • It is this second set of capillaries in the kidney that is known as the Renal Portal System.
        • The purified blood is collected from the kidneys by renal veins.
      • Hepatic Portal System:
        • The two pelvic veins (branch of the femoral vein) join to form the median anterior abdominal vein.
        • This vessel receives a small vesical vein from the bladder and ends in front by passing into the liver and there breaking up into capillaries again.
        • The hepatic portal vein is formed by the fusion of a number of veins from the digestive tract:
          • Rectal vein
          • Intestinal vein
          • Splenic vein
          • Duodopancreatic vein
          • Gastric vein
          • Oesophageal vein
        • Near the liver, the anterior abdominal vein is joined to the hepatic portal vein by a small branch. These two then enter the liver, break up into capillaries for the disposal of their contents - food materials from the hepatic portal vein and deamination of extra amino acids for the formation of urea.
        • The liver is fed with blood from:
          • Hepatic arteries
          • Anterior abdominal vein
          • Hepatic portal vein
        • Blood is discharged from the liver by the paired hepatic veins into the post caval.

LYMPHATIC SYSTEM

a. It consists of

i. Lymph

  1. 1) Lymph is actually filtered out colourless blood or tissue fluid which is collected by lymph capillaries.

    1. a) The lymph differs from blood in that it is colourless and has no respiratory pigments.
  2. 2) It has fewer leucocytes.
  3. 3) Erythrocytes and Thrombocytes are absent.
  4. 4) Lymph acts as a middleman between blood and tissues.
  5. 5) Lymph collects in lymph sinuses.
    1. There are about 20 subcutaneous lymph sinuses below the skin.

ii. Lymph vessels

  1. 1) Lymph capillaries join to form lymph vessels. Valves are present to prevent backflow.
  2. 2) Lymph sinuses and lymph nodes are found at places connected with lymph vessels.

iii. Lymph nodes

iv. Lymph Hearts

  1. 1) Ultimately, lymph is passed into veins by two pairs of lymph hearts
    1. a) Anterior into Subclavian veins
    2. b) Posterior into Femoral vein
  2. 2) Note: Absence of lymph heart in Mammals.

Urogenital System

Sexual Dimorphism and Reproductive Structures

  1. Sexual Dimorphism

    Exhibited by the presence of vocal sacs and amplexusary pads in males.

  2. Reproductive Structures

    1. Oviducts and Ovaries

      Each oviduct is dilated to form ovisac before opening into cloaca. Ovaries are attached to the dorsal abdominal wall by the mesovarium. Theca externa surrounds the ovary, while Theca interna forms the stroma. Follicle cells surround, protect, and nurse the oocytes in the ovary.

    2. Testes

      Tunica albuginea surrounds testes, similar to the rabbit. The mesorchium, a peritoneal fold, connects testes and kidneys. Cells of Sertoli (Nurse cells) support developing sperm, and Cells of Leydig are found in the testes' connective tissue. Vasa efferentia arise from the testes and join Bidder's canal in the kidney before opening into the urinogenital duct.

Reproductive Behavior and Embryonic Development

  1. Reproductive Behavior

    Actual copulation is absent; amplexus is used for pseudo-copulation.

  2. Embryonic Development and Fertilization

    1. Egg Structure

      Yolk is present at the vegetal pole (telolecithal) and remains downwards in water.

    2. Fertilization and Cleavage

      The animal pole later forms the head end. Cleavage is holoblastic.

    3. Gastrulation

      Processes involved: Involution, Epiboly, and Invagination. Yolk plug is formed during gastrulation. Archentereon is the cavity appearing during embryogenesis.

  3. Development and Hormonal Regulation

    1. Development and Larval Stage

      Development is indirect with tadpole larva. Tadpole larva's skeleton is entirely cartilaginous, and it is herbivorous.

    2. Hormonal Regulation and Metamorphosis

      Thyroxine hormone from the thyroid gland regulates complete metamorphosis. Metamorphosis doesn't change the nervous system.

    3. Sex Hormones

      Frog has only estrogen hormone; no progesterone hormone. Sex hormones are steroids.

Nervous System

The nervous system includes:

A. CENTRAL NERVOUS SYSTEM (CNS)

1. Forebrain

  • 1. Olfactory lobe
  • 2. Cerebrum (Lateral ventricles)
    • Foramen of Monro
    • 3. Diencephalon (3rd ventricle/ Diocoel)

2. Midbrain

  • 1. Optic lobes (Optocoel)
  • 2. Crus cerebri

3. Hindbrain

  • 1. Cerebellum
    • Iter/Aqueduct of Sylvius
  • 2. Medulla oblongata (4th ventricle/ Metacoel)

CNS comprises the Brain and Spinal cord.

a. Brain

  • Lodged in the Cranial cavity of the skull.
  • Brain has 2 protective coverings:
    • Outer thick Duramater.
      • The space between these two membranes or meninges called Sub-dural space is filled with a kind of shock-absorbing fluid called Cerebro-spinal fluid.
    • Inner thin Piamater
  • Over optic lobes and Medulla oblongata, white matter is external, while in other parts grey matter is external.
  • Parts of the Brain of Frog:
    • i. Forebrain (Prosencephalon)
      • 1) Olfactory lobes (Rhinencephalon)
        • a) There are two anterior swollen lobes.
        • b) It has a cavity called Rhinocoel/Olfactory ventricle.
          • i) Rhicocoel communicates posteriorly with lateral ventricles.
        • c) Function: Control sense of smell.
      • 2) Cerebral hemisphere/Cerebrum (Telencephalon)
        • a) These are a pair of large oval lobes behind the olfactory lobes and fused with each other to form the cerebrum.
        • b) It has a cavity known as lateral Ventricles/Paracoel (first and second ventricles).
        • c) Two lateral ventricles open jointly through a Foramen of Monro into Diocoel or 3rd Ventricle of Diencephalon.
        • d) On the ventral side of each lateral ventricle, a reticular structure called corpus striatum is present, which is joined by a transverse strip of connective tissue called anterior commisure.
        • e) The corpus striatum is made of medullated nerve fibres and brown nerve cells.
      • 3) Diencephalon (Thalamencephalon)
        • a) It is a rhomboidal structure.
        • b) It has a cavity called Diocoel or Third ventricle.
        • c) Roof of diencephalon has anterior choroid plexus which is the thickened folded and highly vascular piamater.
        • d) Ventrally diencephalon possesses an optic chiasma and pituitary body made of infundibulum and hypophysis.
        • e) The lateral walls of diencephalon are thick and are called optic thalami.
        • f) Remains of pineal stalk in the adult are called the Brown spot.
        • g) Pineal body is regarded as a Vestige of the third eye.
        • h) Function of Diencephalon:
          • i) Metabolism of carbohydrate, proteins, fats.
    • ii. Midbrain (Mesencephalon)
      • 1) Optic lobes
        • a) These are 2 rounded hollow structures so-called corpora bigemina.
        • b) Each optic lobe has a cavity called Optocoel.
        • c) The two optocoels are connected to the 4th ventricle by Iter/Aqueduct of Sylvius. It is the space between Diocoel and Metacoel.
        • d) Optic lobes control sight.
      • 2) Crura cerebri
        • a) The floor of the midbrain is formed by two longitudinal ridges of nervous tissue called Crura cerebri.
        • b) Crura cerebri connects the cerebral hemispheres with the medulla oblongata.
    • iii. Hindbrain (Rhombencephalon)
      • 1) Cerebellum (Metencephalon)
        • a) It is the form of transverse ridges present dorsally behind the optic lobes.
        • b) It is not well developed in frogs.
        • c) Its main function is to bring about proper and efficient coordination of muscular action to keep the body well balanced.
      • 2) Medulla oblongata (Myelencephalon)
        • a) It is the posterior most conical part of the brain.
        • b) Its roof possesses posterior choroid plexus.
        • c) Its cavity is called Metacoel or 4th ventricle.
          • i) Metacoel is in communication with Iter anteriorly and Central Canal posteriorly.
          • ii) In frogs, Metacoel doesn't communicate with the subdural cord.
        • d) The medulla oblongata continues posteriorly as the spinal cord.
        • e) Function: Controls involuntary activities and reflex actions.

b. Spinal cord

  • i. It is the posterior continuation of the brain.
  • ii. It is a sub-cylindrical and somewhat flattened structure lying in the neural canal of the vertebral column.
  • iii. It tapers posteriorly in the Urostyle (10th vertebra) as Filum terminale or Cauda equina.
  • iv. The spinal cord has a dorsal fissure, a deeper ventral fissure, a central canal surrounded by gray matter, and then white matter on the outside.
    • Gray matter extends on the four sides to form dorsal and ventral horns which lead into dorsal and ventral roots of spinal nerves.
  • v. The spinal cord is the center for reflex actions and also serves as the pathway for stimuli passing to and from the brain.

B. PERIPHERAL NERVOUS SYSTEM (PNS)

Nerves from the CNS constitute the PNS.

a. Cranial nerves

  • i. These are nerves arising from the brain.
  • ii. The brain of the frog sends out 10 pairs of cranial nerves.

Note: The last two, i.e., Accessory and Hypoglossal, are absent in frogs.

b. Spinal nerves

  • i. These are nerves arising from the spinal cord.
  • ii. They are mixed nerves that arise from intervertebral foramina by two roots, Dorsal roots and Ventral root.
  • iii. Number of Spinal nerves in Frogs = 10 pairs (since there are 10 vertebrae in frogs)

    • Special point: Spinal nerves, 9 pairs in Rana tigrina.

C. AUTONOMOUS NERVOUS SYSTEM (ANS)

The ANS is a system of nerve fibers and ganglia that controls involuntary activities of various body organs and muscles.

The ANS has two parts:

a. Sympathetic Nervous System:

  • i. Consists of two ganglionated cords which develop on either side of the spinal cord.
  • ii. The number of ganglia in each chain is 9 or 10.
  • iii. Each chain enters the skull through the 10th/Vagus nerve and ends at Gaserian ganglia of the trigeminal nerve.
  • iv. Nerve fibers from sympathetic cords pass into all organs. They stimulate their working by releasing a chemical called Sympathin.

b. Parasympathetic Nervous System:

  • i. Consists of small ganglia present over the walls of organs which are connected to some cranial and spinal nerves.
  • ii. Regulate the functioning of organs through the release of acetylcholine.
  • iii. Usually, they have a moderating effect.
  • iv. The Sympathetic and Parasympathetic autonomic systems have opposite functions.

Respiratory System

The mode of respiration:

  • Amphibious (i.e., aquatic) and aerial because frogs live both on land and in water.

Frogs can perform respiration through three methods:

1. Bucco-pharyngeal respiration

  • i. Exchange of gases takes place in the lining layer of the buccopharyngeal cavity.
  • ii. Mouth, glottis, and gullet are kept closed. Nares are open.
  • iii. Bucco-pharyngeal respiration is performed when the oxygen requirement is low, during rest over land, and while floating in water.
  • iv. It accounts for about 5% of the total intake of oxygen.

2. Cutaneous respiration

  • i. Respiratory substrate is the skin.
  • ii. The skin of frogs is thin and richly supplied with blood capillaries. It is kept moist by mucus and water.
  • iii. Oxygen from the air diffuses into the blood through the moisture over the skin.
    • 1) Dermis is highly vascular and is the site for the exchange of gases, i.e., Dermis acts as alveoli of mammals.
  • iv. Cutaneous respiration occurs in water, during hibernation, and aestivation.
  • v. It accounts for about 30% of the total intake of oxygen.
  • vi. Occurs all the time.
  • vii. Waxing of the skin leads to Asphyxia and finally to death.

3. Pulmonary respiration

  • i. By lungs, which are two in number.
  • ii. Occurs when the oxygen demand is high or the frog is more active, as during swimming in water or during leaping and jumping.
  • iii. It accounts for about 65% of the total intake of oxygen.
  • iv. Organs involved are:
    • 1) External nares
      • a) External nares of frogs have valves. (Membranous valve is present in the nostril i.e., external nares).
    • 2) Olfactory chambers
    • 3) Internal nares
    • 4) Buccopharyngeal cavity
      • a) Glottis opens and air passes into the lungs through the laryngotracheal chamber.
    • 5) Laryngotracheal chamber:
      • a) It is the voice box (sound-producing organ) of frogs.
      • b) It possesses two vocal cords, an opening of the glottis into the pharynx, and bronchus into the lungs. Also, in males, a pair of vocal sacs are present which act as a resonator.
      • c) It is provided with 3 cartilages:
        • i) A ring-like unpaired cricoid cartilage.
        • ii) A pair of arytenoid cartilage
        • Unpaired cartilage Paired cartilage
        • Epiglottis cartilage Arytenoid cartilage
        • Thyroid cartilage Corniculate cartilage
        • Cricoid cartilage Cuneiform cartilage
        • Note: Absence of Thyroid cartilage but Presence of Thyroid gland.
    • 6) Lungs
      • a) A pair of lungs which are highly vascular, pinkish, without lobes and found suspended freely in the peritoneal cavity.
      • b) Each lung has
        • i) Outer: Single peritoneal wall
        • ii) Middle: A layer of unstripped muscle and connective tissue.
        • iii) Inner: Internally, the surface of the lung is divided by a series of irregular septa into a large number of small air sacs called Alveoli.
      • c) Blood capillaries overlie the epithelial lining of the alveoli.
      • d) Exchange of gases occurs between air and blood capillaries.

4. Branchial respiration

  • i. Respiration that occurs through the gills of Tadpole is called Branchial respiration.
  • Tadpole:
    • 1. Aquatic
    • 2. Gill breathers; so can't survive outside the water
      • i. External gills: 3 pairs
      • ii. Internal gills/ Gill clefts: 4 pairs
      • iii. Gill pouch: 5 pairs

Respiratory center is located in the Medulla oblongata.

Ventilation process

1. Done by 2 sets of muscles:

  • i. Sternohyoid or Sternohyal muscles:
    • 1) Run from the hyoid to the coracoids and clavicles of the pectoral girdle.
    • 2) Its contraction lowers the floor of the buccopharyngeal cavity resulting in inspiration.
  • ii. Petro-hyoid or Petrohyal muscles:
    • 1) Run from the hyoid to near pro-otics of the skull.
    • 2) Its contraction raises the floor of the buccopharyngeal cavity resulting in expiration.

2. Both sternohyal and petrohyal muscles contract twice for one complete pulmonary respiration.

3. Sternohyal and petrohyal muscles contract once for one complete buccopharyngeal respiration.

4. Mentameckelian bone is related to the mechanism of respiration.

Physiology of Respiration

  • In vertebrates, the respiration process occurs in the following three steps:
    • i. Breathing or external respiration
    • ii. Gaseous transport
    • iii. Internal respiration or tissue respiration or cellular respiration also called Biological oxidation.
