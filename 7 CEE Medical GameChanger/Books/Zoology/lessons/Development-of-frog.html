Embryology and Development

  • After the 32-cell stage.

2. Introduction

  • The study of development of an organism is called Embryology or Developmental biology.
  • Embryology deals with developmental changes from zygote to death.
  • The entire process of development is called embryogenesis or ontogenesis.
  • Aristotle is regarded as the founder of the science of embryology.
  • The father of Modern Embryology is Karl Ernst Von Baer.
  • The development of a new individual from an asexual reproductive body is called blastogenesis.
  • The development of a new individual from the fertilized egg or zygote is called embryogenesis.
  • Formation of the complete body from the zygote is development.
  • Development includes three basic processes:
    • i. Proliferation of cells by cell growth and division.
    • ii. Differentiation of the cells into various types according to division of labour by gene suppression.
    • iii. Arrangement of differentiated cells into tissue and tissues into organs.
  • Paedogamy is the sexual reproduction of animals in larval condition.
  • Gametes are mature reproductive cells. The gametes of two sexes fuse giving rise to zygote. The gametes are produced in gonads by gametogenesis.
  • The study of abnormal embryonic development is termed Teratology.
  • Oviparous: These are egg-laying animals in which most of the embryonic development of fertilized egg occurs outside the body of the mother, e.g., Insects, fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, etc.
  • Ovoviviparous: These are egg-laying animals in which the egg develops inside the maternal body but is not supplied with the maternal nourishment.
  • Viviparous: These are the animals in which the ova become implanted in the uterine wall after fertilization and develop there until birth. E.g., Eutherian mammals.
    • Viviparity is also found in some elasmobranch fishes and reptiles.
  • Parthenogenesis (virgin birth or virginal reproduction): Development of an egg into a complete individual without fertilization by a sperm or parthenogenesis.
    • Lacerta saxicola of Russia show natural parthenogenesis.

3. General Phases of Development

  • 3.1. Eggs
    • Introduction
      • i. Chalazae are the suspensory ligaments of the yolk in bird's egg.
    • Classification of Eggs
      • i. Based on the amount of yolk
        • 1) Microlecithal (Alecithal, Oligolecithal or Meiolecithal)
          • a) Eggs containing a small amount of yolk or without any yolk.
          • b) E.g., Starfish, Amphioxus, Eutherian mammals including rabbit and human beings.
          • c) Q. Human egg is
            • i) Microlecithal
            • ii) Macrolecithal
            • iii) Mesolecithal
            • iv) Alecithal
              • One. Ans: d. Alecithal
        • 2) Mesolecithal (Medialecithal)
          • a) Eggs containing moderate amount of yolk.
          • b) E.g. Amphibians (frog)
        • 3) Macrolecithal (Megalecithal or Polylecithal)
          • a) Eggs containing a large amount of yolk.
          • b) E.g., Arthropods, fishes, reptiles, birds, and prototherian mammals (monotremata).
      • ii. Based on the distribution of yolk
        • 1) Isolecithal (Homolecithal)
          • a) Yolk is uniformly distributed in the cytoplasm as in microlecithal eggs.
          • b) E.g., protochordates and Echinoderms
        • 2) Telolecithal
          • a) Yolk is concentrated towards the vegetal pole, the nucleus, and major part of cytoplasm is displaced to the animal pole as in mesolecithal and macrolecithal eggs of vertebrates.
          • b) E.g., Frog, Reptiles, Aves, Fishes.
        • 3) Centrolecithal
          • a) Yolk is concentrated in the center of the ovum with cytoplasm surrounding it as in macrolecithal eggs of arthropods.
        • 4) Heterolecithal
          • a) Eggs with unevenly distributed yolk.
        • 5) Discoid or Meiolecithal
          • a) Almost the whole egg is occupied by the yolk except a small disc or active ooplasm.
          • b) E.g., birds and reptiles.
      • iii. Based on the presence or absence of Shell
        • 1) Acleidoic Eggs
          • a) Eggs without a shell.
        • 2) Cleidoic eggs
          • a) Cleidoic eggs are the eggs of reptiles and birds that are insulated from the environment by
            • i) Albumen
            • ii) Membranes, and
            • iii) Shell
              • One. At least 94% of the shell is made of calcium carbonate.
    • Egg membranes
      • i. Types of Egg membranes (There are three types of egg membranes)
        • 1) Primary egg membrane
          • a) Primary egg membrane is formed around the plasma membrane of egg secreted by ovum itself.
          • b) Types of Primary Egg membranes
            • i) Jelly envelope – Echinoderm
            • ii) Vitelline membrane – Molluscs, amphibians, and birds
            • iii) Zona radiata – Shark, some amphibians, and reptiles
            • iv) Zona pellucida - Mammals
        • 2) Secondary egg membrane
          • a) Secondary egg membrane is formed around an egg by the follicle cells of the ovary.
          • b) The chorion found around an egg of an insect is an example of a secondary egg membrane.
        • 3) Tertiary egg membrane
          • a) Tertiary egg membranes are formed by the oviducts or other parts of the maternal genital tract while the egg is passing from the ovary to the exterior.
          • b) Three concentric layers of albuminous jelly around the egg of a frog secreted by the oviduct are tertiary egg membranes.
          • c) The albumen, shell membranes, and calcareous shell of reptiles and birds are the best examples of tertiary membranes.
  • 2. The Sperms
    • a. The sperms are microscopic, thread-like and motile cells but amoeboid in Ascaris.
    • b. A sperm has a head, neck, middle piece, and tail.
      • i. Head is the enlarged end of a sperm, containing the large nucleus capped by the acrosome.
      • ii. Middle piece is the mitochondrial portion.
      • iii. In between the head and middle piece, there is a small somewhat constricted neck part containing the two centrioles.
      • iv. Tail is long consists of main piece (mostly) and end piece.
      • v. The axial filament in the main piece is surrounded by a thin layer of cytoplasm whereas it is naked in the end piece.
  • 3. Fertilization
    • a. Fertilization is a complex physio-chemical process which involves the fusion of a male gamete and a female gamete.
    • b. There are two types of fertilization.
      • i. External fertilization occurs in oviparous animals which lay eggs in water. E.g., Frog.
      • ii. Internal fertilization occurs in ovoviviparous or viviparous animals. E.g., Echidna, rabbit, and man.
    • c. Fertilizin-antifertilizin reaction
      • i. Fertilizins are the substances emitted from mature eggs.
        • 1) Fertilizins are present on the surface of the egg.
        • 2) Chemically, fertilizin is a glycoprotein or mucopolysaccharide.
      • ii. Antilfertilizin is present on the surface layer of the cytoplasm of spermatozoa.
        • 1) Chemically, antifertilizin is an acidic protein molecule.
      • iii. During fertilization, the fertilizin and antifertilizin combine in a specific way causing 'fertilizin-antifertilizin reaction'.
    • d. Place of fertilization
      • i. Fertilization in birds generally takes place when the egg is in the oviduct.
      • ii. In mammals (rabbit and man), fertilization of the ovum occurs in the fallopian tube.
    • e. The egg of insects has a protective wall chorion. The spermatozoa enter the egg through the micropyles.
    • f. Sperm penetrates through the egg wall in most of the organisms by the help of sperm lysins formed from the acrosome.
    • g. When the acrosome of the spermatozoa touches the surface of the egg, the cytoplasm of the egg bulges forward forming receptive cone or fertilization cone.
    • h. Immediately after fertilization, a new membrane namely fertilization membrane develops outside the vitelline membrane.
    • i. The entrance of late arriving sperms in the ovum is blocked by the fertilization membrane.
      • i. Fertilization membrane prevents polyspermic fertilization by preventing further entry of spermatozoa.
    • j. In vertebrates (man, rabbit, and frog), the egg completes its first reduction division in the ovary and reaches the metaphase stage of the second meiotic division (Metaphase -II). The second oocyte is formed before fertilization.
    • k. The second division is completed and the second polar body is extruded only if the egg is fertilized by a spermatozoon.
    • l. The fusion of male and female pronuclei is called amphimixis (syngamy).
      • i. Male and female pronuclei are haploid. Amphimixis results in the formation of a diploid zygote nucleus.
    • m. In all animals, the fertilized ovum develops in the sequence:
      • i. Zygote
      • ii. Cleavage
      • iii. Morula
      • iv. Blastula
      • v. Gastrula and
      • vi. Neurula
  • 4. Cleavage
    • a. Development of a fertilized egg starts with cleavage.
    • b. Cleavage is a process of rapid mitotic divisions of the zygote.
    • c. Cleavage differs from ordinary mitosis in the following respects.
      • i. Cleavage occurs in all cells whereas mitosis doesn't.
      • ii. Interphase is short and does not involve growth.
      • iii. Divisions are very rapid with rapid DNA replication. Nuclear-cytoplasmic ratio increases with each division.
      • iv. There is a decrease in the size of blastomeres; size and shape of the embryo do not change.
      • v. High consumption of oxygen. The metabolism becomes fast.
    • d. Patterns of Cleavages
      • i. Radial
        • 1) All blastomeres are placed in a radially symmetrical manner around the animal-vegetal axis.
        • 2) E.g., Sponges, Coelenterates, and some echinoderms.
        • 3) Biradial cleavage
      • ii. Spiral
        • 1) Blastomeres are arranged in a spiral manner.
        • 2) E.g., Platyhelminthes, Annelids, and most mollusks.
      • iii. Bilateral cleavage:
        • 1) Blastomeres are arranged so that right and left sides become apparent.
        • 2) E.g., Amphibians and higher mammals.
    • e. Types of Cleavage
      • i. Types of Cleavage are influenced by the amount and distribution of yolk.
      • ii. Following basic types of cleavages are found in animals.
        • 1) Total or Holoblastic cleavage:
          • a) Cleavage furrow bisects the entire egg. Two types of Holoblastic cleavages are:
          • b) Equal holoblastic cleavage:
          • i) Occurs in microlecithal or isolecithal types of eggs. This produces blastomeres of equal or approximately equal size.
          • ii) E.g., Starfish, Amphioxus, marsupials and placental mammals like rabbit and man.
          • c) Unequal holoblastic cleavage:
          • i) Occurs in mesolecithal or telolecithal type of eggs. This produces unequal-sized blastomeres called micromeres and macromeres.
          • ii) E.g., Amphibians (frog)
        • 2) Meroblastic cleavage:
          • a) This is also called partial or incomplete cleavage, occurs in macrolecithal (polylecithal) eggs which are either telolecithal or centrolecithal. Two types of meroblastic cleavages are:
          • b) Discoidal cleavage:
          • i) Found in macrolecithal and highly telolecithal eggs of reptiles, birds, and monotreme mammals.
          • ii) This type of cleavage also occurs in some invertebrates like squids and in the majority of fishes.
          • c) Superficial cleavage:
          • i) Found in macrolecithal and centrolecithal eggs of most arthropods.
      • iii. Planes of cleavage
        • 1) Meridional cleavage
        • 2) Vertical cleavage
        • 3) Equatorial cleavage and
        • 4) Transverse cleavage

    • i. Morula

    1. A solid ball of cells showing resemblance with a mulberry is formed by the cleavage of a zygote. This is known as 'morula'.
    2. A morula can be differentiated from the blastula by the absence of a cavity.

    ii. Blastula/ Blastocyst

    1. Cleavage of zygote results in the formation of blastula from morula.
    2. A blastula is made up of the cells called blastomeres.
    3. Four types of blastula in animals are:
      1. Coeloblastula:
        1. Blastula with Central cavity
        2. In Amphioxus and Frog.
      2. Stereoblastula/ Solid blastula:
        1. In which the embryo produced by spiral cleavage characterized by the absence of a blastocoel and is found in all mollusks except cephalopods. As the blastocoel is absent it is called a Solid blastula.
        2. In Cnidarian, Annelida, and some Mollusca.
      3. Discoblastula:
        1. Disc-shaped blastula
        2. In Fishes, Reptiles, Birds, and Egg-laying mammals.
      4. Superficial blastula:
        1. Absence of blastocoel.
        2. E.g., Insects.
    4. Trophoderm in embryonic development of mammals is formed at the stage of blastocyst.

    5. Gastrula

    1. Gastrula is formed by a process called gastrulation.
    2. Gastrulation is the process of embryonic development during which cell movements establish the three primary germinal layers namely:
      1. Ectoderm
      2. Mesoderm
      3. Endoderm
    3. During gastrulation, the cells move to occupy their definite position.
    4. Gastrulation is a process of migration and rearrangement of cells already present in the blastula.
    5. New cavity formed during gastrulation is archenteron (gastrocoel) opened outside through blastopore.
      1. Blastopore is absent in amniotes.
      2. In Protostomia (including Annelida, Mollusca, and Arthropoda) blastopore becomes subdivided into two openings, one of which becomes the mouth and the other the anus.
      3. In Deuterostomia (including Echinodermata and Chordata – frog, rabbit, and man) blastopore develops into the future anus.
    6. Archenteron is lined with endodermal cells.
    7. The movement of cells during gastrulation is called formative or morphogenetic movements.
      1. Such movements include epiboly and emboly.
      2. Epiboly is the growth of one part over another like prospective ectoderm over the rest except blastopore.
      3. Emboly includes morphogenetic movements like migration of ectoderm, mesoderm, and notochord cells from the surface to the interior.
        1. Emboly may occur by
          1. Involution – rolling of cells into the interior.
          2. Invagination – infolding.
          3. Ingression – new cells migrating into blastocoel.
          4. Delamination – formation of the second layer by tangential division of surface cells.
    8. Termination of gastrulation is indicated by the obliteration of blastocoel.

    Development and Reproduction in Frogs

    1. Breeding

    • a. Frog breeds in the rainy season, June to September.
    • b. Frog breeds in water like other amphibians.
    • c. Male frogs produce croaking sounds (mating call) with their vocal sacs.
    • d. The sexual embrace of male and female frogs is called amplexus (false copulation).
    • e. The purpose of amplexus is to ensure simultaneous discharge of male and female gametes at the same place in water.

    2. Ovulation

    • a. Ovulation is the release of eggs from the ovary into the body cavity.
    • b. Eggs in the stage of secondary oocytes are released into the body cavity by ovary rupture during ovulation.

    3. Spawning

    • a. Spawning is the act of laying eggs by the female frog, stimulated by the male during amplexus.
    • b. Spawn is a cluster or mass of eggs laid by a female.
    • c. Each egg is surrounded by a thin vitelline membrane and three layers of jelly coats made of gelatin.
      • i. Gelatin protects the egg from predators and acts as an insulator, keeping the egg warm.

    4. Structure of Egg

    • a. Frog’s egg is mesolecithal (based on yolk amount) and telolecithal (based on yolk distribution).
    • b. Upper black or darkly pigmented part is the animal hemisphere. Lower unpigmented or white part is the vegetal hemisphere.
    • c. Cytoplasm is concentrated in the animal pole.
    • d. Vegetal hemisphere of frog’s egg contains yolk, remaining directed downwards.
    • e. Correct sequence in frog's development:
      • i. Fertilization
      • ii. Cleavage
      • iii. Morula
      • iv. Blastula
      • v. Gastrula

    5. Fertilization

    • a. Fertilization in frog is external and takes place in water.
    • b. Sperms are released on the egg mass before it reaches water.
    • c. Sperm entry triggers the second meiotic division in the egg.
    • d. A sperm enters the egg in the animal hemisphere.
    • e. A gray crescent appears opposite to the sperm entrance, marking the future embryo's dorsal side.
    • f. Sperm entry point marks the embryo's anterior side, establishing bilateral organization.

    6. Cleavage

    • a. Cleavage is the early mitotic cell divisions of the zygote until the blastula stage.
    • b. Frog has regulative development with indeterminate cleavage.
    • c. Stages of Cleavage:
      • i. 1st Cleavage:
        • 1) First cleavage is meridional, holoblastic, and equal.
        • 2) First cleavage results in two blastomeres.
      • ii. 2nd Cleavage:
        • 1) Second cleavage is holoblastic and equal.
        • 2) 4 cells are formed after the 2nd cleavage.
      • iii. 3rd Cleavage:
        • 1) Third cleavage is holoblastic and unequal.
        • 2) Third cleavage results in embryo with 8 blastomeres.
      • iv. 4th Cleavage:
        • 1) Fourth cleavage involves two synchronous meridional divisions resulting in 16 blastomeres.
      • v. 5th Cleavage:
        • 1) Fifth cleavage involves two simultaneous latitudinal divisions resulting in 32 blastomeres.
      • vi. 6th Cleavage:
        • 1) The division after the 5th cleavage becomes irregular and asynchronous.
      • vii. 7th Cleavage:
        • 1) After the 6th or 7th cleavage, the embryo looks like a morula stage.
      • viii. Morula:
        • 1) Morula is a solid ball of cells.
      • ix. Blastula:
        • 1) A cavity called blastocoel appears towards the animal hemisphere. This blastula of frog is called Coeloblastula.

    7. Gastrula

    • a. Gastrulation converts a blastula into a gastrula with three layers:
      • i. Ectoderm
      • ii. Mesoderm
      • iii. Endoderm enclosing an archenteron.
    • b. Gastrulation includes epiboly of ectoderm, invagination of endoderm, and involution of chordamesoderm.
    • c. Gastrulation results in archenteron and blastopore formation.

    8. Neurula

    • a. Neurulation forms a neural tube, notochord, and gut.
    • b. Notochord formation is known as notogenesis.

    9. Metamorphosis of Tadpole to Adult Frog

    • a. Thyroxin hormone from thyroid gland triggers tadpole metamorphosis.
    • b. Metamorphosis is delayed by food abundance and favored by food deficiency.
    • c. Changes during metamorphosis:
      • i. Tadpole stops feeding and relies on tail storage.
      • ii. Tail becomes shorter.
      • iii. Larval epidermis and jaws are lost.
      • iv. Skin glands develop.
      • v. Lungs become functional and gill clefts close.
      • vi. Blood vascular system modifies.
      • vii. True jaws form.
      • viii. Eyes move to the top of the head.
      • ix. Tongue enlarges, intestine shortens, and stomach and liver enlarge.
      • x. Forelimbs develop, young frog leaves water.
      • xi. Frog feeds, grows rapidly, and loses the small tail.
