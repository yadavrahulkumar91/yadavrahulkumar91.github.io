Introduction to Chordata

Chordata Definition

  1. Chordata refers to the presence of a stiff, supporting rod-like structure along the back called Notochord.
  2. Chordata characteristics:
    1. Bilaterally symmetrical
    2. Metamerically segmented
    3. Deuterostomate
    4. Four primary Chordate characteristics:
      1. Notochord
      2. Dorsal tubular nerve cord
      3. Pharyngeal gill slits/pouches
      4. Post-anal tail
    5. Presence of Endostyle or thyroid gland:
      1. An endostyle is a longitudinal ciliated groove on the ventral wall of the pharynx producing mucus for food gathering.
      2. Found in:
        • Urochordates
        • Cephalochordates
        • Larvae of lampreys
      3. Aids in transporting food to the esophagus
      4. Also called Hypopharyngeal groove
    6. Complete digestive tract
    7. Ventral, contractile blood vessel (heart)
    8. Presence of true coelom:
      • Schizocoel in vertebrates
      • Enterocoel in protochordates

ACRANIATA / Protochordata (Invertebrate Chordata / Non-vertebrate Chordates)

Protochordates Overview

  • Protochordates are the primitive chordates.
  • Vertebrae and paired appendages are absent.
  • All protochordates are marine.

Sub-phylum: Urochordata / Sea Squirt / Tunicates

  • Notochord present only in the tail region of larva.
  • Exclusively marine, solitary or colonial, fixed or pelagic.
  • The body wall of tunicates is a connective tissue-like covering called the tunic.
  • Calcareous spicules are found.
  • Retrogressive metamorphosis results in degeneration in adults (e.g., Ascidia - Sea Squirt).
  • Unusual character is that their blood travels in both directions.
  • Examples include:
    • Ascidia (Sea Squirt)
    • Pyrosoma
    • Herdmania (Sea Potato)
    • Ciona
    • Molgula
    • Salpa
    • Doliolum
    • Botryllus

Sub-phylum: Cephalochordata / Lancelets

  • All four characteristic features of chordates are present throughout life.
  • The notochord extends from the tail to the head, giving them their name.
  • Body small (up to 5 cm), elongate, laterally flattened, nearly transparent, tadpole-like animals.
  • They are filter feeders for digestion.
  • Circulation involves contractile waves in the walls of major vessels.
  • Excretion occurs through protonephridia with solenocytes.
  • Reproduction is dioecious, with numerous and metamerically repeated gonads.
  • Gonoducts are absent, and no asexual reproduction occurs.
  • Development is indirect, including a free-swimming larva.
  • Wheel organ is present in lancelets.
  • Examples:
    • Branchiostoma (Amphioxus) / Lancelet
    • Asymmetron

CRANIATA / Euchordata

Sub-Phylum: Vertebrata

  • Vertebrates are the advanced chordates.
  • Vertebrates have a well-developed cranium and vertebral column.
  • Notochord is embryonic, in the adult replaced by the vertebral column.
  • Well-developed head (Cephalization) with special sense organs (smell, sight, and hearing).

Division: Agnatha

  • Agnatha are primitive vertebrates.
  • Endoskeleton is fibrous and cartilaginous.
  • Notochord persists throughout life.
  • Without jaws and paired appendages.
  • Internal ear with one or two semicircular canals.

Class: Ostracodermi

  • Ostracodermi are extinct, first primitive vertebrates.
  • They had a sluggish body covered by bony armor.
  • Bottom dwellers, filter feeders.
  • Examples: Cephalaspis, Drepanaspis

Class: Cyclostomata

  • Mouth ventral, suctorial, and circular.
  • Body: elongated, eel-shaped, without scales, jaws, and lateral fins.
  • Cranium and vertebral column are cartilaginous.
  • Gill slits: 6-15 pairs, pouch-like.
  • Circulation closed type.
  • Ectoparasites on some fishes and scavengers.
  • Migration: Marine but migrate for spawning to freshwater.
  • Example: Petromyzon (Lamprey), Myxine (Hagfish)

Division: Gnathostomata

  • Features of Gnathostomata:
  • No. of Cranial nerves: 10 pairs in Frog, 9-12 pairs in other groups.
  • No. of Spinal nerves: Varies among different groups.

Aves

1. Introduction

  • Ornithology = Study of birds
  • Otology = Study of bird's eggs
  • Phenology = Study of bird migration
  • Birds originated towards the end of Jurassic period of Mesozoic era from aerial reptiles called 'Pterosaurs'.
  • Archaeopteryx was a primitive bird.
    • Archaeopteryx was a connecting link between reptiles and birds.
    • Archaeopteryx had wings, feathers, and a bird-like head, but each wing had three clawed digits and the beak was toothed.
    • Archaeopteryx became extinct during the Cretaceous period.
    • Fossil of Archaeopteryx was obtained from Germany.

2. General Characters

  • Birds are feathered bipeds, air-breathing, truly flying vertebrates (other being bats from Class Mammalia).
  • Body is more or less spindle-shaped and divisible into four distinct regions:
    • Head
      • Jaw bones prolonged into toothless beak or bill.
    • Neck
    • Trunk and
      • Limbs are two pairs. Forelimbs are modified as wings for flying. Hindlimbs or legs are large, each foot usually bears four clawed toes.
    • Tail
  • Skin without glands, only cutaneous gland is Uropygial gland or Preen gland at tail base.

3. Systems (Continued)

b. Digestive System

  • Oesophagus
    • Crop
      • Oesophagus is dilated into a crop for quick feeding and storage.
      • Crop secretes 'pigeon milk' during the breeding season.
      • Pigeon milk is secreted by both males and females.
  • Stomach
    • Stomach is divided into a
      • Glandular Proventriculus and
      • A muscular Gizzard

c. Circulation

  • Heart is completely four-chambered (also in mammals).
  • Sinus venosus is absent.
  • Only the right aortic (systemic) arch persists in the adult.
  • Renal portal system is vestigial.

4. Reproduction

  • Sexes are separate.
  • Some birds show sexual dimorphism (e.g., Parrot and Peacock).
  • Females are oviparous with a single (left) functional ovary and oviduct (Mullerian duct).
  • Different parts of the oviduct secrete the following:
    • Ostium = Dense albumin
    • Magnum = White albumin
    • Uterus = Calcareous shell

5. Nervous System

  • Cranial nerves 12 pairs.
  • Olfactory organs poor. Middle ear contains a single ossicle.
  • Eyes possess nictitating membrane.
    • Pecten
      • Pecten is a comb-like structure found in the eyes near the blind spot.
      • Pecten helps in the nutrition of the eyeball.
      • Pecten is found in all birds except Kiwi.

6. Muscular System

  • Flight muscles are present; three main categories are:
    • Pectoral muscles
      • Pectoralis major and
      • Pectoralis minor
    • Accessory muscles
      • Coraco-brachialis longus and
      • Coraco-brachialis brevis
    • Tensor muscles
      • Tensor longus
      • Tensor brevis and
      • Tensor accessorius

4. Flightless Birds

a. Characteristics of Flightless Birds

  • They have
    • Well-developed powerful legs
    • Small head
    • Rudimentary eyes
    • Rudimentary Wings

b. Features of Flightless Birds

  • Oil glands, Keel, Pygostyle, and Syrinx are absent.
  • Bones of legs are solid.
  • Male has a large penis.

c. Examples of Flightless Birds

  • Ostrich
    • African ostrich is the largest living bird.
    • They are polygamous as one male attends several females.
    • Egg of ostrich weighs nearly 1.5 kg and requires about 50 minutes to boil it. It is the largest egg among animals.
    • The male ostrich incubates the eggs.
    • Ostriches are easily domesticated.
  • Penguin
    • Penguins are found in various regions.
    • They are well-adapted to marine life and have lost the ability to fly.
  • Kiwi
    • Kiwi is found in New Zealand.
    • Kiwi is the smallest living flightless bird.
    • The nostrils of kiwi are located near the apex of the beak.
    • Kiwis are nocturnal and burrowing in habit.
    • Kiwis possess a good sense of smell unlike other birds.
    • In proportion to the size of the body, the kiwi lays the largest eggs of any known animal.
    • Oil glands are present in Kiwi (absent in all other flightless birds).

Mammalia

Introduction

  • Mammology = Study of mammals.
  • Mammals originated from reptiles in the Triassic period of the Mesozoic era.
  • Mammals dominate the Coenozoic era, earning it the name 'age of mammals'.

General Characters

  1. Mammals are hair-clad, mostly terrestrial tetrapod vertebrates.
  2. Hairs of mammals are made up of Î±-keratin.
  3. Most important character of mammals is the presence of Mammary glands (Modified sweat glands).
  4. Presence of a muscular diaphragm separating thoracic cavity from abdominal cavity.
  5. Body is distinctly divisible into:
    • Head
    • Neck
    • Trunk
    • Tail
  6. Limbs:
    • No. of pairs: 2
    • Pentadactyl each with five or few digits.
    • Adapted for walking, running, climbing, burrowing, swimming, or flying.
    • Hindlimbs are absent in aquatic Cetaceans and Sirenians.
  7. Exoskeleton consists of:
    • Lifeless, horny, epidermal hairs
    • Spines
    • Scales
    • Claws
    • Nails
    • Hooves
    • Horns
    • Bony dermal plates, etc.

Skull

  • Dicondylic skull with two occipital condyles (also in Amphibia).

Lower Jaw

  • Each half of the lower jaw with a single dentary bone.

Vertebra

  • Vertebra with flat centrum called amphiplatyan or acoelous.

Cervical Vertebrae

  • Presence of seven cervical vertebrae.

Pectoral Girdle

  • Coracoid of pectoral girdle is vestigial.

Ribs

  • Presence of 12 pairs of ribs (also 12 pairs of cranial nerves).

Skin

  • Skin is richly glandular containing:
    • Sweat glands
    • Sebaceous glands

Respiration System

  • Respiration by lungs (pulmonary), glottis protected by a fleshy and cartilaginous epiglottis.
  • Larynx with vocal cords.

Circulation System

  • Heart is completely 4-chambered (also in birds and crocodiles).
  • Sinus venosus absent.
  • Only left aortic (systemic) arch persists in adults.
  • Renal portal system absent.
  • Erythrocytes are:
    • Small
    • Circular
    • Non-nucleated

Excretion System

  • Kidneys are Metanephric.
  • Ureters open into a muscular urinary bladder.
  • Excretion ureotelic.

Nervous System

  • Brain is highly developed with corpus callosum connecting cerebral hemispheres.
  • Cranial nerves 12 pairs.

Ear

  • External ear with ear pinna.
  • Middle ear with three ear ossicles:
    • Malleus
    • Incus
    • Stapes

Reproduction

  • Sexes separate.
  • Testes mostly in scrotal sacs for sperm development.
  • Ovary contains Graafian follicles.
  • Eggs are:
    • Small
    • Microlecithal
    • Homolecithal
    • Acleidoic
  • Mammals are viviparous, except egg-laying monotremes.
  • Foetus nourishes through placenta.
  • Extraembryonic membranes (amnion, chorion, and allantois) present (amniota).
  • Parental care greatly developed.
  • Placenta is formed by chorio-allantois.

Mammals are the most intelligent animals among vertebrates.

Sub-class: Theria

Infra-class: Metatheria (Pouched mammals)

  • Metatherians are Pouched mammals.
    • Young are born in a very immature state.
    • Gestation is short, with complete development in the abdominal pouch or marsupium.
  • Corpus callosum absent.
  • Epipubic bone present.
  • No allantoic placenta.
  • Teeth one set, monophyodont.
  • Most primitive placental mammal is Shrew.
