    1. (a) Darwin
    2. (b) Lamarck
    3. (c) Hugo De Vries
    4. (d) Hutchinson
    (c) Hugo De Vries. The mutation theory of evolution was proposed by Hugo De Vries, a Dutch botanist, in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. De Vries suggested that new species arise through sudden, heritable changes in traits called "mutations," which can lead to the formation of new species. His theory contributed to the understanding of genetic variation and played a significant role in the development of the modern synthesis of evolutionary biology.
  12. Which of the following is a living fossil?
    1. (a) Limulus

  13. Which of the following is a connecting link between the animal and plant kingdom?
    1. (a) Euglena
    2. (b) Amoeba
    3. (c) Dinoflagellates
    4. (d) Diatom
    (a) Euglena. Euglena is considered a connecting link between the animal and plant kingdom. It is a single-celled organism that possesses both animal-like and plant-like characteristics. Euglena can undergo photosynthesis in the presence of light, similar to plants, but it can also feed on organic matter in its environment, like animals. This unique combination of characteristics makes it a transitional organism between the two kingdoms.

    (b) George Cuvier. George Cuvier is considered the founder of paleontology.   15. The main drawback of Darwin's theory is:
    1. (a) Natural selection
    2. (b) Survival of the fittest
    3. (c) Struggle for existence
    4. (d) Variation
    (d) Variation. The main drawback of Darwin's theory of evolution is the lack of a satisfactory explanation for the origin of variation. Darwin's theory emphasized natural selection as the driving force behind evolution, but it did not provide a comprehensive explanation for how new variations arise in populations. It wasn't until the discovery of genetics and the understanding of inheritance mechanisms that the source of genetic variation and its role in evolution were better understood.
  16. Earlier organisms were:
    1. (a) Chemoheterotrophs
    2. (b) Chemoautotrophs
    3. (c) Saprophytes
    4. (d) Protists
    (a) Chemoheterotrophs. Earlier organisms were primarily chemoheterotrophs. Chemoheterotrophs are organisms that obtain energy by breaking down organic compounds through chemical reactions and rely on external sources for obtaining organic molecules for their nutrition. They include various types of bacteria, fungi, and animals. The evolution of photosynthesis and the subsequent emergence of autotrophs paved the way for the development of more diverse and complex life forms.
  17. 3-stages of evolution was given by:
    1. (a) Earlier microscopists
    2. (b) Darwin
    3. (c) Modern biologists
    4. (d) Haeckel
    (d) Haeckel. Ernst Haeckel, a German biologist, proposed the concept of the three stages of evolution: ontogeny, phylogeny, and biogeny. Ontogeny refers to the development and growth of an individual organism from fertilization to maturity, phylogeny refers to the evolutionary history and relationships between different species, and biogeny suggests that the development of an individual organism during ontogeny recapitulates its evolutionary history during phylogeny. Although Haeckel's ideas have been largely discredited, they contributed to the early understanding of evolutionary development.
  18. The sun, the earth, the stars are formed from:
    1. (a) Aggregates of uranium
    2. (b) Clouds of gas and cosmic dust
    3. (c) Division of pre-existing planets
    4. (d) Collisions of meteors
    (b) Clouds of gas and cosmic dust. The sun, the earth, and the stars are formed from clouds of gas and cosmic dust in space. This process is known as stellar and planetary formation. The gravitational collapse of these clouds of gas and dust leads to the formation of dense cores, which eventually develop into stars or planets, depending on the conditions and composition of the cloud. This process is supported by observations and models in the field of astrophysics.
  19. Correct sequence among the following is:

  20. Coacervates are clusters of:
    1. (a) Proteins
    2. (b) Fats
    3. (c) Sugars
    4. (d) Sugars and fats
    (a) Proteins. Coacervates are clusters or aggregates of proteins. They are considered one of the early hypothetical models for the origin of life. Coacervates can form spontaneously when certain macromolecules, such as proteins, are mixed in an aqueous solution. These clusters have a semi-permeable membrane-like structure and can exhibit some properties associated with living cells, such as the ability to concentrate molecules and exhibit limited metabolic reactions. Coacervates are believed to have played a role in the early stages of the evolution of cellular life.

  31. The morphological study based on phylogenetic character and also dealing with homologous development is:
    1. (a) Phylogeny
    2. (b) Phenetics
    3. (c) Cladistics
    4. (d) Classical taxonomy
    (c) Cladistics. The morphological study based on phylogenetic characters and homologous development is known as cladistics. Cladistics is a method of classification that groups organisms based on shared characteristics called derived traits. It focuses on identifying and analyzing homologous traits, which are traits inherited from a common ancestor. By constructing phylogenetic trees based on these shared characteristics, cladistics aims to reveal the evolutionary relationships and branching patterns among organisms.
  32. The study of evidence of the origin of evolutionary branches according to the phylogenetic tree is called:
    1. (a) Phenetics
    2. (b) Cladistics
    3. (c) Euthenics
    4. (d) Classical evolutionary taxonomy
    (b) Cladistics. The study of evidence of the origin of evolutionary branches according to the phylogenetic tree is called cladistics. Cladistics focuses on analyzing and interpreting the branching patterns of the phylogenetic tree to understand the evolutionary relationships between different species. It uses shared derived traits (synapomorphies) to identify common ancestry and construct phylogenetic trees that represent the evolutionary history of organisms.

  34. Natural selection affects the organisms:
    1. (a) Homozygous recessive
    2. (b) Dominance
    3. (c) Phenotype
    4. (d) Combined genotype
    (c) Phenotype. Natural selection affects the phenotype of organisms. Phenotype refers to the observable characteristics or traits of an organism, including its physical appearance, behavior, and physiological properties. Natural selection acts on the phenotypes of individuals in a population, favoring traits that enhance survival and reproduction in a given environment. As a result, certain phenotypes become more common in subsequent generations, leading to evolutionary change.
  35. Evolution of photosynthetic ability resulted in the division of the living world into:
    1. (a) Unicellular and multicellular
    2. (b) Autotrophic and heterotrophic
    3. (c) Prokaryotes and eukaryotes
    4. (d) Fungi and plants
    (b) Autotrophic and heterotrophic. The evolution of photosynthetic ability resulted in the division of the living world into autotrophic and heterotrophic organisms. Autotrophs are organisms that can produce their own food using energy from sunlight (photosynthesis) or inorganic substances (chemosynthesis). Heterotrophs, on the other hand, rely on consuming other organisms or organic matter for their energy and nutrients. This division is significant in understanding the ecological dynamics and energy flow within ecosystems.
  36. Allopatric speciation is caused by:
    1. (a) Temporal isolation
    2. (b) Adaptive radiation
    3. (c) Geographic isolation
    4. (d) Reproductive isolation
    (c) Geographic isolation. Allopatric speciation is caused by geographic isolation. It occurs when a population of organisms becomes physically separated by a geographic barrier, such as a mountain range, river, or ocean. The isolation prevents gene flow between the separated populations, leading to independent evolutionary changes. Over time, genetic differences accumulate, and if reproductive isolation also develops, the separated populations may become distinct species.

  38. Who gave the principle of biogenetic law?
    1. (a) Hugo de Vries
    2. (b) Ernst Haeckel
    3. (c) Lamarck
    4. (d) Weismann
    (b) Ernst Haeckel. The principle of biogenetic law was proposed by Ernst Haeckel. Haeckel suggested that the development of an individual organism (ontogeny) recapitulates its evolutionary history (phylogeny). According to this principle, the stages of development of an organism from fertilization to adulthood reflect the evolutionary changes and ancestral relationships of its species. However, it is important to note that the biogenetic law is a simplification and does not fully represent the complexity of evolutionary processes.
  39. During the evolution of the Earth, the first compound formed was:

    3. (c) Glycoprotein
    4. (d) None
    (b) Amino acid. During the evolution of the Earth, the first compound formed was likely amino acids. Amino acids are the building blocks of proteins and are essential for life as we know it. Under certain conditions, such as in the early Earth's primordial soup or through extraterrestrial sources, simple organic molecules like amino acids could have been synthesized. These organic molecules eventually gave rise to more complex biomolecules and the origin of life.
  40. Which was most abundant during Earth's formation?
    1. (a) Methane
    2. (b) Hydrogen
    3. (c) Oxygen
    4. (d) Water
    (b) Hydrogen. Hydrogen was likely the most abundant element during Earth's formation. The early universe was primarily composed of hydrogen, and as the Earth formed from the remnants of a primordial nebula, hydrogen played a crucial role in the formation of its atmosphere, as well as in the processes that led to the origin of life. Hydrogen continues to be one of the most abundant elements in the universe and plays a fundamental role in many chemical reactions and physical processes.
