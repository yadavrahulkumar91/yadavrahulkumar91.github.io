Species and History

The most common species of Amoeba is Proteus.

History: Amoeba was discovered by Rossel von Rosenhoff.

Amoeba is cultured in the laboratory using the 'Hay infusion method'.

Cellular Structure

Plasmalemma:

  • Structure of Plasmalemma:
    1. Plasmalemma is trilaminar and selectively permeable.
    2. The outer layer is made up of adhesive mucoprotein called microvilli.
  • Function of Plasmalemma:
    1. Covers and protects the body.
    2. Respiratory and excretory functions.

Cytoplasm:

  • Cytoplasm is differentiated into outer ectoplasm and inner endoplasm.
  • Ectoplasm is tough and outer, while endoplasm is more fluid-like.

Nucleus:

  • Nucleus is solid and biconcave.
  • Contains a protein fiber network called honeycomb lattice.

Vacuoles:

  • Food vacuole: Analogous to an alimentary canal, changes in acidity during digestion.
  • Contractile vacuole: Involved in osmoregulation, absent in marine forms.

Introduction

The genus Entamoeba is commonly found in the intestines of vertebrates and invertebrates.

Nucleus Characteristics

Entamoeba is characterized by vesicular nuclei containing an endosome (nucleolus).

Vesicular Nuclei: Contain nucleoplasm and endosome centrally, surrounded by nuclear membrane.

Introduction:

  • Commonly known as the tissue-dissolving parasite.

Habitat (Location in Human Body):

  • Endoparasite found in the lower part of the ileum and upper part of the colon.

Life Cycle and Pathogenicity:

  • Monogenetic parasite with only one host.
  • Causes amoebiasis or amoebic dysentery and liver abscess in chronic conditions.
  • Stool of a person suffering from amoebiasis is acidic.

Cellular Structure:

  • Has two forms: Magna (Trophozoite) and Minuta (Precyst and Cyst).
  • Magna form is pathogenic and found in the mucosa and submucosa of the intestine, causing ulcers.
  • Minuta form is non-pathogenic and found in the lumen of the intestine.
  • Magna form is feeding and motile, while Minuta form is non-feeding and non-motile.
  • Magna form is larger, active, and has no fixed shape, while Minuta form is smaller and spherical.

Life Cycle Details:

  • Most pathogenic stage is the trophozoite, producing finger-like pseudopodia for feeding.
  • No contractile vacuole in Entamoeba histolytica.
  • Trophozoite reproduces by mitotic binary fission within the host's gut.
  • Minuta form undergoes encystation in the intestine, forming 'Precystic amoeba'.
  • Encystation is initiated by the action of Trypsin.
  • Mature cyst is quadrinucleate, contains chromatoidal bodies and glycogen as food reserve.
  • Quadrinucleated cyst is the infective stage, with each cyst producing 8 individuals.

Mode of Transmission:

  • Transmitted through contaminated food and water containing cysts.
  • Houseflies are common vectors for transmission.

Treatment:

  • Effective drugs include Metronidazole, Tinidazole, and Ornidazole.

Reproduction

Asexual reproduction (By Binary fission)/ Transverse fission

  1. At first, Paramecium stops feeding and Oral groove disappears.
  2. The binary fission is called Transverse fission because division takes place right angle to the body axis.
  3. In binary fission,
    1. macronucleus divides Amitotically and
    2. micronucleus Mitotically.
  4. Each Paramecium divides into two daughter Paramecia.
    1. Anterior one is called as Proter.
    2. Posterior one is called as Opisthe.
  5. Binary fission occurs during favorable conditions.
  6. It is Faster multiplication.
    1. It takes about half an hour to 2 hours to complete binary fission.
    2. In a single day, about 2 to 3 binary fissions occur.

Sexual reproduction (By conjugation)

  1. Conjugation is a temporary union of two individuals of different mating types for the exchange of micronuclear material.
  2. Conjugation overcomes aging or senescence which is caused due to repeated binary fissions.
  3. Conjugation brings about nuclear reorganization.
    1. In Paramecium, nuclear reorganization takes place for Rejuvenation.

Process of Conjugation

  1. During conjugation, two Paramecia of different mating types unite ventrally by their oral grooves. They are called as Conjugants.
  2. Macronucleus disintegrates and gets hydrolysed by enzymes and ultimately absorbed by the cytoplasm.
  3. Micronucleus divides twice, one of the divisions is reductional division, thus, 4 haploid daughter nuclei are formed out of which 3 disintegrate and 1 remains functional.
  4. The functional one divides unequally and forms 2 nuclei, one larger stationaly known as female pro-nucleus and the other smaller migratory male pro-nucleus.
  5. The male pronuclei are exchanged between the conjugants and fusion between the exchanged male pronucleus and stationary female pronucleus occurs.
    • A diploid zygote nucleus is formed which is also called as Synkaryon.
    • The complete fusion of nuclei of two different individuals is termed as Amphimixis.
  6. The conjugants get separated and are now known as ex-conjugants.
    • The zygote nucleus of each ex-conjugant divides 3 times by mitotic division and 8 daughter nuclei are formed.
    • Out of 8 daughter nuclei, 4 are larger and called macronuclei, and the other 4 are smaller and called micronuclei.
      1. Out of 4 micronuclei, 3 disintegrate. The remaining micronucleus divides by mitosis and forms 2 micronuclei.
      2. At the same time, binary fission occurs in each ex-conjugant and thus 2 daughter paramecia with 2 macronuclei and 1 micronucleus are produced.
      3. Again, binary fission occurs after which each micronucleus divides into two micronuclei. Thus, each ex-conjugant gives 4 Paramecia.
  7. Thus, 8 daughter Paramecia are produced in conjugation.

Note on Reproduction:

  1. Autogamy
    1. Micronucleus of a single individual divides and fuses to form micro and macronuclei.
    2. Autogamy is Self-fertilization.
    3. Autogamy occurs in Paramecium aurelia.
    4. Autogamy results in the production of two daughter Paramecia from each.
  2. Cytogamy
    1. Two individuals come in contact; no breakdown of cell membrane and no exchange of nuclear material.
    2. Cytogamy occurs in Paramecium caudatum.
    3. The two cytogamonts do not exchange their male pronuclei.
  3. Endomixis (Parthenogenesis)
    1. Macronucleus disappears, micro divides and without fusion gives new micro and macronuclei in a single individual.
    2. Endomixis occurs in Paramecium aurelia.
    3. Endomixis is a form of asexual reproduction.
    4. In Endomixis, 1 individual produces 4 daughter Paramecia.

Note on Particles

  1. Paramecium has Kappa(Κ), Lamda(λ), Mu(μ) and Pi(π) particles in the cytoplasm.
  2. Kappa particle
    1. In some Paramecium, there is the presence of a special particle in the cytoplasm known as Kappa particles (k-particles).
      1. Paramecium containing Kappa particles secretes a toxic substance called Paramecin.
      2. These Paramecium are called killer strains.
    2. Features of Kappa particles
      1. Kappa particles are very minute.
      2. Kappa particles are capable of self-replicating.
      3. Kappa particles contain nucleic acids.
      4. Kappa particles are also concerned with cytoplasmic inheritance.
    3. If an individual with k-particles (Killer strain) happens to conjugate with an individual without K-particles (Sensitive strain), the individual without K-particles will die.
