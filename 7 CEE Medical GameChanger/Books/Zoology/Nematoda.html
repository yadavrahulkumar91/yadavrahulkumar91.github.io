Features of Nematoda

Characteristics

  1. Features
    1. Triploblastic
    2. Bilaterally symmetrical
    3. Vermiform
    4. Unsegmented cylindrical
    5. True worms
    6. Pseudocoelomate (i.e., pseudocoelom or blastocoel)
      1. Pseudocoelom/false coelom derived from embryonic blastocoel.
    7. Tube within a tube body plan
      1. Alimentary canals of roundworms have both mouth and anus.
      2. Digestive tract complete.
    8. Organ-System grade of body organization
      1. Both respiratory system and Circulatory systems are absent.
        1. Gaseous exchange occurs by General body surface.
        2. So no blood.
      2. Excretory system unique comprised of one or two Renette cell or (H-shaped cell) (Set of collecting tubules) (Protonephridia)
      3. Nervous system consists of Circumpharyngeal nerve ring and six longitudinal nerve cords.
      4. Reproductive system is well developed.
        1. Diocious with sexual diamorphism. [First unisexual phylum].
        2. Eggs with chitinous shell.
        3. Cleavage = Determinate and Spiral.

Body Structure

  1. Body round in cross section and is covered with transparent cuticle composed of Scleroprotein.
    1. After cuticle, Syncytial epidermis is present without cilia.
    2. Body wall has only longitudinal muscles.

Development and Growth

  1. During development, volume of cell increases (No. increasement in the no. of cells). Larva changes into adult, phenomenon is known as Auxetic growth.

Distinctive Features

  1. Body made up of fixed no. of somatic cell known as "Eutely".
  2. No distinct head.
  3. Mouth surrounded by lips bearing sense organs.
  4. Presence of Sensory papillae.
    1. Amphid - in mouth
      1. Amphid is Olfactoreceptor in Ascarisc
    2. Phasmid - in Anus
      1. Phasmid are Chemoreceptor and Glandulo-receptor.
    3. Papillae - Tangoreceptor

Classification

  2. Class - Gastrotricha
  3. Class - Kinorhycha
  4. Class - Nematomorpha
  5. Class - Nematoda

    2. Group - Phasmida

Ascaris lumbricoides (Roundworm) Notes

Overview

  1. Ascaris is the most common endoparasite of man.
  2. Residence in man = Small intestine of man (Mostly in Jejunum)

Pathology and Disease

  1. Pathology
    1. Disease = Ascariasis
    2. Symptoms:
      1. Abdominal discomfort
      2. Vomiting
      3. Fever
      4. Patient grit at night
      5. Large no. of adult worms sometimes cause mechanical blockage of the intestinal tract causing colic pain.
    3. Toxin produced by Ascaris may interfere with protein digestion.
    4. Treatment
      1. Antihelminthic drugs
        1. Chenopodium
        2. Antipar
        3. Alcopar
        4. Santonin
        5. Tetrachloroethylene
        6. Decaris
        7. Diethylcarbamazine
      2. One-time treatment drugs
        1. Albendazole
        2. Mebendazole
        3. Pyrantel pamoate

Anatomy and Physiology

  1. Body structure
    1. Elongated, Unsegmented cylindrical with tapering ends.
    2. Sexes are separate with sexual dimorphism.
    3. Four longitudinal streaks or lines are found through the semitransparent cuticle.
      1. One mid-dorsal
      2. One mid-ventral
      3. Two laterals
    4. Body wall
      • Consists of
        1. Outer cuticle
          • Thick, four-layered, secreted by underlying epidermis.
          • Protects from Mechanical injury and Resistant to action of digestive enzymes of the host.
          • Specialized character of Parasitism
        2. Middle Epidermis
        3. Inner longitudinal muscle layer

Reproduction and Life Cycle

  1. Reproduction
    1. Ascaris is dioecious or unisexual.
  2. Life cycle
    1. Eggs
      1. The eggs are fertilized in the lower part of Uteri.
      2. The egg of Ascaris coming out of the body of hosts through faecal matter are fertilized and cleaved.
      3. Ascaris is monogenetic.
      4. The egg is
        • Mammilated
          1. Each egg is surrounded by a protein coat with a rippled surface hence the eggs are called mammillated eggs.
        • Oval in shape
        • Three protective coverings
          1. Outer Protein layer
          2. Middle Chitinous shell
          3. Inner membrane made of esterified glycosides.
      5. Embryonic development takes place only outside the body of human host in soil because it requires low temperature, more oxygen, and suitable moisture.
      6. Eggs containing infective larvae may remain viable in soil for two years or longer.
    2. Zygote will be developed into a juvenile (first stage) within 10-14 days.
    3. Larva
      1. The larva is called rhabditoid or rhabditiform for its close resemblance with Rhabditis.
      2. The larva of the first stage is not infective. It rests for a week and completes the first moult within the egg and becomes the second stage rhabditiform larva which is infective.
      3. The transmission of the infective stage through embryonated eggs takes place by contaminated food and water.
      4. Infective eggs of Ascaris remain viable for about 6 years.
      5. The embryonated eggs pass into the intestine of a man and the second stage larva hatches out from the eggs in the duodenum.
      6. Three types of Migration by Ascaris larvae are
        1. Primary migration
          • Intestinal wall → hepatic portal vein → Liver → Hepatic vein → Heart → Pulmonary artery → Lungs
        2. Secondary migration
          • Lungs → bronchi → trachea → pharynx → gullet → Oesophagus → Stomach → Intestine
        3. Aberrant migration
          • Lungs → Spleen/Liver/Lymph nodes/Brain/Spinal cord and Eyes usually elicit an inflammatory response.
      7. Moulting
        • In the lungs, the larva completes its second and third moulting.
        • In the small intestine, it completes the fourth or final moulting which is essential for the worm's survival, and only those that undergo this final moult develop to sexual maturity.

Ancylostoma duodenale (Hookworm) Notes

Overview

  1. Monogenetic
    1. Found in the small intestine of humans and feed upon blood (Sanguivorous).
  2. Mode of infection
    1. By penetration of soft skin of soles. Wearing slippers prevents it.
  3. Disease = Ancylostomiasis
    • Moulting Place Larval stage (Filariform)
      1st moulting Soil 2nd stage Filariform larva
      2nd moulting Soil 3rd stage Filariform larva (Infective stage)
      3rd moulting Oesophagus 4th stage Filariform larva
      4th moulting Small intestine Adult
  4. Infective stage = Filariform larva (3rd stage larva)

Reproduction

  1. Male hookworm
    • Copulatory bursa with bursal rays: Present
    • Cloaca or Anus: Cloaca
  2. Female hookworm
    • Copulatory bursa with bursal rays: Absent
    • Cloaca or Anus: Anus
  3. No. of teeth is 3 pairs with 2 pairs ventrally and 1 pair dorsally.
