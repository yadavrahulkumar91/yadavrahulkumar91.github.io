TISSUE SYSTEM

Epidermal Tissue System

  • Forms outer covering of plants
  • It includes:

    • Epidermis:

    1. Epi means upon + derma means skin and hence it is a continuous outer layer interrupted by Stomata and Lenticels.
    2. Generally single layered but multilayered epidermis is found in:
      1. Phylloclades of Casuarina,
      2. Leaves of Nerium and Ficus as well as
      3. Hygroscopic roots of epiphytes
        1. Velamen tissue found on the surface of hygroscopic root is multiple epidermis whose cells are dead.
    3. Layer of cuticle is found on the epidermis of stem and leaves but absent in root.
    4. Cuticle layer acts as protection to epidermis and checks excessive transpiration.
    5. Epidermal cells are living and generally don't possess chloroplast except shade plants (Sciophytes), hydrophytes and some ferns, etc.
    6. Further chloroplasts are present in guard cells (Specialized epidermal cells).
    7. Note: Guard cells (living cell) even having chloroplast is heteromorphic due to the absence of RUBISCO.
    8. Bulliform cells or Motor cells or Bubble-like cells are found in upper epidermis of isobilateral leaves (monocot).
    9. It helps in rolling and unrolling of leaves (mainly rolling).
    10. Outermost layer of root is known as Epiblema or Piliferous layer or Rhizodermis which is not cutinised or suberised and also doesn't contain stomata.
    11. Stomata and lenticels are confined to Epidermis.
    12. In grasses and Equisetum, silica is present in the epidermal cells.
    13. Epidermal cells containing cystoliths (CaCO3) are called lithocysts.

    Stomata:

    1. Minute pores occur on epidermal surface of leaves and also some herbaceous stem.
    2. Each stoma/pore is guarded by 2 specialized kidney-shaped or bean-shaped epidermal cells, called Guard cells.
    3. Guard cells are also guarded by specialized epidermal cells called subsidiary cells/ accessory cells.
    4. In some monocots like doob grass (Cynodon dactylon) and maize, guard cells are dumb-bell or barbell-shaped.
    5. In dicot, guard cells are kidney-shaped.
    6. In dicot leaves, stomata are scattered whereas in monocots these are scattered in rows.
    7. In dicots, stomata are generally present on lower epidermis whereas in monocots on both surfaces.
    8. In free-floating hydrophytes (e.g. Lotus, Nymphea), stomata are present on upper epidermis, whereas in submerged hydrophytes, these are absent or non-functional.
    9. In algae and fungi, stomata are totally absent.

    Trichome/ Hairs:

    1. Unicellular → in Roots
    2. Multicellular → in Stems
    3. Trichomes help in checking excess loss of water and secondly provide protection.

    Ground Tissue System

    Between epidermis and vascular tissue.

    It comprises:

    Hypodermis

    1. Outermost layer of Cortex.
    2. Collenchymatous in herbaceous dicot stem.
    3. Sclerenchymatous in monocot stem.

    Cortex

    1. Present inside hypodermis.
    2. Usually parenchymatous with abundant intercellular space.
    3. Innermost layer of cortex is called as Endodermis.

    Endodermis/ Starch Sheath:

    1. Endodermis is single layered which separates cortex from stele.
    2. Part of extrastelar ground tissue system.
    3. Cells of endodermis are:
      1. Barrel-shaped,
      2. Without inter-cellular spaces,
      3. Living, and
      4. Containing starch, hence known as starch sheath.
    4. Radial and tangential walls of endodermal cells possess thickenings of lignin, suberin, and cutin in the form of casparian stripes or Casparian bands.
    5. Passage cells are present just against protoxylem in root. Casparian stripes and passage cells are found in exodermis also.
    6. A well-developed endodermis is a constant feature of roots (not in stem).

    Pericycle

    1. Single or Few layered.
    2. In Smilax root (monocot), pericycle is multilayered and sclerenchymatous.
    3. In some cases like Sunflower stem, pericycle is heterogeneous i.e., composed of both thick-walled and thin-walled cells.
    4. Thin-walled parenchymatous or rarely thick-walled sclerenchymatous cells inside the endodermis.
    5. Present in most roots except in roots of parasitic plants and hydrophytes.
    6. Thick-walled pericycle is mechanical in function whereas thin-walled pericycle is storage in function.
    7. In dicot root, pericycle can also form Cambium.

    Medulla (Pith) and Medullary Rays (Pith Rays)

    1. The core of plant axis is occupied by a mass of parenchymatous tissue called Medulla or Pith.
    2. Medulla radiated out and present between vascular bundle is called Medullary rays or Pith rays.
    3. Medulla helps in storage of food while medullary rays help in lateral transportation.
    4. Pith is distinct in Dicot stem and Monocot root, scattered in Monocot stem but indistinct in Dicot root.
    5. In some monocots, the central region of the stem is hollow and is called Pith cavity.
    6. Pith is developed in Dicot stem and Monocot root.

    Conjunctive Tissue

    1. Parenchymatous tissue present between Xylem and Phloem bundles.

    Part of ground tissue present outside endodermis is called Cortex and inside endodermis is called Stele.

    1. Stele includes all tissue inside the endodermis.
    2. Stele in monocot is atactostele and in dicot is eustele.
    3. Endodermis is absent in monocot stem. Thus, ground tissue of monocot stem is not differentiated into cortex and stele.

    III. Vascular Tissue System

    1. Central column of axis (root and stem) is called 'Stele' which is made of a number of vascular bundles, constituting vascular tissue system.
    2. Stelar region includes all tissues inside pericycle.
    3. The vascular bundle has Xylem, Phloem, and Cambium (if present).
      1. Note: Cambium is a stripe of Primary meristem.
    Vascular bundle is mainly 3 types.

    Radial:

    1. Xylem and Phloem occur in separate bundles, present in different radii, alternate to each other and equal in number.
    2. E.g: Roots (Dicot and Monocot both).

    Conjoint:

  • Xylem and Phloem in the same bundle and in the same radius. E.g: Stem
  • 2 types:

    • Collateral:

    Phloem lies towards the outer side or pericycle and xylem towards the center or pith.

    Open:
    1. Xylem and Phloem separated by Cambium layer.
    2. Cambium is absent.
    3. E.g: Dicot stem
    Closed:
    1. Xylem and Phloem separated by Cambium layer.
    2. Cambium is absent.
    3. E.g: Monocot stem

    Bicollateral:

    1. Phloem is present on both sides of xylem.
    2. The bundle is always Open.
    3. Arrangement of Vascular tissue = Phloem → Cambium → Xylem → Cambium → Phloem
    4. Examples:

      5. Stem of members of:

      1. Cucurbitaceae
      2. Solanaceae
      3. Convulvulaceae family
    5. Q. Bicollateral vascular bundles are found in the stem of:
      1. Cycas/ Sunflower
      2. Gram/ Canna
      3. Pumpkin
      4. Dracaena

    Concentric:

  • Xylem and Phloem are found in a definite circle.
  • Always closed.
  • Concentric vascular bundles are characteristics of pteridophytes and a few monocots.
  • 2 types:

    • Amphicribal/ (Hadro) - Xylem centric:

    1. Xylem centrally located and surrounded by Phloem.
    2. E.g: Found in underground stem of some monocots like:
      1. Ferns
      2. Some aquatic angiosperms
      3. Lycopodium
      4. Selaginella

    Amphivasal/ (Lepto) - Phloem centric:

    1. Xylem surrounds centrally situated Phloem.
    2. E.g: Found in underground stem of some monocots like:
      1. Dagger plant (Yucca)
      2. Dragon plant (Dracaena)
      3. Sweet flag (Acorus)

    Root-stem transition:

    1. Conversion of radial and exarch vascular bundle into conjoint and endarch vascular bundles is called as Root-stem transition.
    2. Swinging of xylem occurs during this transition and Phloem remains stationary.
    3. Root-stem transition zone is present in Hypocotyl.

    Origin of Lateral roots and Lateral branches of Stem:

    1. Origin of lateral root is endogenous and is originated from pericycle.
    2. Seat of origin of lateral root is situated just against Protoxylem.
    3. Origin of lateral branches of stem is Exogenous.
    4. Lateral branches are originated from outer part of Cortex.

    Leaf Anatomy: Dicot vs Monocot

    S.N. Dicot leaf Monocot leaf
    1. Thickness of cuticle Thicker in upper epidermis than in lower epidermis. Same in both epidermis.
    2. Bulliform/ Motor cells Absent Present
    3. Number of stomata Greater in lower surface than upper. Equal in both surfaces.
    4. Stomata guarded by Kidney-shaped guard cells. Dumb-bell shaped guard cells.
    5. Mesophyll divided into
    • a. Upper tightly packed palisade parenchyma
    • b. Lower loosely arranged spongy parenchyma
    		 Not differentiated into spongy and palisade parenchyma.
    6. Bundle sheath extension Present in larger veins is parenchymatous. Present in sclerenchymatous.
    7. Bundle sheath cells Colourless Green and help in C4 cycle. This type is known as Kranz anatomy.
    8. Lysigenous cavity in xylem Absent Usually present

    Secondary Growth in Dicot Stem

    1. Cells of medullary rays become meristematic and form interfascicular cambium to join intrafascicular cambium to form a complete ring called Cambium ring.
    2. The cambium ring consists of 2 types of cells, the fusiform initials and the ray initials.
      1. Fusiform initials form secondary phloem on the exterior and secondary xylem on the interior side.
        • That present in secondary xylem is known as Wood or Xylem ray.
        • That present in secondary phloem is called Phloem ray.
    3. Ray initials form parenchymatous medullary rays.
    4. In spring (favourable season), cambium produces conducting elements of wider diameter called Spring or Early wood.
    5. In winter, it produces elements of narrow diameter known as Autumn or Late wood.
    6. Each year's growth is distinct due to sudden stem appear as concentric rings known as Growth rings/ Annual rings and represent total wood formed in a year.
      1. Each year's growth is distinct due to the sudden transition from autumn wood to spring wood of the next year. The transition from spring to autumn wood is gradual.
      2. Annual rings are used to study the climate of that area known as Dendroclimatology.
      3. Annual rings are formed due to unequal activity of cambium in autumn and spring seasons.
      4. Annual rings are absent in plants growing in places having uniform climate.
      5. The age determination of trees by counting the annual growth rings is known as Dendrochronology.

    Wood Types: Hard Wood vs Soft Wood

    i. Hard Wood/ Porous Wood: Soft Wood/ Non-porous Wood:
    • Woods with a number of pores due to the presence of vessels. E.g: Dicots.
    1) Ring Porous Wood: 2) Diffuse Porous Wood:
    • Such wood where vessels are concentrated in the spring wood.
    • Produced due to seasonal variation.
    Early Wood/ Spring Wood: Late Wood/ Autumn Wood
    Wide lumen vessels wood produced during spring season when the growth activities are maximum. Narrow lumen vessels wood produced during winter when the cambium remains dormant.
    • Such wood where vessels are evenly distributed. Eg: In the evergreen tropics.
    • Found in areas where there is no climatic variation.
    • Wood that doesn't contain vessels (pores). E.g: Gymnosperms.

    Wood Characteristics: Heart Wood vs Sap Wood

    iii. Heart Wood/ Duramen: Sap Wood/ Alburnum:
    • The dead dark coloured portion of the wood at the centre due to the deposition of organic compounds like oil, aromatic substances, gums, resins, tannins, etc.
    • Heart wood helps in mechanical support.
    • The substances deposited in the heartwood are collectively called Extractives.
    • Heart wood increases in diameter during secondary growth.
    • Heartwood is much more durable and resistant to micro-organisms and insects, pests, etc. than sapwood.
    • Oils, resins, gums, and tannins are deposited in the cells of heartwood which provide color to the heartwood. These are also antiseptic, so heartwood is hard and durable.
    • The vessels of heartwood get plugged by the ingrowths of balloon-like projections of adjacent parenchyma into their cavities called Tyloses.
    • The peripheral light coloured, wetter, water-conducting wood.
    • Functional xylem of dicot tree is Sapwood.
