Introduction

  1. Study of Lichens is called as Lichenology.
  2. Lichens are symbiotic association between Algae (Green or Blue green algae) and Fungi (Ascomycetes and Basidiomycetes).
    • Algal component/partner called Phycobiont.
      1. E.g: Blue-green algae, Green algae
      2. Note: Chlorophyll of the algal component absorbs SO2 by replacing Mg++ ions and forms a toxic substance called "Phycophytin" which causes the death of lichen.
        • Lichens do not grow near big cities because lichens are very sensitive to air pollution or smoke, especially SO2 pollution.
      3. Lichens are also good absorbers of Radioactivity.
    • Fungal component/partner called Mycobiont
      1. E.g.: Ascomycetes, Basidiomycetes
  3. Lichens have a distinct structure different from algae and fungal partner.
  4. Lichens show very slow growth.
  5. Lichens are composite or dual organisms.
  6. Lichens are also called as "Fungi parasitizing algae".
  7. The relationship of Fungi and Algae as Master and Slave respectively is called Helotism.
    • Accordingly, fungus performs
      1. Absorption
      2. Protection
      3. Reproduction while
    • Algae only do photosynthesis.
  8. The plant body in lichen is a thallus, irregular shaped and differently coloured.

Classification of Lichens

  1. Based on Habitat:
    • Saxicolous: Lichens which grow in rocks/stones.
    • Terricolous: Lichens which grow on soil.
    • Corticolous: Lichens which grow on the bark of trees.
    • Lignicolous: Lichens which grow on wood.
  2. Based on Fungal partner/component:
    • Ascolichen: Fungal partner belongs to ascomycetes.
      1. Majority of lichens are ascolichens.
      2. Gymnocarpae: When the fruiting body is Apothecium type (cup-shaped).
      3. Pyrenocarpae: When the fruiting body is Perithecium type (flask-shaped).
      4. Note: In ascolichens, the algal partner is mostly a member of green algae and rarely blue-green algae.
    • Basidiolichens: Fungal partner belongs to basidiomycetes.
      1. In basidiolichens algae partner is generally "blue-green algae".
    • Special Points:
      1. In 80% case of lichen, the algal partner is a member of green algae or chlorophyceae and in 20% cases blue-green algae or myxophyceae.
      2. Important members of green algae in lichens are:
        • Trebauxia
        • Cladophora
        • Pleurococcus
      3. Common blue-green algae are:
        • Anabaena
        • Nostoc
        • Scytonema
      4. Most of lichens are the association of Green algae and Ascomycetes.
  3. Structure of Thallus and Its attachment to substratum:
    • Crustose:
      1. Entire thallus is attached to the substratum so that it cannot be separated without damaging it.
      2. E.g: Graphis, Lecanora, Dermatocarpon, Rhizocarpon
    • Foliose
      1. Thallus is lobed (leafy) and is attached to the substratum with the help of Rhizines.
      2. E.g.: Parmelia (Rock flower), Peltigera (Dog lichen), Physcia
    • Fructicose: (Shrubby lichens)
      1. These are much-branched lichens with a bushy appearance and attached to the substratum by a disc.
      2. E.g.: Usnea (Old man's beard), Cladonia (Reindeer moss), Cetraria (Iceland moss)
    • Note: Racodium is a filamentous lichen.
  4. Algal component:
    • Chlorophycophilous lichens: algal partner belongs to chlorophyceae (green algae).
    • Cyanophycophilous lichens: algal partner belongs to cyanophyceae (blue-green algae).
  5. Anatomy to thallus:
    • Homoimerous:
      1. Algal and fungal partners are uniformly distributed throughout the lichen thallus.
      2. They are rare.
      3. E.g: Crustose.
    • Heteromerous:
      1. Thallus consisting of distinct algal and fungal zones.
      2. Most of the lichens.
      3. E.g: Foliose, Fructicose

Some special structures associated with lichen thallus

  1. Breathing pores:
    • Loosely arranged areas in the upper cortex are breathing pores and they help in gaseous exchange.
  2. Cyphellae:
    • Loosely arranged areas in the lower cortex are cyphellae and these also help in gaseous exchange.
    • Cyphellae are analogous to stomata of higher plants.
  3. Cephalodia:
    • Cephalodia retain moisture.
  4. Isidia:
    • Isidia increase the surface area of lichen and are means of vegetative reproduction.

Reproduction in Lichens

  1. Vegetative reproduction:
    • Fragmentation
    • Isidia:
      1. Isidia is an outgrowth of lichen thallus which after detachment can form a new thallus.
    • Soredia:
      1. These are granular structures produced in a large number at a particular patch of lichen thallus called Soralium.
      2. Soredia are algal cells surrounded by fungal hyphae. Soredia, after separation from the parent thallus, can regenerate a new body.
      3. Q. A group of algal cells imprisoned in fungal mycelium serving for vegetative multiplication of a lichen is called
        • Isidium
        • Soredium
        • Cephalodium
        • Helotism
  2. Asexual reproduction:
    • By the formation of pycnidiospores or pycniospores which are produced inside pycnidium, which is a flask-shaped structure opening through a pore called Ostiole.
  3. Sexual reproduction:
    • Sexual reproduction is a function of the fungal partner only, algae has no role.
    • Male sex organ is called Spermatogonium and inside it a number of non-motile sperms or spermatia are produced.
    • Female sex organ is carpogonium which has a lower coiled portion called Ascogonium and an upper straight portion coming out through the upper cortex called Trichogyne.
    • After fertilization, a fruiting body is formed. Fruiting body is either apothecium type (Cup-shaped) or perithecium (flask-shaped).

Economic importance

  1. As food and fodder:
    • Lichens are rich in carbohydrates.
  2. Lichens as a source of dye:
    • Litmus (acid-base indicator) is obtained from Rocella tinctoria.
  3. Lichens as a source of Medicine:
    • Medicinal property of lichens is due to a substance Lichenin.
    • Lobaria (lungwort) is used to treat TB and lung cancer.
    • Xanthoria is used as medicine for Jaundice.
  4. Ecological importance:
    • Lichens are called Pioneer of vegetation in Rock/Lithosere.
    • Crustose lichens are pioneers in Xerosere (succession beginning in dry conditions).
    • In Xerosere, the sequence of different stages is: Crustose lichens → Foliose lichens → Moss stage → Herb stage → Shrub stage → Tree (climax)
  5. Poisonous lichens:
    • Cetraria juniperina
    • Letharia vulpina (wolf moss)
  6. Sweet scented lichen thalli used for making soaps, cosmetics, perfumes are:
    • Evernia
    • Ramalina
