Introduction

  • Gymnosperms are called "naked-seeded" plants because they lack an ovary.
  • Some members of gymnosperms are known as "living fossils," which are organisms present in both living and fossil forms without significant evolutionary changes.
    • Examples: Cycas, Ginkgo, Metasequoia

Conduction Tissue

  • Xylem is without vessels, except in the Gnetales order where vessels are present (+ve).
    • Gnetum, Ephedra, Welwitschia
  • Phloem lacks companion cells and instead has albuminous tissue (+ve).
  • Sieve tube is absent, and sieve cells are present (+ve).

Leaf and Size

  • Leaves are unipinnately compounded.
  • Gymnosperms are generally large-sized and branched, with exceptions like Cycas and Zamia pygmaea (smallest gymnosperm).

Reproductive Structures and Pollination

  • Male and female reproductive structures are called Male cone and Female cone, respectively, except for the Female cone of Cycas.
  • Pollination is anemophilous (by wind).
  • Pollination in gymnosperms involves the transfer of microspores from microsporophyll to ovule directly.

Fertilization and Endosperm

  • Ovule is typically orthotropous, except in Pinus, where it's anatropous (PAN).
  • Fertilization is Siphonogamous type, except for Cycas, which has Zooidogamous type.
    • Zooidogamous type involves motile male gamete movement with the help of flagella or cilia.
    • Siphonogamous type involves non-motile male gametes and pollen tube.
  • Endosperm is haploid.

Cycas Plants

Introduction

  • Cycas is a member of Cycadopsida.
  • Cycas revoluta is native to Nepal.

Habitat and Plant Body

  • The plant is found in physical xerophytic conditions, which include hot climates and tropical regions.
  • The plant body is an unbranched tree, differentiated into:
    • Root
      • Coralloid root is -ve geotropic (apogeotropic) and contains Nostoc and Anabaena symbiotically.
    • Stem
      • The stem is solid, cylindrical, and unbranched, referred to as Caudex type.
      • Leaf scars are found around the stem due to persistent leaf bases.
      • The wood is porous and soft, commercially less important.
    • Leaf
      • Leaves show xerophytic adaptations like thick cuticles and sunken stomata.

Reproductive Structures

  • Male plant consists of Male cone where microspores or pollen grains are produced.
  • Female plant doesn't contain a female cone, but megasporophylls are loosely arranged in a rosette form.

Ovules and Fertilization

  • Ovules of Cycas are orthotropous, unitegmic, and the largest among the plant kingdom.
  • Ovules consist of:
    • Pollen chamber
    • Archegonial chamber
    • Archegonia without Neck Canal Cell (NCC)
      • 2 Neck cells (Cover cells) are +ve.

Male Gametes and Pollination

  • Male gametes of Cycas are top-shaped, multiflagellated, and the largest among the plant kingdom.
  • Pollination is anemophilous type, taking place at the 3-celled stage.
    • 1 Prothallial cell (PC)
    • 1 Generative cell (GC)
    • 1 Tube cell (TC)

Fertilization and Seed Development

  • Fertilization is zooidiogamous type, intermediate between zooidiogamy and Siphonogamy.
  • Pollen tube is formed but does not carry the male gamete; it only works as a haustorium to absorb liquid.
  • After fertilization, the ovule develops into two cotyledons.

Pinus Species and Plant Characteristics

Species of Pinus Commonly Found in Nepal

  • 3-needled: Pinus roxburghii/ Chir pine/ Rani salla
  • 5-needled: Pinus wallichiana/ Blue pine/ Gobre salla

Plant Body and Characteristics

  • Plant body of Pinus is sporophyte divided into Root, Stem, and Leaves.
  • Root
    • Tap root and lateral roots are developed.
    • Vascular bundle:
      • Diarch to tetrarch arrangement.
      • Radial type arrangement.
      • Protoxylem = Exarch.
      • Resin canals found between patches of protoxylem.
    • Pith absent or reduced (Protostele).
  • Stem
    • One main axis of growth (Monopodial).
    • Main stem is monopodial and excurrent.
    • Two types of branches:
      • Long branches (unlimited growth).
      • Short branches (limited growth, with needles).
    • Wavy stem due to leaf base presence.
    • Layer structure: Cuticle, Epidermis, Cortex zone, Endodermis, Parenchymatous.
    • Vascular bundle:
      • Conjoint, collateral, open endarch arrangement.
      • Wood characteristics:
        • Monoxylic and Pycnoxylic (compact and hard).
        • Stele = Eustele.
        • Secondary growth is normal.
        • Tracheids possess bordered pits.
  • Leaf
    • Needle-like foliage leaves on dwarf shoots.
    • Cataphylls/scale leaves on both long and dwarf shoots.
    • Wavy due to leaf bases.

Sexual Reproduction

  • Pinus is Monoecious and Autoecious.
  • Monoecious: Male and Female cones found on separate branches of the same tree.
  • Fertilization occurs after 11-15 months of pollination, by Siphonogamy (Non-motile gamete).
  • Seeds are matured and ready for germination after 3 years of development.
  • Seed germination is Epigeal type.
  • Each seed consists of 2 to 18 cotyledons.

Male Cone

Male cones develop in the axils of scale leaves found in clusters.

Female Cone

Female cones arise in the axils of scale leaves at the tip of new strong shoots at some distance from the male cones. They grow in about 3 years.

Female cone is considered as a modified inflorescence due to the presence of a bract.

Megasporophyll of Female Cone

The megasporophyll of the female cone consists of:

  1. Ovuliferous scale
    • Each ovuliferous scale bears 2 naked ovules towards the basal region on the upper (adaxial) side.
    • Ovules/Megasporangia are unitegmic and anatropous type.
    • Megaspores are formed by meiosis in the megaspore mother cell (MMC).
    • Functional megaspore germinates to form the female gametophyte bearing archegonia (2 to 5 in number).
    • Archegonium consists of 4+4=8 neck cells:
      • 1 Venter Canal Cell (VCC)
      • 1 Egg cell
      • 0 Neck Canal cell (NCC)
  2. Bract scale

Pollination and Fertilization

1. Pollen tube acts as a pollen carrier.

2. Pollen grains are winged (wings develop from exine).

  • Winged Pollen grain = Pinus (Develops from exine)
  • Winged seed = Moringa
  • Winged fruit = Hiptage & Shorea

3. Pollination takes place at the 4-celled stage (2 prothallial cells, 1 generative cell, 1 tube cell).

4. During pollination, the atmosphere becomes yellowish, called the Shower of sulphur.

5. The body cell divides to form 2 passive male gametes.

6. The pollen tube bursts, and the two gametes are liberated. Only one of these fuses with the egg nucleus, the other degenerates.

Male Gamete

The male gamete is non-motile and winged.
