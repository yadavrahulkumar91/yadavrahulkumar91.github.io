Cell Cycle and Division

Reason for Cell Cycle/Cell Division

  • A cell divides in order to maintain balance between the bulk of cytoplasm and nucleus.
  • The ratio of nucleus to the volume of the cytoplasm is called the Nucleocytoplasmic ratio.
Nucleocytoplasmic ratio Equation Explanation
Nucleocytoplasmic ratio \( \frac{V_n}{V_{cyto}} = \frac{V_N}{V_{cell} - V_N} \) In a cell going to divide, nucleocytoplasmic ratio (karyoplasmic ratio) and surface area per unit volume decreases.
  • The increase in nucleocytoplasmic ratio indicates increasing activity of cell division.
  • Smaller cells are metabolically more active than larger cells due to high nucleocytoplasmic ratio.

Cell Cycle

‚Ä¢ Introduction

  • The cell cycle is defined as the sequence of events in which the cell accumulates a large amount of nutrition and then divides into daughter cells.
  • The process of forming daughter cells from the parent cell is called Cell division.
  • Note: Generation time/cell cycle time is the total time taken by the parent cell to divide into daughter cells.
  • The sequence of events that occurs between two cell divisions is called the Cell cycle.
  • Phases of the Cell cycle
  • Sequences of the Cell cycle:
  • \( G_1 \rightarrow S \rightarrow G_2 \rightarrow M \)
  • \( S \rightarrow G_2 \rightarrow M \rightarrow G_1 \)
  • \( G_2 \rightarrow M \rightarrow G_1 \rightarrow S \)
  • \( M \rightarrow G_1 \rightarrow S \rightarrow G_2 \)

  • Special points

Interphase / I-phase/ Non-dividing phase

Introduction

  1. Also known as
    • Resting phase/
      • Though called a resting phase (due to non-dividing nucleus), it is metabolically or physiologically most active phase.
    • Metabolic phase/
    • Growth phase/
    • Preparative phase/
      • Nucleus is largest in size during Interphase and is prepared for division. [IOM 2001]
    • Invisible phase/
      • As replicated chromosomes are not visible at this stage.
  2. Longest phase of the cell cycle.

Transcription occurs throughout interphase but the rate of transcription is highest during G1 phase.

G1 phase/ Gap I/ Growth I/ Post mitotic phase/ Pre-DNA synthesis phase

  1. Preparatory phase of DNA synthesis
  2. Maximum growth occurs in G1 phase.
  3. Synthesis of
    • RNA (t-RNA, m-RNA, r-RNA)
    • Ribosomes
    • Histone protein is started but bulk of histone protein is synthesized during S-phase
    • Non-histone proteins i.e, Structural and functional protein occurs.
    • Enzymes
      • DNA polymerase
      • RNA polymerase
  4. G1 phase is absent in Amoeba, Slime moulds and Yeast.
  5. Decision of cell division occurs in this phase.
    • Non-dividing cell remain in G1 phase.
    • Restriction point/ R-point/ Check point
      • It is present in G1 phase.
      • When a cell has crossed this point or enter into S-phase, division of such cells become compulsion.

Go phase/ Quiescent stage/ Quiet stage

  1. Stage of arrest of cell cycle and onset of differentiation (forming permanent tissue)
  2. Cells of quiescent centre in root tip are in Go stage.
  3. Skeletal muscle cells, Nerve cells, RBC are in Go phase.

S phase/ Synthetic phase/ DNA synthesis phase

  1. Metabolically most active stage.
  2. Synthesis
    • Replication/ synthesis of nuclear DNA and
      • DNA replication occurs with the help of DNA polymerase enzyme
      • DNA polymerase is active.
    • Synthesis of histone protein
  3. Also called "Invisible phase" of mitosis as replicated chromosomes are not visible at this stage.
  4. After this phase a cell can enter into cell division without interruption.
  5. Each chromosome has 2 Chromatids.

G2 phase/ Gap II/ Growth II/ Pre-mitotic phase/ Post-DNA synthesis phase

  1. Also called Resting phase of I-phase.
  2. Replication of Cytoplasmic DNA occurs.
  3. Synthesis
    • Synthesis of required organelles for M-phase.
    • Synthesis of tubulin protein for spindle apparatus.
  4. Energy storage takes place.
  5. Centrioles are duplicated.
  6. During G2, a cell contains Double the amount of DNA (4n) as compared to original diploid cell (2n).
  7. Note:
    • Division of Chloroplast and Mitochondria started during G2 phase and continued up to cytokinesis.
    • In meiotic cell division, G2 phase is very short or almost absent.

b. Mitotic Phase/ M phase/ Dividing phase/ Cell division

Introduction

  1. Shortest phase of the cell cycle.
  2. It includes
    • Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)
    • Cytokinesis (Division of cytoplasm)
      • Cell plate: E.g- Plant
      • Constriction or Furrowing E.g: Animal

3 types of Cell divisions

i. Amitosis

  1. Also called
    • Direct cell division
      • Because division completes without forming spindle fibre.
    • Incipient cell division
    • Primitive cell division
  2. It is unequal cell division.
  3. It is the main method of division of prokaryotic cells but also occurs in some eukaryotic cells like Saccharomyces (yeast).
  4. A karyokinesis in which the nuclear membrane persists throughout the division cycle is called Amitosis.
  5. E.g: Budding, Fission

Introduction

  • Also called

  • In mitosis, a single cell divides into two daughter cells.
  • The cell which undergoes mitosis is called a Mitocyte.
  • No. of mitosis required for the production of n-cells (Daughter cells) from a single cell is (n-1).
  • No. of daughter cells produced after 'n' turns of Division, 2n = given no.

1) Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)

All the 4 stages occur continuously without any stoppage.

a) Prophase

  1. 1st and longest stage of mitosis.
  2. Metabolically most active stage of cell division.
  3. Note: Interphase is metabolically most active stage of Cell cycle.
  4. Chromatin changes to chromosome i.e., chromosome becomes visible.
  5. The chromosomes of prophase are called Prochromosomes.
  6. Shows Spiralisation (Plectonemic coiling)
    • Note: Paranemic coiling is found in Meiotic prophase.
    • Q. In which phase of nuclear division is plectonemic coiling found?
      • Meiotic prophase
      • Mitotic prophase
      • Amitotic division
      • Crossing over
  7. Centromere and Chromatids are not visible.
  8. Cell becomes more viscous.
  9. Formation of spindle fibre is started during late prophase.
    • Spindle fibres are made of 97% tubulin protein and 3% RNA.
  10. Nuclear membrane, nucleoplasm, and nucleolus disappear during late prophase.

b) Metaphase

  1. Chromosomes are thickest with visible centromere and visible chromatids. So, it is the best stage to study chromosomes. (Metaphase II > Metaphase I)
  2. Chromosomes are Shortest in metaphase.
  3. Chromosomes are arranged in a single line at the equator (Metakinesis). So, a single metaphasic plate/ equatorial plate is formed.
  4. Structure, size, and counting of the number of chromosomes are done at this stage.
  5. No. of chromatids in a chromosome at metaphase is 2 in mitosis and 2 in meiosis.
  6. Spindle fibre is well developed.
  7. Spindle fibre of the opposite pole is attached to the kinetochore or primary constriction (of centromere) of the chromosome.
  8. Idiogram/ Karyotype of chromosomes is prepared.

c) Anaphase/ Migratory phase

  1. Shortest stage, inert (less active stage of cell division).
  2. Shape of chromosome can be studied.
  3. The shape of chromosomes in anaphase are V of J or L or I shaped, based on the position of centromere.
  4. Centromere is divided so chromatids are separated.
  5. Sister chromatids move towards opposite poles.
  6. No. of chromatids in a chromosome of anaphase is One.
  7. Poleward movement occurs due to
    • Spindle fibre and
    • Centromere
  8. The main kinetics for poleward is generated by centromere.
    Note: The number of chromosomes remains the same but the number of chromatids is reduced to half in anaphase in comparison to metaphase.
  9. Calcium ion promotes anaphase.

d) Telophase

  1. Also called Re-organisation phase.
  2. It is the reverse of Prophase.
    • Nuclear membrane and Nucleolus reappear.
    • Chromosome becomes decondensed, uncoiled and reaches to opposite poles.
    • Spindle fibres disappear, chromosome reorganizes and the cell resumes its metabolic activity.
  3. It results in the formation of two daughter nuclei with the same number of chromosomes and the same amount of DNA.

2) Cytokinesis (Cytoplasm division)

a) By cell plate: (In Plant)

  1. During cell plate formation, spindle fibres aggregate at the equator basically Golgi body initiates the cell plate formation which later converts into Phragmoplast and Ultimately cell wall.
  2. Plane of the cell plate is determined by Microtubules.
  3. Growth of the cell plate occurs in a Centrifugal manner.

b) Furrowing (In Animal)

  1. Plane of furrowing is determined by Microfilament.
  2. Growth of furrows occurs in a Centripetal manner.

Types of Meiosis

Meiosis is a double division

Meiosis-I

Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)

Prophase-I

  • Longest and most complex phase of meiosis.
  • Important table
  • Leptotene
    • Chromosomes are thread-like and beaded called Chromomere.
    • Bead structure in chromatin is Nucleosome.
    • Bouquet üíê formation occurs, so-called "bouquet stage".
    • Best stage for the observation of chromomere.
  • Zygotene
    • Synapsis occurs.
    • Lengthwise pairing of homologous chromosomes in a very precise manner i.e., chromomere to chromomere is called as Synapsis or Syndesis or Zipping.
    • Synapsis occurs in a zipper-like fashion and may begin at the centromere or at ends of the chromosome or at any point.
    • Synapsis is favored by Synaptonemal complex (protein).
    • Synaptonemal complex is Tripartite.
    • Each pairing of homologous chromosomes is called bivalent which exists in the form of Dyad (2 stranded stage).
    • Thickening and Shortening of chromosome occur.
  • Pachytene
    • Most important sub-stage of Prophase-I.
    • Thickest chromosome.
    • Exchange of genetic material/ chromatid segments/ DNA/ genes (i.e., Crossing over) takes place.
    • Crossing over occurs at 4-stranded stage.
    • Occurrence of single, double, and triple crossing over is in the order.
    • Crossing over is favored by 3 main enzymes.
      • Endonuclease: For breakdown (Nicking)
      • Unwindase: For separation
      • Ligase: For rejoining
    • Equal exchange of segments occurs between non-sister chromatids (homologous chromatids) of homologous chromosome during crossing over.
      Note: Crossing over is the major source of continuous variation in sexually reproducing organisms. But the ultimate source of variation is mutation, which produces discontinuous variation.
    • Synapsis and synaptonemal complex and bivalent become mature.
    • Sister chromatids become visible thus bivalents exist in the form of a tetrad i.e., Tetrad formation occurs in Pachytene.
    • Tetrad contains 2 homologous chromosomes, 4 chromatids, 2 centromeres, and 4 kinetochores.
    • Morphological details of the chromosome are best studied.
  • Diplotene
    • Formation of chiasmata takes place.
    • Synaptonemal complex starts to dissolve except at a few points called Chiasmata (indication of crossing over).
    • Profuse decondensation of lampbrush chromosomes in the female species producing yolk eggs.
      E.g: All vertebrates and some invertebrates.
    • In females, ovum remains in diplotene stage from birth to puberty. So, Diplotene is longest in human female.
  • Diakinesis
    • Chiasmata disappears due to terminalization.
    • Movement of chiasmata occurs from centromere to telomere in a zipper-like fashion which is called terminalization.
    • At the end of diakinesis, each chromosome has two chromatids called dyad.
    • Formation of spindle fibers is started and nuclear membrane, nucleolus, nucleoplasm start to disappear.
    • Note: Terminalisation starts during Diakinesis.
      Terminalization occurs and ends during Diakinesis.
    • Synaptonemal complex (SC)
      • It is a feature of meiotic prophase I.
      • Ubiquitin is the characteristic protein forming SC (Synaptonemal Complex).
      • 1st observed by Moses.
      • Synaptonemal complex appears in Zygotene, persists in pachytene (more clear), and disappears during diplotene.
      • It helps in the proper pairing of homologous chromosomes and in producing recombination.
  • Metaphase-I
    • Paired chromosomes arrange themselves at the equatorial plane (congression).
    • Chromosomes are arranged in two distinct lines (i.e., Double metaphase plate is formed).
    • Appearance of Spindle fibers.
    • Chromosome at Metaphase-I consists of 2 chromatids and 1 centromere.
  • Anaphase-I
    • It is the stage of separation of maternal and paternal chromosomes of the homologous pair also called disjunction and the separated chromosomes are called univalents or dyads.
    • Centromere is not divided.
    • No. of chromosomes is reduced to half but no. of chromatids remains the same in anaphase-I in comparison to metaphase-I.
  • Telophase-I
    • Similar to mitotic telophase.
    • Cytokinesis (Cytoplasm division)

Interkinesis:

  1. It is a brief intrameiotic interphase, but without S-phase (DNA synthesis does not occur).
  2. Time period between meiosis-I and meiosis-II is called Interkinesis.

2) Meiosis-II

Similar to that of mitosis and so called meiotic mitosis.

a) Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)

  • Prophase-II
  • Metaphase-II
  • Anaphase-II
  • Telophase-II

b) Cytokinesis (Cytoplasm division)

  • Successive type:

    Both meiosis I and meiosis II are followed by wall formation and lead to the formation of isobilateral tetrads.

  • Simultaneous type:

    Meiosis I is not followed by wall formation but wall formation occurs simultaneously after meiosis II and leads to the formation of tetrahedral tetrads.

Significance of Meiosis

  • Maintains the number of chromosomes constant in sexually reproducing organisms.
  • Recombination occurs as a result of crossing over which has a role in evolution.
  • Meiosis shows the basic relationship among organisms.
  • Note:
    • No. of cytokinesis in Meiosis = 3 (1 in meiosis I and 2 in meiosis II)
    • No. of spindles in Meiosis = 3 (1 in metaphase I and 2 in metaphase II)
    • No. of chromatids per chromosome is 2 in metaphase of mitosis/meiosis; 1 in anaphase of mitosis and 2 in anaphase I of meiosis I.
