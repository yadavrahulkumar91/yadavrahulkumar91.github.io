Introduction

Molecules (organic/inorganic) present in living organisms is known as Biomolecules.

Collection of biomolecules in the cells is called Cellular pool.

Molecules in Cellular Pool

Cellular pool is crystallocolloid in nature.

Cellular pool contains both Organic (Carbohydrate, Lipid, Protein) and Inorganic (Water, Mineral, Salts, etc) compounds.

Organic Compounds

Carbohydrate

Introduction

Composition of Carbohydrates

  1. Carbohydrates are organic compounds composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms in the ratio $1:2:1$.
  2. It is represented by $ C_n (H_2 O)_n $.

Metabolism of Carbohydrates

Glycolysis
  1. Definition: Glycolysis is the process in which glucose is converted to pyruvate (aerobic condition) or lactate (anaerobic condition), along with production of a small quantity of energy.
  2. Site: Glycolysis occurs in Cytoplasm.
Cori's Cycle/ Lactic Acid Cycle
  1. Definition: Cori's Cycle is a process in which glucose is converted to lactate in the muscle; and in the liver this lactate is re-converted into glucose.
  2. Site: Cori's cycle occcurs in Liver.
Glycogenesis- The Process of Glycogen Synthesis
  1. Definition: The biochemical process of converting glucose molecules into glycogen for storage.
  2. Location: Mainly occurs in the liver and muscles.
Glycogenolysis
  1. Definition: Glycogenolysis is a metabolic process by which glycogen is broken down into individual glucose molecules.
  2. Site: This process occurs primarily in the liver and muscles.
  3. Function: It serves as a mechanism to release glucose into the bloodstream when energy demands are high, such as during periods of physical activity or when blood glucose levels drop.
Glucogenesis/ Gluconeogenesis
  1. Definition: Glucogenesis is the process by which glucose molecules are produced from non-carbohydrate precursors like Proteins and Fats.
  2. Site: It occurs mainly in Liver.

Oligosaccharides

Oligosaccharides are carbohydrates composed of a small number of monosaccharide units linked together. They are more complex than monosaccharides but have fewer units than polysaccharides.

Disaccharide

  1. A disaccharide is an oligosaccharide made up of two monosaccharide units linked through a glycosidic bond. These sugars are often used for quick energy and as transport forms in plants.

  2. Examples of Disaccharides:
    1. Maltose (glucose + glucose)
    2. Sucrose (glucose + fructose)
    3. Lactose (glucose + galactose)
Trisaccharide
  1. A trisaccharide is an oligosaccharide consisting of three monosaccharide units connected by glycosidic bonds. They serve various roles, including energy storage and structural components.
  2. Example of Trisaccharide:
    1. Raffinose (Glucose + Fructose + Galactose)
    2. It is found in beans and other legumes.
Tetrasaccharide
  1. A tetrasaccharide is an oligosaccharide made up of four monosaccharide units joined together. They have diverse functions in biological systems.
  2. Example of Tetrasaccharide:
    1. Stachyose (two galactose + one glucose + one fructose)
    2. It is found in certain seeds.
Polysaccharides

Polysaccharides are complex carbohydrates composed of many monosaccharide units linked together in long chains. They serve various functions in organisms.

Starch

Starch is a complex carbohydrate that serves as a primary source of energy for many organisms. It is commonly found in various plant-based foods and is an essential part of the human diet.

  1. Types of Starch

    2. There are two main types of starch: amylose and amylopectin.

Lipid

Introduction

  1. Lipids are biological molecules insoluble in water, but readily soluble in non-polar organic solvents like chloroform and benzene.
  2. Lipids are not polymers.
  3. They form a heterogeneous group, but all are made of Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen.
  4. The number of oxygen atoms is very small compared to carbon atoms.
  5. True lipids are esters of fatty acids and an alcohol.

Fatty acids:

  1. Fatty acids are large molecules containing an acidic group. The general formula is R-COOH.
  2. There are two types of fatty acids based on bond nature (saturation).
  3. 2 types of fatty acids on the basis of essentiality

Major Categories of Lipids

  1. Simple Lipids

    1. These are esters of fatty acids and alcohol.
    2. They could be Fats, Oils, or Waxes.
    3. Fats
      1. These are esters of fatty acids and glycerol, also known as triglycerides.
    4. Oils
      1. These are fats which are rich in unsaturated fatty acids.
    5. Waxes
      1. These are esters of long chain fatty acids and long chain alcohols other than glycerol.
      2. Waxes are insoluble in water but soluble in benzene, light petroleum, etc.
      3. They act as protective agents against the penetration of moisture.
      4. Candles are made up of Paraffin wax and Higher fatty acids.
      5. Wax can act as a moisture barrier and heat insulator.

  2. Difference between Fats and Oils

  3. Compound/Complex Lipids

    1. When simple lipids combine with another compound, they form a compound lipid.
    2. Examples:
      1. Glycolipid = Carbohydrate + Lipid
      2. Phospholipid = Phosphoric acid + Lipid
      3. Lipoprotein = Protein + Lipid
    3. Phospholipids are commonly present in cellular and intracellular membranes of cells.
    4. Surfactants in lungs that prevent collapse are also phospholipids.

  4. Derived Lipids

    1. These are of various types. The important example is Steroids.
    2. Steroids are composed of fused 5 hydrocarbon rings and a long hydrocarbon chain.
    3. Example:
      1. Cholesterol
      2. Diosgenin (used for making antifertility pills)
      3. Hormones like corticoids, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, etc. are steroids.

Special points

  1. Lipids are not polymers.
  2. All lipids are not fat. For example, Steroids are lipids but not fat.
  3. Hydrolysis of lipid by superheated steam produces glycerol and fatty acid.
  4. Saponification is an alkaline hydrolysis of fat:
    Fat + NaOH → Soap + Glycerol
    1. Soap is the sodium or potassium salt of higher fatty acid.
      1. Sodium salts are hard soap and are used for washing and cleaning.
      2. Potassium salts are used for bathing.
  5. Chylomicrons are lipids.
  6. Rancidification = Spoiling of fats and oils when kept for a long time.

Functions of Lipids:

  1. Energy Storage: Lipids serve as a concentrated and efficient form of energy storage in the body.
  2. Insulation and Protection: Some lipids contribute to insulation and protection of organs by forming adipose tissue.
  3. Cell Membrane Structure: Lipids are a crucial component of cell membranes, providing structural integrity and regulating permeability.
  4. Hormone Production: Certain lipids are involved in the production of hormones that regulate various physiological processes.
  5. Transport of Fat-Soluble Vitamins: Lipids help transport fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K) within the body.
  6. Protection of Nervous System: Myelin, a lipid-rich substance, insulates nerve fibers and enhances nervous system function.

Amino Acids

  1. Amino acids

    1. Introduction

      1. Amino acids are amino substituted acids containing both amino(−NH2) and carboxyl(−COOH) groups.
      2. Amino acids are amphoteric in nature and slightly acidic in pH.
      3. Each amino acid contains a centrally located carbon atom called α-carbon to which 4 groups are attached:
        1. An acidic amino group (−NH3+).
        2. A basic carboxylic group (−COO−).
        3. A hydrogen atom.
        4. A group of varying chemical structures called a side chain (−R).

    2. Zwitter ions

      1. When protons from the carboxyl group are migrated to the amino group, a dipolar ion of the amino acid is formed called Zwitter ions.
        1. A dipolar ion containing both positive and negative charges is formed.
      2. Isoelectric point
        1. The pH at which an amino acid will not migrate into an electric field is known as its Isoelectric point.
        2. At the isoelectric point, the amino acid has no net charge, meaning the amount of negative charge on the amino acid is exactly balanced by the amount of positive charge.
        3. The isoelectric point of each amino acid is constant.
        4. An amino acid has the minimum solubility at its isoelectric point because the concentration of the dipolar ion is maximum.
    3. Amino acids are building blocks of proteins.

Introduction

Denaturation of Proteins:

Definition:

Denaturation of proteins refers to the process in which a protein's secondary, tertiary, and sometimes quaternary structures are disrupted, resulting in the loss of its biological activity.

Causes:

This can be caused by various factors, such as

  1. heat
  2. pH extremes
  3. chemicals
  4. mechanical agitation

Properties:

  • Structural Changes: Denaturation leads to the disruption of the protein's native three-dimensional structure, causing it to unfold.
  • Loss of Function: Denatured proteins often lose their biological function, as their active sites and binding regions become distorted.
  • Reversibility: In some cases, denaturation can be reversible if the disrupting factor is removed. However, in many cases, the damage is permanent.
  • Protein Types: Different proteins respond differently to denaturation; some proteins are more resistant to denaturation than others.

Examples:

  • Cooking Eggs: When you cook an egg, the heat causes the proteins in the egg white to denature. The clear egg white transforms from a liquid to a solid as the proteins unfold and bond together.
  • Curdling of Milk: The process of making cheese involves denaturing milk proteins using acid or enzymes. The denatured proteins then aggregate and form curds.
  • Heat-Shocked Proteins: Heat shock proteins (HSPs) are produced by cells in response to stressors like high temperatures. HSPs assist in refolding denatured proteins to their functional states.
  • Denaturation in Digestion: In the stomach, proteins denature due to the low pH caused by stomach acid. This denaturation exposes peptide bonds, making proteins more susceptible to enzymatic digestion.

Structure of Protein:

Primary Structure:

  • Refers to the linear sequence of amino acids in a protein.
  • Amino acids are linked by peptide bonds.
  • Sequence dictates protein's unique characteristics and function.

Secondary Structure:

  • Results from hydrogen bonding within the polypeptide backbone.
  • Common secondary structures include alpha helices and beta sheets.

Tertiary Structure:

  • Three-dimensional folding of the entire polypeptide chain.
  • Stabilized by various interactions like
    1. hydrogen bonds
    2. Van der waal's interactions
    3. Disulfide bonds
    4. Electrostatic bonds
    5. Hydrophobic interactions
  • Determines protein's final functional shape and active sites.

Quaternary Structure:

  • Applies to proteins composed of multiple polypeptide chains (subunits).
  • Describes the arrangement of these subunits and their interactions.
  • Forms functional complexes with diverse functions.

Functions of Proteins:

  1. Enzymatic Activity: Proteins act as enzymes, facilitating biochemical reactions in the body.
  2. Structural Support: Some proteins provide structural support to cells, tissues, and organs.
  3. Transport: Proteins are involved in transporting molecules such as oxygen, nutrients, and waste products.
  4. Hormone Regulation: Certain proteins function as hormones, regulating various physiological processes.
  5. Immune Defense: Antibodies are specialized proteins that play a key role in the immune system's defense against pathogens.
  6. Cell Signaling: Proteins are essential for transmitting signals within and between cells.
  7. Muscle Contraction: Muscle proteins, such as actin and myosin, enable muscle fibers to contract.
  8. Storage: Proteins can serve as a source of amino acids for energy when needed.

Biochemical Methods of Testing Proteins

Biuret's Test:

  • Utilizes a chemical reaction to detect protein presence.
  • Relies on peptide bonds reacting with copper ions in an alkaline solution.
  • Forms a violet-colored complex upon reaction, indicating protein presence.

Millon's Reaction:

  • Detects phenolic compounds, including specific tyrosine residues.
  • Involves treating the sample with Millon's reagent (mercuric nitrate in nitric acid).
  • Forms a red solution upon reaction with phenolic compounds, confirming protein presence with tyrosine residues.

Ninhydrin Test:

  • Detects amino acids, peptides, and proteins with free amino groups.
  • Treatment with Ninhydrin reagent and heating leads to a purple or blue color.
  • Color change indicates the presence of compounds with free amino groups.

Xanthoproteic Test:

  • Specifically detects aromatic amino acids like tyrosine and phenylalanine.
  • Requires treatment with concentrated nitric acid.
  • Results in a yellow color initially, turning orange upon heating if aromatic amino acids are present.

Sakaguchi Test:

  • Specifically tests for arginine presence in proteins.
  • Includes adding sodium hypobromite to the protein sample.
  • A red-colored complex forms if arginine is present in the sample.

Molisch's Test:

  • Originally for carbohydrate detection, can also indicate glycoprotein presence.
  • Treatment with α-naphthol and sulfuric acid leads to a violet ring at liquid junction.
  • Violet ring formation suggests the presence of carbohydrates and potentially glycoproteins.

Vitamins

Functions of Vitamins:

  1. Antioxidant Protection: Some vitamins, such as vitamin C and vitamin E, act as antioxidants, protecting cells from damage.
  2. Metabolic Reactions: Vitamins are essential co-factors for various enzymatic reactions in the body's metabolism.
  3. Immune System Support: Certain vitamins, like vitamin D, play a role in supporting the immune system's functioning.
  4. Bone Health: Vitamins such as vitamin D and vitamin K are important for maintaining healthy bones.
  5. Blood Clotting: Vitamin K is necessary for proper blood clotting and wound healing.
  6. Vision: Vitamin A is crucial for maintaining good vision and eye health.
  7. Cell Growth and Division: Vitamins like vitamin B12 and folic acid are involved in cell growth and DNA synthesis.
  8. Nervous System Function: Some vitamins, such as the B vitamins, are important for the proper functioning of the nervous system.

Inorganic Compounds

Water

Functions of Water:

  1. Hydration: Water is essential for maintaining proper hydration levels in the body.
  2. Temperature Regulation: Water helps regulate body temperature through processes like sweating and evaporation.
  3. Transport Medium: Blood, which consists largely of water, transports nutrients, gases, and waste products throughout the body.
  4. Metabolic Reactions: Many biochemical reactions in the body occur in a water-based environment.
  5. Cellular Function: Water is a key component of cells, enabling various cellular processes.
  6. Waste Elimination: Water is necessary for excreting waste products through urine and sweat.
  7. Lubrication: Water serves as a lubricant for joints, organs, and tissues.
  8. Electrolyte Balance: Water helps maintain the balance of electrolytes in the body's fluids.

Mineral

Functions of Minerals:

  1. Bone Health: Minerals like calcium and phosphorus are essential for maintaining strong and healthy bones.
  2. Electrolyte Balance: Minerals such as sodium, potassium, and chloride help maintain proper fluid and electrolyte balance in the body.
  3. Nerve Function: Minerals play a crucial role in transmitting nerve impulses and supporting nervous system function.
  4. Fluid Regulation: Minerals help regulate the movement of fluids in and out of cells and blood vessels.
  5. Cellular Energy Production: Some minerals are co-factors in energy-producing reactions within cells.
  6. Hemoglobin Formation: Minerals like iron are essential for the formation of hemoglobin, which carries oxygen in the blood.
  7. Immune System Support: Minerals like zinc and selenium are involved in immune system functioning.
  8. Enzyme Activation: Many enzymes require specific minerals as co-factors to carry out their functions.

Salts

Functions of Salts:

  1. Electrolyte Balance: Salts, such as sodium and potassium, are vital for maintaining proper fluid and electrolyte balance in the body.
  2. Nerve Signaling: Salts play a crucial role in transmitting nerve signals and facilitating communication between cells.
  3. Muscle Contraction: Salts are necessary for muscle fibers to contract and function properly.
  4. Fluid Retention and Blood Pressure: Salts, particularly sodium, can influence fluid retention and blood pressure regulation.
  5. Acid-Base Balance: Salts help regulate the body's pH levels and maintain acid-base balance.
  6. Enzyme Activation: Some salts are involved as co-factors in enzymatic reactions that are essential for various processes.
  7. Bone Health: Certain salts, like calcium phosphate, contribute to the formation and maintenance of healthy bones.
  8. Digestive Processes: Salts aid in digestion by facilitating the breakdown of food particles and nutrient absorption.
