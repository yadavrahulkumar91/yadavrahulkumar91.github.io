Absorption of Water

  1. Movement of water from soil to xylem of roots
  2. Involves
    1. Imbibition
    2. Osmosis
    3. Diffusion
    4. Capillary action
  3. Water is absorbed by plants through the root hair zone (maturation zone).
  4. Root cap has no role in water absorption because it has no root hair.
  5. Water will be absorbed by root hairs when the external medium is hypotonic.
  6. Root hairs are exogenous, unicellular, arise from epiblema (epidermis).
  7. Lateral roots are endogenous, multicellular, arise from pericycle.
  8. Epidermis with root hairs → Piliferous region
  9. Main source of H2O → Rainwater
  10. Most easily available soil water → Capillary water
  11. Hardly available soil water → Gravitational water
  12. Completely unavailable soil water is hygroscopic water.
    • Total water content in soil = Holard
    • Water available to plants = Chesard
    • Water unavailable to plants = Echard
  13. Special points
    1. Root hairs act as an osmotic system for water absorption.
    2. Root hairs are more developed in herbs compared to shrubs and trees.
    3. Root hairs are more developed in angiosperms than gymnosperms due to the presence of mycorrhiza in gymnosperms.
    4. Root hairs have a permeable cell wall, with an outer layer of pectin substance (for attachment of soil particles) and an inner layer of cellulose.
  14. Absorption of water in certain plants
    1. Thallophytes (Algae and Fungi) = General body surface
    2. Hydrophytes = General body surface
    3. Lichens = Rhizines
    4. Marchantia = Unicellular Rhizoids
    5. Moss/ Funaria = Multicellular Rhizoids
    6. Gametophytic body of Fern (Prothallus of Fern) = Unicellular Rhizoids
    7. Sporophytic body of Ferns = Fibrous root/ Adventitious root
    8. Pinus = Mycorrhizal roots
    9. Higher plants = Root hairs
    10. Parasitic plants = Haustorial roots

    Root hairs absent in Mycorrhizic, parasitic, and hydrophytic roots.

  15. Pathway of water absorption in higher plants
    Soil → Root hair → Cortex → Endodermis (Passage cell) → Pericycle → Protoxylem → Metaxylem

Absorption of Minerals

  1. Mineral absorption is independent of water absorption.
  2. Mostly occurs through the zone of elongation (not by the Root hair zone).
  3. Mineral salt absorption occurs directly by cells of epiblema and not by root hair.
  4. Absorption of minerals occurs in the form of ions (cations or anions) present in the soil solution.
  5. First step in mineral absorption → Ion exchange.

Passive vs Active Absorption Mechanisms

i. Passive Absorption Active Absorption
Occurs according to concentration gradient without using ATP. Occurs against concentration gradient by using ATP.
Spontaneous process and proceeds towards equilibrium. Not a spontaneous process.
1. Donnan's equilibrium:
  • Entry of anions and cations into the cells across the cell membrane to maintain electrochemical equilibrium is known as Donnan's equilibrium.
  • If the root surface contains H^+ ions, anions from the soil solution are absorbed and vice-versa to maintain electrochemical balance.
 1. Carrier concept:
  • Given by Van Den Honert.
  • Occurs with the help of Carrier molecule by forming carrier-ion complexes.
2. Ion-exchange:
  • Given by Jenny and Over Street.
  • Exchange of anion and cation absorbed to the root surface of soil solution with similarly charged ions.
  • Ion exchange occurs when oscillation volume overlaps.
  • 2 types:
    • Contact exchange
    • Carbonic acid exchange
 2. Landegardh's cytochrome pump theory:
  • Anions are actively absorbed with the help of cytochrome.
  • Cations are passively absorbed to maintain electrochemical balance.
3. Mass flow or Bulk flow:
  • Given by Kramer.
  • Absorption of minerals occurs along with mass flow absorption of H_2 O under the influence of transpiration pull.
  1. Factors affecting mineral salt absorption
    1. Influence of temperature:
      1. Temperature increase boosts mineral salt absorption, except at very high temperatures due to protein denaturation.
    2. pH effect:
      1. Lower pH increases anion absorption; higher pH increases cation absorption.
    3. Oxygen (O2) impact:
      1. Less O2 reduces active mineral salt absorption.
    4. Ageing and roots:
      1. As roots age, salt absorption decreases due to suberization.
  2. Special point: Mineral salt transport occurs through the Xylem.

Ascent of Sap

  1. Upward movement of H2O with dissolved minerals through the lumen of xylem vessels against the force of gravity is called Ascent of Sap.
  2. Path of ascent of sap in gymnosperm is Tracheids.
  3. Best experiment to show the path of ascent of sap is the Ringing or Girdling experiment.
    1. It is the removal of bark in the form of a complete ring.
    2. This experiment is not applicable in plants having Bicollateral, Amphivasal, and Scattered vascular bundles.
  4. Theories of Ascent of Sap
    1. Vital theories
      1. Relay pump theory:
        1. Through Xylem parenchyma.
        2. Given by Godlewiski.
      2. Pulsation theory:
        1. Through innermost cortical cells.
        2. Given by J.C. Bose.

        Strasburger discarded vital theories.
        Living cells are not responsible for the ascent of sap by Strasburger.

    2. Root pressure theory:
      1. Given by Priestly.
      2. A sort of hydrostatic pressure develops in the root due to the accumulation of absorbed water which is responsible for the ascent of sap.
    3. Physical force theory:
      1. Atmospheric pressure theory:
        1. Given by Boehm.
        2. Atmospheric pressure raises up the water to fill the vacuum created by continuous evaporation of water from the plant surfaces.
      2. Imbibition theory
        1. Proposed by Unger and supported by Sachs.
        2. Upward movement of water takes place by imbibition through the thick walls of the Xylem cells and Sclerenchyma cells of the Phloem.
      3. Transpiration pull/ Cohesion tension theory:
        1. Most convincing theory OR, Most widely accepted theory.
        2. Proposed by Dixon and Jolly.
        3. It operates only in passive water absorption.
        4. Based on cohesive and adhesive properties of water.
        5. Transpiration is necessary for the ascent of sap.

Transpiration

  1. Transpiration is the loss of water vapor (i.e., in pure form) from the aerial parts of plants.
  2. Transpiration occurs when the outer atmosphere has less moisture than substomatal cavities.
  3. The conditions under which transpiration would be most rapid are low humidity, high temperature, guard cell turgid (open), moist soil.
  4. Transpiration increases in hot, dry, and windy conditions.
  5. Transpiration is reduced due to the deposition of cutin.
  6. Rate of transpiration is reduced when there is a decrease in light intensity.
  7. Loss of water occurs in pure form (i.e., water not mixed with minerals).
  8. Transpiration is necessary for the plant for absorption of H2O and minerals, ascent of sap, and maintaining body temperature (cooling effect).
  9. Transpiration is a "Controlled phenomenon".
  10. Transpiration doesn't occur from roots.
  11. "Transpiration is a necessary evil" by Curtis.
  12. In herbaceous plants, 99% of total water absorbed by plants is lost through transpiration. So, only 1-2% is actually used by them. [IOM 2000]
  13. Transpiration mostly occurs through 3 sites:
    1. Stomata:
      1. Maximum (90% of total transpiration).
    2. Cuticle:
      1. About 9% of total transpiration.
      2. Transpiration through the general body surface.
    3. Lenticel:
      1. Very less, about 1%.
      2. Present in the bark of woody trees.
  14. Transpiration rate of a dorsiventral leaf is higher on the lower surface due to a higher number of stomata. [BPKIHS]
  15. Transpiration from the 2 leaf surfaces is compared by dry paper strips previously soaked in a solution of cobalt chloride. i.e., cobalt chloride test is done to observe unequal transpiration in a dorsiventral leaf.
    1. Change in the color of cobalt chloride paper from blue to pink [Hydrated - pink; dry - blue] indicates that water vapor evaporated from both the surfaces of the leaf. But the quick change in the color of CoCl2 paper at the lower surface indicates a higher rate of transpiration from the lower than that of the upper surface.
    2. Dry CoCl2→Blue
    3. Moist CoCl2→Pink
  16. Bell jar experiment shows the loss of water vapor from the aerial parts of a plant by transpiration.
  17. Rate of transpiration is measured by Ganong's potometer.
  18. The opening and closing of the stomatal pore regulate the process of stomatal transpiration which is due to the change in turgor pressure of the guard cells.
  19. Transpiration through leaves: Foliar transpiration.
    Transpiration through the corky covering of the stem: Bark transpiration.

Comparison: Transpiration vs Evaporation

i. Transpiration Evaporation
It is a physiological process. Physical process.
Only from living cells. From both living and non-living surfaces.
Through epidermis and especially through stomata. From free surfaces.
Forces like V.P; T.P; O.P; are involved. No such forces are involved.
Responsible for preventing the surface of the plant (leaves) from drying or it keeps the surface wet. Leads to dryness of the surface.
Regulates body temperature of plants. Doesn’t regulate temperature.
  1. Factors affecting transpiration:
    1. Plant factors or Internal factors:
      1. Root/shoot ratio: Higher ratio leads to increased transpiration rate.
      2. Leaf area:
        1. More leaf area generally results in higher transpiration; xerophytes have reduced leaf surfaces transformed into spines.
        2. Smaller plants transpire more per unit area compared to larger plants.
        3. Removing leaves increases transpiration per unit area.
      3. Cuticle:
        1. Thinner cuticles lead to greater cuticular transpiration; xerophytes have thick cuticles to reduce transpiration.
      4. Stomata:
        1. More stomata per unit area usually result in greater transpiration; xerophytes with sunken stomata reduce transpiration rate.
    2. External or Environmental factors:
      1. Light:
        1. Light plays a key role in stomatal opening and closing.
        2. Stomata usually open during the day and close in darkness.
        3. Stomata open most in red light (660 nm), followed by blue light (445 nm); no opening in green, UV, and far-red light.
      2. Temperature:
        1. Transpiration rate increases with temperature up to 35°C.
      3. Relative humidity (R.H.):
        1. Transpiration rate is inversely proportional to relative humidity.
        2. Low air humidity increases transpiration rate.
      4. Wind:
        1. Gentle wind increases transpiration by removing saturated air; violent wind decreases transpiration.
      5. Soil condition:
        1. Transpiration rate depends on absorption, so soil condition affects it.
  2. Special point: Rate of transpiration is highest in matured leaves.
  1. Anti-transpirants
    1. Phenyl mercuric acetate (PMA)
    2. Abscisic acid (ABA), Salicylic acid, Aspirin
    3. Waxy substances like silicon emulsion
    4. High concentration of CO2
  2. Significance of Transpiration
    1. Only 1-2% of absorbed water is used by plants. Transpiration helps in the removal of excess water.
    2. Transpiration maintains the temperature of the plant body.
    3. Transpiration creates a suction force on the leaf surface which aids in the ascent of sap.
    4. The ash and sugar content of the fruit increases with an increase in transpiration.
  3. Disadvantages of Transpiration
    1. High transpiration rates cause wilting of plants.
    2. Transpiration reduction leads to decreased photosynthesis and halted growth.
    3. High transpiration rates inhibit flower, fruit, and seed production.

Guttation

  1. Due to root pressure.
  2. Guttation is the loss of water (in the form of liquid droplets) from the margin and tip (but mainly the margin) of leaves.
  3. The lost water drop is impure as it may contain dissolved minerals.
  4. In guttation, plants can get rid of excess water.
  5. Guttation occurs through hydathodes (water stomata).
  6. Hydathodes are found in the margin and tip of the leaves (but mainly the margin).
    • Loosely arranged mass of parenchyma in hydathode is called Epithem.

    Note: Hydathodes remain always open.

  7. Guttation occurs during early morning or night when the rate of transpiration is low and absorption is high. This ultimately increases root pressure. So, guttation is not due to the activity of hydathodes but due to root pressure.
  8. Water drops present on leaf margins of Tropaeolum, Balsam, and Grasses in the early morning are due to Guttation.
  9. Occurs in a moist environment in mesophytic herbs like tomato, balsam, cucurbita, Colocassia, etc.
  10. Guttation is an uncontrolled phenomenon.

Comparison: Transpiration vs Guttation

11. Transpiration Guttation
Loss of water is in vapour form. Loss is in liquid form.
It occurs during the day time. During the night or early morning.
Pure water (no minerals). Impure water (contains minerals).
Through stomata or epidermis or cuticle or lenticels. Through hydathodes.
Controlled phenomenon. Uncontrolled phenomenon.

Exudation/ Bleeding

  1. Exudation is the loss of water sap from an incision of plant body parts.
  2. Exudation of water from leaf incision: Bleeding
  3. Exudation of water from leaf margin: Guttation

Wilting

  1. Loosening of the plant part.
  2. Wilting occurs when transpiration > Water absorption, due to loss of turgidity.
  3. It occurs when xylem is blocked or xylem is removed.
  4. Percentage of water left in the soil when the plant wilts is known as the permanent wilting coefficient (PWC)/ Permanent wilting percentage (PWP).
  5. During wilting, the sequence of events shall be:
    • Exosmosis
    • Plasmolysis
    • Temporary wilting
    • Permanent wilting
  6. Three types of wilting:
    • Incipient wilting:
      • Not visible due to partial loss of turgidity.
    • Temporary wilting:
      • It happens during hot days when the rate of transpiration is high and the rate of absorption is less, and is recovered at night.
      • It is a reversible process.
    • Permanent wilting:
      • Plants fail to recover/return to the original state.
      • Xylem vessels are blocked.
      • It is an irreversible process.
  7. Note: During wilting, there is wrinkling of the cell wall because during wilting there is only loss of water with no replacement.

A. Stomata

  1. a. Overview

    1. They are minute pores found on the epidermis.
    2. Number of stomata per cm2 of a common leaf is about 1000.

  2. b. Stomata consists of

    1. i. Guard cells:

      1. Kidney shaped → Dicot
      2. Dumb-bell shaped → Monocot
      3. The thickest wall among inner, outer, and lateral walls of guard cells is the inner wall.

    2. ii. Subsidiary cells:

      Surrounding cells that support the movement of guard cells.

  3. c. Types of Stomata

    1. i. Based on distribution

      1. Apple or Mulberry type:

        2. Stomata only on lower/dorsal surface. Eg: Trees

      2. Potato type:

        3. Stomata more on lower surface than upper surface. Most common. Eg: Dorsiventral leaf.

      3. Oat type:

        4. Stomata equally distributed on both surfaces. Eg: Isobilateral leaf, grasses.

      4. Water lily type:

        5. Stomata only on upper surface. Eg: Free-floating hydrophytes.

      5. Potamogeton type:

        6. Stomata absent or vestigial. Eg: Submerged hydrophytes.

    2. ii. Based on light

      1. Photoactive:

        2. Stomata opens during daytime. Eg: Usual plants.

      2. Scotoactive:

        3. Stomata opens during nighttime. Eg: Succulent xerophytes (CAM plants) i.e., Opuntia, Bryophyllum, etc.

  1. d. Opening and Closing of Stomata

    1. i. Stomata Open

      Guard cell becomes turgid.

      Note: Stomata open when guard cells show an increase in both osmotic and turgor pressures.

    2. ii. Stomata Close

      Guard cell becomes flaccid.

    3. iii. Theories

      1. a. Starch-Sugar Hypothesis

        1. Proposed by Lloyd and elaborated by Sayre and Steward.

        2. Accordingly, stomatal opening and closing mainly depend on the activity of enzyme Starch phosphorylase in different pH.

        3. Starch (Stomata close) (⇄┴(pH High)┬(pH low) Sugar (Stomata open)

        4. Conversion of starch to organic acids is required for stomatal opening.

      2. b. Potassium Malate Theory/ Active K+ Exchange Mechanism

        1. Most accepted theory on the mechanism of opening and closing of stomata.
        2. Given by Fujino and later modified by Levitt.
        3. Opening of stomata during daytime:
          ■(█(Daytime (Light)@↓@ CO2 conc↑@↓@pH↑)@↓@█(Conversion of insoluble starch into soluble sugar@↓@Influx of K+ into guard cell@↓@ Osmotic pressure↑@↓@Endosmosis@↓@Turgid guard cell@↓@Opening of stomata))
        4. Closing of stomata during nighttime:
          █(Nighttime (Dark)@↓@CO2 conc↓@↓@pH↓@↓@Conversion of soluble sugar into insoluble starch@↓@Eflux of K+ from guard cell@█(↓@Osmotic pressure ↓@↓@Exosmosis@↓@Flaccid guard cell@↓@Closing of stomata))

        5. Special points:

          • Influx of K+ into guard cell → Stomata opens
          • Outflux of K+ from guard cell → Stomata closes
          • Opening of stomata is an active process, but closing of stomata is a passive process.
          • Transpiration in old stems and fruits occurs through lenticels.

    4. Plants growing in high altitudes show xeromorphic adaptations (adaptation of minimum transpiration).
