1. Living plants have the characteristic of showing some changes in their position due to changes in the environment or due to some endogenous causes. Such changes are called movements.
  2. These movements are due to the sensitivity or irritability of protoplasm.
  3. Stimulus may be defined as any change in the external environment which produces a reaction (response) in the plant or plant organ.
    1. The minimum stimulus intensity required to induce a response is known as the threshold intensity.
    2. According to the nature of the stimulus, the movement may be:
      • i. Autonomous (spontaneous), due to internal stimuli, or
      • ii. Paratonic (induced), due to external stimuli like light, temperature, and chemicals.
        • Stimuli and Their Prefixes
        Stimuli Prefix
        Light Photo
        Temperature Thermo
        Water Hydro
        Chemical Chemo

  4. The visible change or reaction of a plant or plant organ as a result of stimuli is called a response.
  5. The interval between the presentation of the stimulus and the actual development of the response is known as reaction time or presentation time.
  6. Movement is not confined to the organism as a whole but occurs down to the cellular level. For example, the cytoplasm of living cells constantly flows like a river in the cells; this streaming motion is called cyclosis.
  7. Hygroscopic movement:
    • Found in bryophytes and pteridophytes.
    • For example, twisting movement of elater, sporangial wall of fern.

Autonomic:

Hyponasty:

  • More growth of lower surface (abaxial).
  • Plants part moves upward.
  • Eg:
    • Closed flower's petal
    • Young leaf of fern (circinately coiled leaf).

Epinasty:

  • More growth on the upper surface (adaxial).
  • Plants part moves downward.
  • Eg:
    • Opening of flower is due to epinasty on the leaf
    • Erect leaf of fern (matured leaf/ frond)
  • Note: Hyponasty and Epinasty are common in bifacial structures like leaf, sepal, and petal.

Nutational movement:

Alternate growth on either surface.

  • Eg:
    • Zig-zag growth of shoot apex,
    • Forward growth of branches

    I. Autonomic/ Self movement

    Amoeboid:

    • Movement due to pseudopodia-like structure.
    • Eg: Synchytrium (Slime mould)

    Ciliary:

    • Movement due to cilia, flagella.
    • Eg: Chlamydomonas, Volvox

    Cyclosis/ Streaming movement:

    • Protoplasmic streaming movements are referred to as autonomic movements of locomotion.
    • Chloroplasts moving by cytoplasmic streaming in the cells of the aquatic plant Elodea
      1. Rotation
        • Movement either in clockwise or anticlockwise direction i.e., movement in a single direction.
        • Eg: Leaf of Hydrilla and Vallisneria
      2. Circular
        • Movement both in clockwise and anticlockwise direction i.e., movement in all possible directions.
        • Eg: Staminal hairs of Tradescantia

    II. Paratonic/ Induced movement:

    Also called Tactic/ Taxis movement.

    Phototactic:

    • Stimulus: Light
    • Eg: Zoospores of Chlamydomonas and Volvox.
    • Moves from dark to diffused light.
    • Also moves from intense to diffused light (or vice versa).

    Thermotactic:

    • Stimulus: Temperature
    • Moves from cold water to warm water and vice-versa.
    • Eg: Chlamydomonas and bacteria

    Chemotactic:

    • Stimulus: Chemicals (Malic acid)
    • Eg: Movement of antherozoid from antheridium to archegonium in Bryophytes and Pteridophytes.

    Rheotactic:

    • Stimulus: Water current
    • Eg: Aquatic alga
