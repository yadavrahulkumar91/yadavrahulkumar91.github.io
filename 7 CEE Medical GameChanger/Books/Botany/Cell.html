Definition The study of cells, their structure, composition, and functions. A broader field that encompasses the study of cells as well as their physiological properties, interactions, and processes within the context of a living organism.

Cell Theory and Its Aspects

Cell Theory

  1. Cell theory was proposed by Schwann (German Zoologist) and Schleiden (German Botanist).
    • Note: 1st idea of cell theory was given by Dutrochet by boiling the leaf of Mimosa pudica in nitric acid and separating the cell.
  2. It states that all living beings consist of cells and cells are the smallest structural and functional unit of life.
  3. Cell theory was extended by Rudolf Virchow.
    • Rudolf Virchow proposed "Law of cell lineage" stated "omnis cellula e cellula," meaning all cells originate from pre-existing cells only by reproduction.
    • Cell principle/Cell doctrine/Modern or modified cell theory = Cell theory + Cell lineage.

Exceptions of cell theory:

  • Virus = Obligate parasites, Sub-cellular in nature (Major exception)
  • Vaucheria (Algae) = Multinucleated
  • Rhizopus, Mucor (Fungi) = Multinucleated
  • Paramecium (Protista) = Binucleate/multinucleated
  • Opalina (Protist) = Multinucleated

Cell Size

The Normal Cell Size

The normal cell size is 0.2 Œºm to 20 Œºm.

Unit of Measurement

The unit of measurement in micron (Œºm) in light microscope and is angstrom (√Ö) in electron microscope.

Factors Affecting Cell Size

  1. \( \text{Cell size} \propto \text{Chromosome number} \)
  2. \( \text{Cell size} \propto \text{Size of Chromosome} \)
  3. \( \text{Cell size} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Metabolic activity}} \) \( (\because \text{Smaller cells are metabolically active}) \)
  4. \( \text{Cell size} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Nucleocytoplasmic ratio}} \) \( (\because \text{Nucleocytoplasmic ratio} \propto \text{Metabolic activity}) \)
  5. \( \text{Cell size} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Surface area of the cell per unit volume}} \) \( (\because \text{Smaller cells have greater surface area per unit volume}) \)

Cell Dividing Capacity

Cellular Totipotency

Cellular totipotency (Latin: Totus = all; potens = power)

  1. Capacity of individual or single, nucleated, living vegetative plant cell to regenerate whole plant.
  2. Idea of cellular totipotency was firstly given by Haberlandt.
  3. Phenomenon of cellular totipotency was proved experimentally by Steward and his co-worker.
  4. He used phloem tissue from roots of carrot as an explant.
  5. Used coconut milk in his experiment as liquid media.
    1. Coconut ü•• milk naturally contains cytokinin which induces cell division.
    2. So, cytokinin (zeatin) is also called coconut milk factor.
    3. Zeatin/ Cytokinin/ Kinetin is also rich in immature maize grain.

Pluripotency

  1. Pluripotency is the potential ability of an animal cell to develop into any type of cell in the animal body but not into the whole animal.
  2. Stem cells are only totipotent in animals.

Cell Organization

Cell Wall Structure and Components

Cell Wall Overview

  • Common in plants; also in bacteria and cyanobacteria.
  • It is non-living.
  • Exceptions in plant cells without a cell wall include:
    1. Mycoplasma
    2. Gametes
    3. Zoospores
    4. Slime moulds (Myxomycetes)

Constituents of Cell Wall

  1. Plant cell wall is made up of:
    • Cellulose:
      • Œ≤-D glucose
    • Hemicellulose:
      • Arabinose
      • Mannose
      • Xylose
      • Galactose, etc.
    • Pectin:
      • Galactose
      • Galacturonic acid
      • Arabinose
  2. Cellulose is the most abundant carbohydrate of the Biosphere.
  3. Cotton contains the highest amount of Cellulose (90%) in the biosphere.
  4. Structural units of Cell wall:
    • Micelle
    • Microfibril
    • Macrofibril
  5. In bacteria and cyanobacteria, cell wall is made up of:
    • Murin
    • Mucopeptide
    • Peptidoglycan:
      • Polysaccharide part of peptidoglycan is the polymer of N-acetyl glucosamine (NAG) and N-acetyl muramic acid (NAM).
  6. In fungi, cell wall is made up of Chitin (fungal cellulose):
    • Chitin (\(C_{22}H_{54}N_{4}O_{21}\))n is a polymer of N-acetyl glucosamine (NAG).
    • NAG is an amino sugar.

Properties of Cell Wall

  • Cell wall is:
    1. Porous
    2. Permeable
    3. Inert
    4. Hydrophilic
    5. Semitransparent
    6. Protective covering secreted by Cytoplasm

Formation of Cell Wall

  • Cell wall is formed during cytokinesis from vesicles of ER and Golgi bodies.

Layers of Cell Wall

  • In higher plants, cell walls consist of different layers:

Middle lamellae:

  • Outermost layer of cell wall.
  • Layer between cell walls of two cells is Middle lamellae.
  • Cementing layer that joins 2 adjacent plant cells together.
  • Contains cementing material Ca-pectate and Mg-pectate (Mainly Ca-pectate).
  • Retting of fibers and ripened fruit becomes soft due to dissolution of middle lamellae (dissolution of Ca-pectate) and dissolution is brought by Pectinase enzyme.
  • Grows by Intussusceptions or Apposition.

Primary cell wall:

  • Elastic in nature
  • Unipartite (Single layer)
  • Found universally in all cells having cell wall.
  • Lies inner to middle lamella.
  • Made up of Cellulose (Œ≤-D glucose unit), Hemicellulose, and Pectin roughly in equal amount.
  • Lignin is never found in the primary cell wall but may have Silica, Wax, Cutin, etc.
  • Common in Parenchyma and Meristematic cells.
  • Grows by Intussusception.

Secondary cell wall:

  • Thick
  • Inelastic in nature
  • Present inner to the primary cell wall.
  • Tripartite: S1, S2 (thickest layer), and S3.
  • Developed by Apposition.
  • Pits are common.
  • Provides mechanical strength, stiffness, and hardness to the cell.
  • Made up of Cellulose (high), sometimes hemicellulose, and additional components like Cutin, Suberin, Pectin, Lignin, Wax, Silica, Mucilage, etc.

Tertiary cell wall:

  • Sometimes S3 of secondary wall is called tertiary wall when it contains Xylon (Polymer of D-xylose) and Mannon.
  • Thick and warty in appearance.
  • Found in Tracheids of gymnosperms.

Plastids and Their Types

Plastid Overview

  • Plastids were discovered and the term was given by Haeckel.
  • Mainly found in plants and certain protists, absent in fungi.
  • Involved in the formation and storage of carbohydrates.
  • Usually three types based on pigments.

Leucoplast

  • Largest plastid.
  • Usually found in underground parts.
  • Function: Storage of reserve food material.
  • Types based on nature of stored food: Amyloplast (starch), Proteinoplast (protein), Elaioplast (oil/fat).

Chromoplast

  • Second largest plastid.
  • Colored plastid (other than green).
  • Contain fat-soluble carotenoids for coloration in fruits, seeds, and flowers.
  • Formed from Chloroplasts (chlorophyll replaced by other colored pigments) or Leucoplasts (development of colored pigments).
  • Other colored plastids include Phaeoplast (brown) and Rhodoplast (red).

Chloroplast

  • Discovered by Sachs, named chloroplasts by Schimper.
  • Green plastid.
  • Function: Formation of carbohydrate and temporary starch storage.
  • Also called Kitchen of cell, Photosynthetic apparatus, Autoplasts.
  • Common in all photosynthetic organisms except photosynthetic bacteria and cyanobacteria.
  • Shape varies among different organisms.
    1. Shape generally Discoid/ Oval/ Tablet üíä shaped or Biconvex.
    2. Cup ü•§ shaped = Chlamydomonas
    3. Spherical shaped = Chlorella
    4. Spiral/ Ribbon shape/ Scalariform = Spirogyra
    5. Girdle/ Ring/ Collar/ Horse üêé shoe shaped = Ulothrix
    6. Reticulate/ Net like = Oedogonium
    7. Stellate or Star shaped = Zygnema (Double star)
    8. Discoid shaped = Vaucheria, Chara
  • No. of chloroplasts varies; maximum in mesophyll cells of leaves.
    1. Single chloroplast = Chlamydomonas and Chlorella
    2. Single chloroplast in each cell = Ulothrix
    3. Double chloroplast in each cell = Zygnema
    4. No. of chloroplasts is maximum in mesophyll cells of leaf.
  • Chloroplast structure includes Grana (site of light reaction) and Stroma (ground substance).

    • Grana

    1. Grana is the site for Light reaction.
      1. Note:
      2. Agranal chloroplast is common in algae and bundle sheath chloroplast of C4 plants.
      3. Agranal chloroplast in which thylakoids are not stacked is called Lamellar chloroplast.
      4. Thylakoids
      5. Thylakoids (baggy trousers) are the structural unit of the chloroplast, whereas
        1. Quantasomes
        2. Quantasomes are the functional unit of the chloroplast.
          1. Photosynthetic pigments are present in the membranes of thylakoids at specific areas called Quantasomes. (280 pigments)
          2. Quantasomes of Eukaryotes consist of 230 chlorophyll molecules (160 chl-a +70 chl-b) and 50 carotenoid molecules.
          3. Quantasomes of Prokaryotes consist of only 50 chlorophyll molecules.
      6. Grana are interconnected by means of Stroma lamellae or Fret membrane or Intergranal thylakoid.

    Stroma

    1. Stroma is the ground substance of the chloroplast.
    2. Stroma includes:
      1. Matrix contains enzymes of dark reaction/ CO2 fixation.
      2. DNA + ribosome
  • Thylakoids are the structural units of chloroplasts.
  • Photosynthetic pigments are present in membranes of thylakoids at specific areas called Quantasomes.

Blue-Green Algae

Blue-green algae lack chloroplasts; the site of photosynthesis is photosynthetic lamellae.

Mitochondria

Overview

  • Powerhouse of the cell or electric house of the cell or cellular furnace or ATP mills.
  • Discovered by Kolliker in striated muscle of insects.
  • Term "mitochondria" used by Benda; Altman coined the term "bioplast" for mitochondria.
  • Size varies from 0.5 to 1 Œºm.
  • Absent in prokaryotes, anaerobic eukaryotes, mature mammalian RBC, calcareous cells, and sieve tubes of phloem.

Structure of Mitochondria

  • Membranes:
    • Outer membrane is permeable due to porins, while inner membrane is impermeable.
    • Inner membrane has infoldings called Cristae (animals) or tubuli/microvilli (plants).
    • Cristae are the site of oxidation-reduction and bear the electron transport chain with Oxysomes.
    • Oxysomes are functional units with head, stalk, and base components.
  • Matrix:
    • About 70% of total cell enzymes are found in mitochondria.
    • Enzymes of Kreb's cycle are in the matrix except Succinic dehydrogenase.
    • Enzymes for fatty acid synthesis, amino acid synthesis, etc., are in the matrix except ATPase and Cytochrome oxidase.

Functions

  • Petite character in yeast due to mitochondrial inheritance.
  • Mitochondria aid in fatty acid elongation and form the middle piece of sperm.

Process and Additional Details

  • Process of ATP synthesis in mitochondria is called Oxidative Phosphorylation (O2 dependent, light independent).
  • Mitochondria collectively referred to as Chondriome.
  • Mitochondria without outer membrane are called Mitoplasts.
  • Acristate mitochondria found in spermatid.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

Overview

  • Firstly observed by Porter from liver cells.
  • ER is also called:
    1. Working bench of cell
    2. Pipeline of cell
    3. Endoskeleton
    4. Organelle of Membrane factory
    5. Organelle of Detoxification
  • Extensively developed in Pancreas, Liver, Digestive gland, Fibroblast; poorly developed in sperms, meristematic cells, etc.

Structure and Composition

  • Unit membrane structure, bounded by a single trilaminar lipoprotein membrane.
  • Not visible under a light microscope.
  • Absent in prokaryotic cells and certain eukaryotic cells like mammal's RBC, egg, embryonic cells, monocytes of WBC.
  • ER is poorly developed in rapidly dividing cells like meristematic cells.
  • ER is made up of:
    1. Cisternae (flattened sac/bag)
    2. Tubules
    3. Vesicles
  • ER continues from the nuclear membrane to the cell membrane.
  • ER continues from one cell to another by plasmodesmata, known as Desmotubule.
  • ER forms more than 50% of total membranes in eukaryotic cells and occupies 10% of total cell volume.

Types and Functions

  • ER is of 2 types: Rough ER (RER) and Smooth ER (SER).
    • Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum
    a. Forms 2/3 of total ER. Forms 1/3 of total ER.
    b. 80s ribosomes are attached by their largest subunit with the help of glycoprotein called Ribophorin. Lacks attached ribosomes.
    c. Mainly consists of Cisternae and a few tubules. Mainly consists of a network of tubules and vesicles.
    d. Arises from nuclear membrane. Arises from RER.
    e. Function: Protein synthesis and Glycosylation. Function: Lipid metabolism.
  • The broken part of RER produced during ultracentrifugation is called Microsome.
  • Part of cytoplasm basophilic due to presence of RER/ribosome is known as ergastoplasm.
  • RER without ribosomes is called Transitional ER.
  • SER present in epithelial cells of retina eyes is called Myloid bodies (responsible for photoreception).
  • Special type of ER in skeletal muscle cells is called Sarcoplasmic reticulum (involved in release of Ca++).
  • SER in liver cells helps in detoxification of harmful chemicals and glycogenesis/glycogenolysis.

Functions and Additional Details

  • ER provides mechanical support due to presence of microtubules, known as Endoskeleton of cell.
  • ER assists in intercellular and intracellular transport and provides mechanical support to cytoplasm.
  • ER keeps cell organelles in position.
  • ER is involved in the formation of nuclear membrane, cell membrane, vacuoles, lysosomes, glyoxysome, Golgi bodies, and exine of pollen grain.
  • ER aids in detoxification of toxic substances.
  • The major phospholipid synthesized in ER is Lecithin (Phosphatidylcholine).

Golgi Bodies

Overview

  • Discovered by Camillo Golgi from nerve cells of a cat and Barn owl.
  • Also called Idiosome, Dalton complex, Middle man of cell.
  • Dictyosome is the name of the Golgi body used in plant cells.

Presence and Formation

  • Absent in prokaryotic cells and certain eukaryotic cells like RBCs, sieve tubes, sperm of Bryophyte and Pteridophyte, and cells of fungi.
  • Formed from vesicles of SER (Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum).

Structure

  • Consists of:
    1. Cisternae (parallely arranged)
    2. Tubules
    3. Vesicles
  • New cisternae are constantly formed by budding off SER and fusion of vesicles. The outside convex is known as the 'Forming face'.
  • Microtubules are not found in Golgi bodies.

Functions

  • Principal director of macromolecular traffic in cell.
  • Maximum number of Golgi bodies found in Rhizoidal cells of Chara.
  • Involved in the formation of Zymogen granules.
  • Functions include:
    1. Secretion and storage of secretions.
    2. Chemical modification and transport of materials received.
    3. Cell plate formation during cytokinesis.
    4. Formation of lysosomes.
    5. Formation of Acrosomes in sperms.
    6. Secretion of mucilage in root tip region.
    7. Formation of special carbohydrate called Sialic acid in cell membrane.
    8. Formation of yolk, melanin granules, hemicellulose, galactose.
    9. Assistance in Glycosidation and Glycosylation.
    10. Protein packaging.
  • Common enzymes of ER and Golgi bodies:
    1. Peptidyl transferase (main enzyme of RER)
    2. Glycosyl transferase (main enzyme of Golgi bodies)

Ribosomes

Overview

  • Discovered and named Ribosome by Palade, also known as Palade particle.
  • Also called:
    1. Engine of the cell
    2. Protein factory üè≠ of the cell
    3. RNP (Ribonucleo protein particle)
  • Smallest, unmembranous, Electron microscopic, and Most abundant cytoplasmic organelle.

Composition

  • Made up of r-RNA and Protein.
  • Synthesized in Nucleolus and Cytoplasm.

Formation and Measurement

  • Ribosomes are formed inside Nucleolus.
  • Measuring unit of Ribosome = Svedberg unit (S)
    • S ‚Üí Sedimentation coefficient (Rate of settlement) [i.e., 1 S = 10^-13 sec]

Types of Ribosomes

70s ribosomes

  1. Found in Prokaryotic cell and in Mitochondria and Chloroplast of Eukaryotic cell.
  2. Sub-units
    1. 30s (has single r-RNA)
    2. 50s (has two r-RNA)
  3. Note:
    1. Total r-RNA in 70s ribosome = 3 and Total proteins = 55 proteins.
    2. 55s ribosomes are found in mammals' mitochondria, not 70s, and 55s has sub-units 35s and 25s.

80s ribosomes:

  1. Found in Eukaryotic cell
    1. Free = Synthesize non-secretory, non-export protein
    2. RER = Synthesize secretory export protein
  2. Sub-units
    1. 40s = has single r-RNA
    2. 60s = has 3 r-RNA
  3. Note:
    1. Total r-RNA in 80s ribosome = (1+3)=4 and total protein = 80 proteins.
    2. Two ribosomal sub-units are joined by Mg++.
    3. When Mg++ concentration is increased 10 times 2 ribosomes combine to form a dimer.
    4. During protein synthesis, a number of ribosomes (6 to 8) are found to attach to m-RNA and form polysomes/polyribosomes/Ergasomes.
    5. During protein synthesis, smaller subunits of ribosome attach to m-RNA, not the bigger one.
    6. Ribosomes are also found in organelles of eukaryotic cells like plastids, mitochondria, and nucleus. So ribosomes are also called Organelles within organelles.

Structure of Nucleus

  • Nuclear envelope/ Karyotheca/ Nuclear envelope:
    1. Double-layered, lipoproteinaceous.
    2. Inner nuclear membrane is smooth as it lacks ribosome.
    3. Separates nucleoplasm from cytoplasm.
    4. Perinuclear space (50-70 A‚î¥o) between two nuclear membranes.
    5. Pore complex with Octagonal pores and Annulus gates.
  • Nucleoplasm/ Nuclear sap/ Karyoplasm/ Karyolymph:
    • Includes nucleotides and different ions (Mn++, Mg++).
    • Cyclosis is absent as its viscosity is not constant.
  • Nucleolus/ Plasmosome:
    1. Discovered by Fontana and named by Bowman.
    2. Membraneless structure also called "Ribosomal factory of the cell".
    3. Largest part of the nucleus.
    4. Contains rRNA and non-histone acidic proteins, but no DNA.
    5. Shape maintained by Ca++ and Mg++.
    6. Biogenesis occurs at 2o constriction-I of SAT chromosome.

Chromatin

  • Term "Chromatin" was given by Flemming.
  • Chromosome name coined by Waldeyer.
  • Made up of DNA (acidic, -ve charged) and histone protein (basic, + charged).
  • In ultrastructure, chromatin appears like beads on a string:
    1. Beaded structure is known as Nucleosome.
    2. Each Nucleosome has 8 histone molecules (2 copies of 4 types).
    3. 4 types of histones: H2A, H2B, H3, H4
    4. Coiled by double-stranded DNA.
  • Core or nucleosome made up of histone protein is known as Nu body.
  • Two nucleosomes are joined by linker DNA.
  • Stain of chromatin/chromosome is Feulgen stain = Basic fuschin
    • Feulgen stain reacts with "Deoxyribose".

Chromosome

  • No. of Chromosomes:
    1. Smallest no of chromosomes in Haplopappus gracillis.
    2. Largest no of chromosomes in Ophiglossum (Pteridophyta).
  • Condensed form of chromatin is called Chromosome.
  • A chromomere, also known as an idiomere, is a beaded structure in meiotic chromosomes.
  • Haploid set of chromosome is called genome.
  • Chromosome is the physical basis of inheritance.
  • Chromosomes carry hereditary units (genes) and are known as vehicles/bearers of genes.
  • Chromosome theory of inheritance was proposed by Sutton and Boveri.
  • Morphological feature of chromosome of a species is called Karyotype.
  • Diagrammatic representation of karyotype in descending sequence is called Idiogram.
  • Differential staining of the chromosome due to differential thickening is called Heteropycnosis.
  • Saliva test in Olympic games is related to X-chromosomes (Barr bodies).

Ultrastructure of an Eukaryotic Chromosome

  • Pellicle: Sheath or covering of chromosome.
  • Matrix: Ground substance.
  • Chromonemata: Coiled threads forming the bulk of chromosomes.
  • Primary (1o) constriction/Centromere:
    • Narrow non-stainable area where 2 chromatids are attached.
    • Provides site for spindle attachment, known as Kinetochore.
  • Secondary (2o) constriction-I (NOR): Contains genes for transcription of r-RNA.
  • Secondary (2o) constriction-II: Contains genes for transcription of t-RNA.
  • Satellite/Trabant: Knob-like structure near 2o constriction-I.
  • Telomere: Terminal end/seal end of chromosome.
