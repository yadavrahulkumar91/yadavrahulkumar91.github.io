  2. 2. What is the potential drop across an electric hot plate which draws 5A when its resistance is 24Ω? [IOM 2010]
    1. a. 120V
    2. b. 140V
    3. c. 130V
    4. d. 150V
    (b) The potential drop across an electric device can be calculated using Ohm's law, which states that the potential difference (\(V\)) is equal to the current (\(I\)) multiplied by the resistance (\(R\)): \(V = I \times R\). Given that the current (\(I\)) is 5A and the resistance (\(R\)) is 24Ω, we can substitute these values into the equation to find the potential difference (\(V\)): \(V = 5 \times 24 = 120\) volts. Therefore, the potential drop across the electric hot plate is 120V.
  3. 3. A 2μC charge is enclosed by a Gaussian surface of radius 0.5m. If the radius of the Gaussian surface is increased two times, the number of flux passing through the new surface will be [MOE 2068]
    1. a. Two times
    2. b. The same
    3. c. Four times
    4. d. 0.5 times
    (c) The flux passing through a closed surface is given by Gauss's law, which states that the flux (\(\Phi\)) is equal to the charge (\(Q\)) enclosed by the surface divided by the permittivity of free space (\(\epsilon_0\)): \(\Phi = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0}\). In this case, the charge enclosed by the Gaussian surface is 2μC. Initially, the radius of the Gaussian surface is 0.5m, and the flux passing through it is \(\Phi_1 = \frac{2\mu C}{\epsilon_0}\). When the radius of the Gaussian surface is increased two times (to 1m), the new flux passing through it is \(\Phi_2 = \frac{2\mu C}{\epsilon_0}\) (since the charge enclosed remains the same). Comparing the two flux values, we find that \(\Phi_2 = 4\Phi_1\), which means the flux passing through the new surface is four times the flux passing through the original surface. Therefore, the number of flux passing through the new surface is four times.
  4. 4. A sphere of radius 10cm is charged to a potential of 300 volts. The energy of the sphere is [MOE 2068]

    2. b. 5×10^(-6) J
    3. c. 5×10^(-5) J
    4. d. 5×10^(-3) J
    (c) The energy of a charged sphere is given by the formula: \(U = \frac{3}{5}\frac{ Q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0r}\). In this case, the radius (\(r\)) of the sphere is 10cm (0.1m), and the potential (\(V\)) is 300 volts. To find the energy (\(U\)), we need to determine the charge (\(Q\)). The potential of a sphere is related to its charge by the formula: \(V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q}{r}\). Rearranging this formula, we find: \(Q = 4\pi\epsilon_0Vr\). Substituting the given values, we get: \(Q = 4\pi\times8.85\times10^{-12}\times300\times0.1\) coulombs. Now we can substitute the charge value into the energy formula: \(U = \frac{3}{5}\frac{(4\pi\times8.85\times10^{-12}\times300\times0.1)^2}{4\pi\times8.85\times10^{-12}\times0.1}\). Simplifying the equation, we find: \(U = 5\times10^{-5}\) joules. Therefore, the energy of the sphere is \(5\times10^{-5}\) joules.

The electric field strength created by a point charge decreases with distance according to the inverse square law: \(E = \frac{kQ}{r^2}\), where \(k\) is the electrostatic constant, \(Q\) is the charge, and \(r\) is the distance from the charge. Substituting the electric field equation into the energy density equation, we get: \(u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\left(\frac{kQ}{r^2}\right)^2\). Simplifying the equation, we find: \(u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0\frac{k^2Q^2}{r^4}\). Therefore, the energy density in the electric field falls off with the distance from the point charge as \(r^{-4}\).

Since electrons have a negative charge, they move from higher electric potential (higher voltage) to lower electric potential (lower voltage). The drift velocity of the electrons is constant in a steady state, meaning the average velocity of the electrons remains constant as they move through the wire. Therefore, the correct statement is that electrons move from higher to lower potential with constant drift velocity.
