Gram Staining

Principle of Gram Staining

Gram staining is a differential staining technique used in microbiology to categorize bacteria into two groups - Gram-positive and Gram-negative - based on their cell wall characteristics.

Cytoplasmic pH Theory

The cytoplasm of Gram positive bacteria are said to be more acidic (2) than those of Gram negative ones (3). Hence the dye is said to bind with more affinity to Gram positive cells.

Cell Wall Theory

  1. The Cell Wall theory proposes that the differences in the structure and composition of bacterial cell walls are responsible for the divergent staining reactions observed during Gram staining.
  2. Gram-positive bacteria possess a thick peptidoglycan layer that retains the crystal violet-iodine complex due to its high cross-linking and structural integrity. The peptidoglycan layer is primarily responsible for trapping and anchoring the dye in the cell wall.
  3. On the other hand, Gram-negative bacteria have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharides. The outer membrane acts as a permeability barrier, preventing the crystal violet-iodine complex from entering the periplasmic space or being retained effectively within the cell wall.
  4. During the decolorization step, the thinner peptidoglycan layer of Gram-negative bacteria cannot retain the dye, and the dye-leaching from the cell wall results in the uptake of the counterstain (safranin).
  5. Thus, the structural differences in the cell wall, particularly the thickness of the peptidoglycan layer and the presence of the outer membrane, play a significant role in the differential staining characteristics of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria.

Procedure:

  1. Prepare a heat-fixed smear of the bacterial sample on a glass slide.
  2. Cover the smear with crystal violet, a primary stain, and let it sit for about one minute.
  3. Rinse off the excess stain with water.
  4. Apply iodine solution (Gram's iodine) as a mordant to enhance crystal violet retention.
  5. Wash off the iodine solution with water.
  6. Decolorize the smear with alcohol or acetone. This step differentiates Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria based on their cell wall properties.
  7. Quickly rinse off the decolorizer with water.
  8. Counterstain the smear with safranin, a secondary stain.
  9. Wash off the safranin stain with water and allow the slide to air dry.

Interpretation:

  • Gram-positive bacteria retain the crystal violet-iodine complex and appear purple-blue under the microscope.
  • Gram-negative bacteria do not retain the crystal violet-iodine complex and appear pink-red after counterstaining with safranin.
  • The different staining properties are due to the differences in the structure of the bacterial cell walls.

Reason for Using Iodine

Iodine serves as a mordant in the Gram staining process. A mordant is a substance that enhances the ability of a stain to bind to the bacterial cells. When iodine is applied after the application of crystal violet, it forms a complex with the crystal violet dye inside the bacterial cells.

CLED (cysteine-, lactose- and electrolyte-deficient) Agar

Composition:

  • Peptone: Provides essential nutrients for bacterial growth.
  • Lactose: Absence of lactose allows the differentiation of lactose fermenting and non-fermenting bacteria.
  • Cystine and electrolytes: Promote the growth of urinary tract pathogens and help in maintaining osmotic balance.
  • Agar: Solidifying agent that gives the medium its gel-like consistency.
  • Phenol Red: pH indicator that changes color based on acid or alkaline production during bacterial growth.

Uses:

  • CLED Agar is primarily used for the isolation and differentiation of urinary pathogens from urine samples.
  • The medium is selective and differential, allowing the growth of Gram-negative bacteria, while inhibiting the growth of most Gram-positive organisms.
  • It differentiates between lactose fermenting and non-fermenting bacteria based on the color change of the pH indicator.

Interpretation:

  • Lactose fermenting bacteria produce acid during fermentation, which turns the medium yellow.
  • Non-lactose fermenting bacteria do not produce acid and leave the medium red or pink.
  • The medium also supports the growth of urinary pathogens, such as Escherichia coli, Klebsiella spp., Proteus spp., and other related species.

Antibiotics Sensitivity Test

  1. Dilution method (quantitative/MIC/MBC)
  2. Disc diffusion method:
    • Stokes method
    • Kirby Bauer method

Disc diffusion method

  • Commercially prepared filter paper discs are impregnated with a specified amount of antimicrobial agent and applied to the surface of an agar medium.
  • The method is suitable for organisms growing rapidly overnight at 35-37°C.
  • Antibiotics diffuse in the agar medium such that:
    • Distance from the disc (\(d\)) \(\propto\) \(\frac{1}{\text{concentration of antibiotics}}\)
  • In areas where the concentration of the drug is inhibitory, no growth occurs, forming a zone of inhibition around the disc.
    • Result interpretation
    • By comparing the zone of inhibition of the antibiotics with the standard chart as described by Kirby-Bauer, the organisms can be categorized as:
      1. Sensitive (S)
      2. Intermediate/ moderately sensitive (MS)
      3. Resistance (R)
    The Zone Of Inhibition:
    • Zone of inhibition can be defined as the area (zone) around the antibiotic sensitivity disc in which the growth of microorganisms is inhibited.

Lactophenol Cotton Blue (LPCB)

Lactophenol Cotton Blue (LPCB) is a staining solution used in microbiology and mycology for the preparation and examination of fungal samples.

Composition

  • Lactophenol: A solution containing phenol, lactic acid, and glycerol, which helps in preserving and clearing the fungal samples.
  • Cotton Blue: A blue dye that stains fungal structures, making them easier to observe under the microscope.

Uses

  • LPCB is used to prepare wet mounts or smears of fungal samples for microscopic examination.
  • It is commonly used in the identification of fungal species, particularly in the study of molds and yeast.

Procedure

  • A small amount of the fungal sample is mixed with LPCB on a glass slide.
  • LPCB helps in clearing the sample and making the fungal structures more visible under the microscope.
  • The slide is covered with a coverslip and observed under a microscope.
  • Fungal structures such as hyphae, conidia, and spores can be easily identified and studied using LPCB staining.

Advantages

  • LPCB provides excellent clearing and staining properties, making fungal structures easier to visualize and identify.
  • The solution helps in preserving fungal samples for extended periods, making it suitable for long-term storage and reference.
  • It is a widely used and reliable method for fungal identification in research, clinical, and educational settings.

Culture Plate

The culture plate, also known as a petri dish or agar plate, is a common laboratory tool used in microbiology for cultivating and observing microorganisms. It consists of a flat, round, shallow dish made of transparent material, usually glass or plastic.

Uses:

  • Culturing microorganisms: The culture plate provides a solid growth medium, typically agar-based, where microorganisms can be cultured and allowed to grow.
  • Isolation of colonies: Microorganisms can be streaked or spread on the surface of the agar to isolate individual colonies for further study and identification.
  • Antimicrobial susceptibility testing: Culture plates are utilized to test the effectiveness of antibiotics and determine the susceptibility of microorganisms to various antimicrobial agents.
  • Studying colony characteristics: Microbial colonies on the culture plate can be observed for their color, shape, size, and other characteristics, aiding in the identification of different species.

Rapid Kit Test (Immunochromatographic Test)

The Rapid Kit Test, also known as the Immunochromatographic Test, is a diagnostic method used to quickly and efficiently detect specific antigens or antibodies in patient samples.

Principle

  • The test is based on the principle of immunochromatography, where antigen-antibody interactions lead to the formation of visible lines on the test strip.

Procedure

  • A sample (e.g., blood, urine, saliva) is applied to the test strip.
  • If the target antigen or antibody is present in the sample, it binds to the corresponding capture molecules on the test strip.
  • Conjugated particles (e.g., colloidal gold) labeled with specific antibodies then bind to the antigen or antibody, forming a complex.
  • This complex migrates along the test strip by capillary action.
  • As the complex moves along the strip, it encounters a region containing immobilized antibodies that capture the complex, forming a visible colored line.
  • A control line is also present to verify that the test is working correctly.

Advantages

  • Rapid results: The test provides quick results within minutes, making it suitable for point-of-care testing and emergency situations.
  • User-friendly: The test is relatively easy to perform and interpret, requiring minimal training.
  • Portable: Rapid kits are often compact and portable, allowing for testing in remote or resource-limited settings.
  • Specific and sensitive: Rapid kits can offer high specificity and sensitivity for detecting target antigens or antibodies.

Instruments

Autoclave

Autoclaving is the most surest and efficient method to kill all bacteria and spores.

Autoclaving Conditions

  • Temperature: 121°C
  • Chamber pressure: 15 lb square inch
  • Duration (Holding time): 15-20 mins

Uses

  • Sterilize culture media
  • Rubber goods
  • Syringes
  • Gowns
  • Aprons
  • Dressings
  • Gloves
  • Towels
  • Infected materials

Sterilization Control

Methods for sterilization control include:

  • Dried spores of Bacillus thermophilus
  • Brown's tube (turning red to green on attainment of adequate temperature)
  • Thermocouple

Precautions

  • The air must be allowed to escape from the chamber before proceeding, as the temperature of the air-steam mixture at a given pressure is lower than that of pure steam.
  • Contents should be arranged properly to ensure free circulation of steam inside the chamber.

Centrifuge

The Centrifuge is a laboratory instrument that operates on the principle of centrifugal force.

Uses

  • To separate plasma or serum from blood for serology.
  • To concentrate components of biological fluids such as CSF (Cerebrospinal Fluid), urine, and serum for microscopic examination.

Hot Air Oven

The most widely used sterilizer for dry heat, particularly for glassware.

Sterilization Temperature

  • Either 160°C for 60 mins
  • Or 170°C for 40 mins
  • Or 180°C for 20 mins

Sterilization Control

Thermocouples or spores of non-toxigenic strain of Clostridium tetani or Brown's tube with screen tube.

Used in Sterilization of:

  • Glassware like syringes, petri dishes, test tubes, pipettes
  • Surgical instruments like forceps, scalpels, scissors
  • Oily fluids which are impermeable to water, such as oils and fats
  • Chemicals such as powder which would clump or form a cake in the presence of moisture

Water Bath

The Serological Water Bath is a laboratory instrument used to maintain a relatively constant temperature in serological reactions, such as agglutination reactions.

Advantages over Incubator for Serology

  • The water current in the serological water bath enhances the antigen-antibody (Ag-Ab) reaction, leading to more efficient and effective reactions.

Incubator

The Incubator is an electrically heated and thermostatically controlled device.

Use

  • To create and maintain a constant temperature (typically 36°C) for the growth of mesophilic organisms.
