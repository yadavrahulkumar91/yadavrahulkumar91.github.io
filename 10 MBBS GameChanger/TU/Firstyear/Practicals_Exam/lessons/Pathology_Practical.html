Tuberculosis, lymph node

  1. Microscopic features
    1. Well-formed epithelioid granuloma composed of central caseous necrosis surrounded by epithelioid histiocytes
    2. Multinucleated giant cells (Langhans giant cells)
    3. Peripheral cuffing of granuloma by lymphocytes

Epidermal cyst

  1. a. Microscopic features:
    1. i. Cyst lining composed of stratified squamous epithelium with a granular layer.
    2. ii. Cyst wall does not contain eccrine glands of hair follicles or sebaceous glands.
    3. iii. Cyst contents composed of abundant keratin flakes.
    4. iv. Foreign body giant cell reaction is frequently present in ruptured cysts.

Melanin pigment

  1. a. Histological features
    1. i. Melanin is a brown pigment produced by melanocytes.
    2. ii. Melanin is made in melanosomes in melanocytes that are interspersed with basal cells of the epidermis.
    3. iii. Melanocytes interdigitate their cytoplasm with adjacent epidermal cells and transfer melanin granules to them.

Hyperplasia (BPH)

  1. Microscopic features
    1. Both glands and stroma may be involved although hyperplasia of the former is usually more prominent.
    2. Numerous crowded glands are present. There is still stroma between the glands.
    3. The glands are larger than normal, with more complex infoldings but are still lined by a double layer of uniform columnar cells and basal cuboidal cells that show no atypia.
    4. A rounded pink concentration, typical for corpora amylacea found in benign prostatic glands is present.

Hyaline Change

  1. Homogenous, glassy, eosinophilic appearance within cell or extracellular space.
  2. It may also occur in vessel wall.

Thrombus

  1. Microscopic features
    1. The internal elastic lamina is degenerated and is disrupted at the site of attachment of thrombus to the vessel wall
    2. The residual lumen of the original artery is slit like and shows flowing blood.
    3. The structure of thrombus shows lines of Zahn composed of layers of light-staining fibrin strands and platelets enmeshed in dark-staining red cells.
    4. The underlying atheromatous plaque may be seen.
    5. Organised thrombus shows ingrowth of granulation tissue at the base having spindle cells and capillary channels.

Leprosy

  1. Leprosy
    1. Lepromatous leprosy
      1. Microscopic features
        1. The dermis contains large aggregates of lipid-laden foamy macrophages known as lepra cells.
        2. The dermal infiltrate of lepra cells characteristically does not encroach upon the basal layer of epidermis leaving uninvolved (Clear zone) subepidermal dermis.
        3. The overlying epidermis is thin and flat.
        4. With fite-faraco stain, lepra cells are crowded with acid fast lepra bacilli.
    2. Tuberculoid leprosy
      1. Microscopic features
        1. The dermis shows granulomas which closely resemble hard tubercles i.e, they consist of epitheloid cells, langhans giant cells and a few lmyphocytes at the periphery but generally little or no caseation necrosis.
        2. The granulomas erode the basal layer of the epidermis, i.e., there is no clear zone.
        3. The lesions of tuberculoid leprosy have predilection for dermal nerves which may be destroyed and infiltrated by epitheliod cells and lymphocytes.
        4. With fite-faraco stain, lepra bacilli are almost never found (paucibacillary leprosy).

Osteosarcoma Microscopic

  1. Gross features
    1. They are bulky, grey white tumors arising from the medullary cavity.
    2. They contain areas of hemorrhage.
  2. Microscopic features
    1. Tumor cells demonstrate pleomorphism.

    3. Abundant mitosis are seen in the tumor.
    4. New bone formation by tumor cells.

Giant Cell Tumor (Magnified) Microscopic

  1. Gross features
    1. Tumour is eccentrically located in the epiphyseal end of a long bone.
    2. Cut surface of the tumor is hemorrhagic, necrotic and honey combed.
    3. Surrounding cortex is thinned out.
  2. Microscopic features
    1. Numerous osteoclast like giant cells with 100 or more nuclei.
    2. Large mononuclear cells.
    3. Vascular stroma, necrosis and mitoses are seen.

Ewings sarcoma

  1. Gross
    1. Arising in the medullary cavity, tumor usually invades the cortex, periosteum and soft tissue.
    2. The tumor is soft tan-white and frequently contains area of hemorrhage and necrosis.
  2. Microscopic features
    1. Tumor is composed of small rounded cells with scanty cytoplasm.
    2. Homa-wright rosette are present which consists of rounded cell clusters with a central fibrillary core.
    3. Fibrous septa are also seen separating the tumor cells.

Schwannoma

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

  1. Gross (Lung)
    1. This carcinoma usually arises centrally in the lung.
    2. The neoplastic tissue is gray-white and firm to hard.
    3. Areas of hemorrhage or necrosis.
    4. Sometimes these necrotic foci cavitate.
  2. Microscopic features
    1. Tumor cells arranged in nests, cords, and single scattered.
    2. Round to polygonal cells having abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and round hyperchromatic nuclei.
    3. Intercellular bridges.
    4. Keratin pearls.

Basal Cell Carcinoma

  1. Microscopic features
    1. Composed of nests, cords, islands of uniform basaloid cells growing deep into the dermis.
    2. Peripheral palisading of tumor cells.
    3. Retraction artifact from adjacent stroma.

Naevus

  1. Microscopic features
    1. The lesion is composed of melanocytes forming aggregates of nests at the dermo-epidermal junction (junctional naevus), which subsequently migrate to the underlying dermis (compound naevus). The older lesions may be entirely confined to the dermis (dermal naevus).
    2. The melanocytes forming naevi are found to oral cells and have rounded or oval nuclei. The cytoplasm of naevus cells is homogeneous and contains abundant granular brown-black melanin pigment.
    3. The pigment is more marked in the naevus cells in the lower epidermis and upper dermis, but the cells in the mid dermis and lower dermis hardly contain any pigment.

Leiomyoma

  1. Gross
    1. Sharply circumscribed, discrete, round, firm gray-white tumors varying in size.
    2. They can occur within the myometrium (intramural), just beneath the endometrium (submucosal), or beneath the serosa (subserosal).
    3. The characteristic whorled pattern of smooth muscle cells on the cut section makes these lesions readily identifiable.
  2. Microscopic features
    1. Interlacing bundles of uniform spindle cells.
    2. Mitoses are not seen.
    3. The cytoplasm of smooth muscle fibers is more pink, and their nuclei are short, plump, and fusiform.
    4. Larger leiomyomas may have central softening with hemorrhage.
    5. Associated with hyalinization.

Hemangioma

  1. Microscopic features
    1. Cavernous hemangioma has large dilated vascular channels filled with numerous red blood cells that extend to the underlying adipose tissue.
    2. The vascular channels, which may vary in size and shape, are lined by flat endothelial cells.
    3. A capillary hemangioma has smaller vascular channels.

Retinoblastoma

Microscopic features

Oligodendroglioma

  1. Tumor cells in sheets with spherical nuclei containing finely granular chromatin surrounded by a clear halo of vacuolated cytoplasm
  2. Network of anastomosing capillaries, resembling "Chicken wire"
  3. Calcification, mitotic figures and necrosis
  4. Tumor cells that infiltrate the cerebral cortex often collect around neurons (perineuronal satelitosis).

Otitis media

  1. Acute and chronic inflammatory cells, glandular metaplasia, with cilia, fibrosis, hemorrhage, foci of calcification (tympanosclerosis) and reactive bone formation.
  2. Cholesterol granulomas and cholesterol clefts.
  3. Cholesteatoma: Cysts lined by keratinizing squamous epithelium or metaplastic mucus-secreting epithelium and filled with amorphous, keratinous debris.

Nasal polyp

Microscopic features

  1. Polypoidal structure lined by respiratory to squamous epithelium.
  2. Edematous, fibrotic or loosely myxoid stroma
  3. Stroma is infiltrated by mixed inflammatory infiltrates composed by lymphocytes, neutrophils, plasma cells, eosinophils with mast cells.
