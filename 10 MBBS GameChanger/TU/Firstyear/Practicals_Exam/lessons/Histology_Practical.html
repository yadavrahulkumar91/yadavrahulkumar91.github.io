Histology 1

Simple Squamous Epithelium

  • Main histological features:
    • Single layer of flattened cells
    • Cells have centrally located nuclei
    • Cells are tightly packed and form a thin, smooth surface
  • Example: lining of blood vessels and alveoli in the lungs

Simple Cuboidal Epithelium of Thyroid (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Single layer of cube-shaped cells
    • Central, spherical nuclei in each cell
    • Cells are arranged in a regular pattern
  • Example: lining of thyroid follicles

Simple Cuboidal Epithelium

  • Main histological features:
    • Single layer of cube-shaped cells
    • Each cell has a centrally located spherical nucleus
    • Cells have a distinct cell membrane
  • Example: lining of kidney tubules and certain glands

Simple Columnar Epithelium (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Single layer of tall, column-shaped cells
    • Nuclei are elongated and located at the base of the cells
    • Cells have microvilli on their apical surface for absorption
  • Example: lining of the small intestine

Simple Columnar Epithelium (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Single layer of tall, column-shaped cells
    • Nuclei are located near the base of the cells
    • Cells may have goblet cells interspersed, which secrete mucus
  • Example: lining of the stomach and intestines

Pseudostratified Epithelium (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Appears stratified but is actually a single layer of cells
    • Nuclei are at different heights, giving the appearance of multiple layers
    • Contains goblet cells that secrete mucus
  • Example: lining of the respiratory tract, such as the trachea

Stratified Squamous Epithelium of Esophagus (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Multilayered epithelium with squamous (flat) cells on the surface
    • Basal cells are cuboidal or columnar and actively divide to replace superficial cells
    • Protective and durable barrier for the esophageal lining
  • Example: lining of the esophagus

Stratified Squamous Non-keratinized Epithelium of Epiglottis (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Multilayered epithelium with squamous (flat) cells on the surface
    • Cells in the surface layer retain their nuclei
    • Non-keratinized, meaning no tough layer of keratin on the surface
  • Example: epiglottis, the flap-like structure in the throat that prevents food from entering the trachea during swallowing

Epithelium of Parotid Gland

  • Main histological features:
    • Simple cuboidal or simple columnar epithelium
    • Contains serous cells that produce watery saliva
    • Arranged in acini or clusters
  • Example: parotid gland, one of the major salivary glands located near the ear

Transitional Epithelium of Ureter (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Specialized type of stratified epithelium
    • Multiple layers of cells that change shape (from cuboidal to squamous) depending on the organ's distension
    • Cells have prominent rounded or dome-shaped apical surfaces

Trachea (Left) + Hyaline Cartilage (Right) (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Trachea:
      • Contains pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium
      • Goblet cells are present, secreting mucus to trap foreign particles
      • C-shaped rings of hyaline cartilage provide support and maintain the tracheal structure
    • Hyaline Cartilage:
      • Consists of chondrocytes embedded in a matrix of collagen fibers
      • Appears glassy and smooth under the microscope
      • Provides flexible support and reduces friction between articulating bones
  • Example: respiratory system in the human body

Esophagus

  • Main histological features:
    • Non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium
    • Lamina propria containing blood vessels and connective tissue
    • Muscularis mucosae for movement of the mucosal layer
  • Example: part of the digestive system, connecting the throat (pharynx) to the stomach

Transitional Epithelium of Ureter

  • Main histological features:
    • Stratified epithelium with multiple layers
    • Distinctive dome-shaped cells when the bladder is empty
    • Cells flatten and appear squamous when the bladder is distended
    • Tightly packed cells with little intercellular space
    • Surface cells have a rounded or cuboidal shape
  • Examples
    • Ureters: The transitional epithelium lines the ureters, which are muscular tubes connecting the kidneys to the urinary bladder.
    • Urinary bladder: Transitional epithelium is also found in the urinary bladder, where it allows for stretching and distension as the bladder fills with urine.
    • Urethra: The urethra, the tube through which urine is eliminated from the body, also contains transitional epithelium in certain regions.

Transitional Epithelium of Urinary Bladder

  • Main histological features:
    • Specialized type of stratified epithelium
    • Multiple layers of cells that change shape (from cuboidal to squamous) depending on the organ's distension
    • Cells are rounded when the bladder is empty and flattened when the bladder is stretched
  • Example: lining of the urinary bladder, which allows the bladder to stretch and accommodate varying volumes of urine

Mammary Gland (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Composed of secretory glandular tissue
    • Arranged in lobules and ducts
    • Lobules contain alveoli or milk-producing cells
  • Example: present in female mammals, responsible for producing and secreting milk to nourish offspring

Loose Connective Tissue (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Consists of scattered cells within a gel-like ground substance
    • Contains fibers such as collagen, elastic, and reticular fibers
    • Cells include fibroblasts, macrophages, and adipocytes
  • Example: found beneath the skin (subcutaneous tissue) and surrounding various organs to provide support, flexibility, and cushioning

Tendon (40X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Composed mainly of parallel collagen fibers
    • Consists of fibroblasts that produce and maintain the collagen matrix
    • Appears dense and strong under the microscope
  • Example: tendons connect muscles to bones and transmit forces generated during muscle contractions

Reticular fiber of Spleen

  • Main histological features:
    • Thin, delicate fibers composed of type III collagen
    • Form a branching network throughout the splenic parenchyma
    • Provide structural support to the spleen
    • Support the framework for the immune cells and other cellular components of the spleen
    • Stain positively with special stains like silver stains
  • Examples:
    • Splenic Parenchyma: Reticular fibers are a prominent component of the splenic parenchyma, forming a network that supports the cellular elements within the spleen.
    • Red Pulp: Within the red pulp of the spleen, reticular fibers form a mesh-like framework that houses red blood cells and immune cells.
    • White Pulp: Reticular fibers are also present in the white pulp of the spleen, providing structural support to the lymphoid tissue and facilitating immune responses.

White adipose tissue

  • Main histological features:
    • Large, unilocular adipocytes
    • Single, centrally located nucleus
    • Cytoplasm filled with lipid droplets
    • Minimal cytoplasmic organelles
    • Rich blood supply
  • Example: Subcutaneous fat in the hypodermis

Thick skin

  • Main histological features:
    • Epidermis with multiple layers of keratinocytes
    • Distinctive stratum lucidum layer
    • Well-developed stratum corneum layer
    • Increased number of sweat glands
    • Presence of hair follicles
  • Examples:
    • Palms of the hands: Thick skin is found on the palms of the hands, which is specialized for increased durability and protection.
    • Soles of the feet: Thick skin is also present on the soles of the feet, providing resistance to mechanical stress and friction.
    • Fingertips: The fingertips contain thick skin, which enhances tactile sensitivity and precision.

Aorta

  • Main histological features:
    • Consists of three layers: tunica intima, tunica media, and tunica adventitia
    • Tunica intima is the innermost layer, composed of endothelial cells and a thin layer of connective tissue
    • Tunica media is the middle layer, composed of smooth muscle cells and elastic fibers
    • Tunica adventitia is the outermost layer, composed of connective tissue containing blood vessels and nerves
  • Examples:
    • The aorta is the largest artery in the body and carries oxygenated blood from the heart to the rest of the body.

Stratified Epithelium in duct of Parotid gland

  • Main histological features:
    • Stratified epithelium with multiple layers
    • Basal layer composed of cuboidal or columnar cells
    • Superficial layers composed of flattened squamous cells
    • Presence of intercellular bridges
    • Protective function against mechanical and chemical stresses
  • Examples:
    • Ducts of the Parotid Gland: The ducts of the Parotid gland, a major salivary gland located near the ear, are lined with stratified epithelium to protect and facilitate the passage of saliva.
    • Other Exocrine Gland Ducts: Stratified epithelium can also be found in the ducts of other exocrine glands, such as the submandibular gland and sublingual gland, serving a similar protective and transportation function.

Reticular Fiber

  • Main histological features:
    • Thin, branching fibers composed of type III collagen
    • Form a delicate network or mesh-like structure in various tissues and organs
    • Provide structural support and help anchor cells in certain tissues, such as lymphoid organs and the stroma of organs
  • Example:
    • Found in lymph nodes, bone marrow, spleen, and other hematopoietic or lymphoid tissues

Non-keratinized stratified Squamous of Oesophagus

  • Main histological features:
    • Stratified epithelium with multiple layers
    • Superficial layers composed of squamous cells
    • Basal layer composed of cuboidal or columnar cells
    • Non-keratinized, meaning no layer of dead, keratinized cells is present
    • Provides protection against mechanical and chemical stresses
  • Examples:
    • Oesophagus: The non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium lines the oesophagus, which is the muscular tube connecting the throat to the stomach. This epithelium protects the underlying tissues from the abrasive action of swallowed food and liquids.
    • Other Mucous Membranes: Non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium can also be found in other areas of the body where protection is needed, such as the oral cavity, vagina, and anal canal.

Elastic fiber

  • Main histological features:
    • Elastic fibers are composed of elastin, an elastic protein
    • Thin, branching fibers that form a network
    • Elastic fibers have a yellowish appearance when stained
    • Provide elasticity and resilience to tissues
    • Allow tissues to stretch and recoil
  • Examples:
    • Lungs: Elastic fibers are abundant in the lungs, particularly in the alveolar walls, providing elasticity for expansion and contraction during respiration.
    • Arteries: The walls of arteries contain elastic fibers, allowing them to stretch and recoil with each heartbeat, maintaining blood pressure and flow.
    • Skin: Elastic fibers are present in the dermis of the skin, contributing to its elasticity and the ability to stretch and return to its original shape.

Elastic Fiber

  • Main histological features:
    • Composed primarily of elastin protein
    • Highly elastic and can stretch and recoil to their original shape
    • Provide resilience and flexibility to various tissues
  • Example:
    • Found in the walls of large arteries (such as the aorta), lung tissue, skin, and other connective tissues requiring elasticity

Brown Adipose Tissue

  • Main histological features:
    • Cells are densely packed with numerous small lipid droplets
    • Contain a high number of mitochondria
    • Rich in blood vessels
    • Distinctive brown appearance due to the presence of abundant mitochondria and iron-rich proteins
    • Specialized for heat production and energy expenditure
  • Examples:
    • Infants: Brown adipose tissue is highly present in newborns and infants, primarily located in the interscapular region, neck, and around the major blood vessels. It plays a crucial role in maintaining body temperature in newborns.
    • Hibernating animals: Animals that undergo hibernation, such as bears, also possess brown adipose tissue. It aids in thermogenesis during periods of prolonged inactivity and low external temperatures.
    • Adults: While brown adipose tissue is typically less abundant in adults, small depots can still be found in certain areas such as the neck, supraclavicular region, and along the spine. Its presence in adults is associated with metabolic activity and energy expenditure.

Fibrocartilage

  • Main histological features:
    • Consists of dense collagen fibers
    • Chondrocytes embedded in lacunae
    • Intermediate between hyaline cartilage and dense regular connective tissue
    • Provides both strength and flexibility
    • Contains no perichondrium
  • Examples:
    • Intervertebral discs: Fibrocartilage is present in the intervertebral discs, acting as shock absorbers and allowing flexibility between adjacent vertebrae.
    • Pubic symphysis: Fibrocartilage is found in the pubic symphysis, a joint connecting the two pubic bones in the pelvis, providing stability and flexibility during childbirth and movements of the pelvis.
    • Menisci of the knee: The menisci, located within the knee joint, are composed of fibrocartilage and aid in distributing forces, enhancing joint stability, and cushioning the articular surfaces of the knee.

Epiglottis

  • Main histological features:
    • Elastic cartilage
    • Contains numerous elastic fibers
    • Covered by a layer of stratified squamous epithelium on the superior surface and respiratory epithelium on the inferior surface
    • Leaf-shaped structure located at the base of the tongue
    • Acts as a flap to prevent food and liquid from entering the larynx during swallowing
  • Examples:
    • Respiratory system: The epiglottis is a vital component of the respiratory system, located within the larynx. It plays a crucial role in directing food and liquid away from the airway and into the esophagus during swallowing.
    • Protection of the airway: The epiglottis, along with other structures in the respiratory system, helps to protect the airway from foreign objects and ensures the proper flow of air during breathing.

Decalcified compact bone

  • Compact bone that has undergone decalcification
  • Removal of mineralized calcium salts
  • Retains the organic components, including collagen fibers and bone cells
  • Allows for better visualization of cellular and structural details
  • Loss of bone's inherent strength and rigidity

Hyaline cartilage

  • Main histological features:
    • Amorphous extracellular matrix
    • Chondrocytes embedded in lacunae
    • Smooth and glassy appearance
    • Lacks blood vessels and nerves
    • Provides cushioning and support
  • Examples:

Skeletal muscle

  • Main histological features:
    • Long, cylindrical muscle fibers
    • Striated appearance due to the arrangement of actin and myosin filaments
    • Multiple nuclei located at the periphery of the muscle fibers
    • Voluntary control
    • Capable of generating force and enabling body movements
  • Examples:
    • Arms and legs: Skeletal muscles are abundant in the arms and legs, allowing for movement and locomotion.
    • Abdomen and chest: Skeletal muscles in the abdomen and chest are involved in activities such as breathing, coughing, and core stabilization.
    • Facial muscles: Skeletal muscles in the face enable facial expressions, speech, and chewing.

Transverse Section of Skeletal Muscle (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Muscle fibers are cylindrical and multinucleated
    • Striated appearance due to the arrangement of actin and myosin filaments
    • Surrounded by connective tissue called endomysium
    • Fascicles, groups of muscle fibers, are surrounded by perimysium
    • The entire muscle is enveloped by epimysium
  • Example:
    • Represents a section of skeletal muscle, which is responsible for voluntary movements of the body

Longitudinal Section of Smooth Muscle (10X)

  • Main histological features:
    • Smooth muscle cells are spindle-shaped and non-striated
    • Single, centrally located nucleus in each smooth muscle cell
    • Cells are closely packed together
    • Smooth muscle lacks the organized sarcomere structure found in skeletal muscle
  • Example:
    • Representative of smooth muscle tissue found in various organs such as the digestive tract, blood vessels, and uterus

Compact bone

  • Main histological features:
    • Dense and solid bone tissue
    • Composed of tightly packed osteons (Haversian systems)
    • Contains lamellae, osteocytes, and canaliculi
    • Harbors Haversian canals that house blood vessels and nerves
    • Provides strength, support, and protection
  • Examples:
    • Long bones: Compact bone forms the outer shell (cortex) of long bones, such as the femur, humerus, and ulna. It provides structural integrity and protects the bone's internal components.
    • Skull: The cranial bones, including the frontal, parietal, and occipital bones, consist predominantly of compact bone. They safeguard the brain and maintain the shape of the skull.
    • Shaft of long bones: The diaphysis, or the shaft, of long bones comprises compact bone, providing rigidity and support for weight-bearing activities.

Histology Genetics

Turner syndrome

  • Monosomy X: Turner syndrome is typically characterized by the presence of a single X chromosome (45,X karyotype) in females instead of the usual two X chromosomes (46,XX).
  • Mosaicism: Some individuals with Turner syndrome may have a mosaic karyotype, where there is a mixture of cells with the 45,X monosomy and cells with a normal 46,XX karyotype.
  • Structural abnormalities: In rare cases, individuals with Turner syndrome may exhibit structural abnormalities involving the X chromosome, such as deletions or rearrangements.

Patau syndrome

  • Trisomy 13: Patau syndrome is caused by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome 13 (47,XY+13 or 47,XX+13) in each cell, resulting in trisomy 13.
  • Mosaicism: Some individuals with Patau syndrome may have a mosaic karyotype, where there is a mixture of cells with the trisomy 13 and cells with a normal karyotype.
  • Translocation: In rare cases, Patau syndrome may be caused by a translocation of chromosome 13, where a portion of chromosome 13 attaches to another chromosome.

Down Syndrome

  • Trisomy 21: Down Syndrome is primarily caused by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21 (47,XY+21 or 47,XX+21) in each cell, resulting in trisomy 21.
  • Mosaic Down Syndrome: In some cases, individuals with Down Syndrome may have a mosaic karyotype, where there is a mixture of cells with trisomy 21 and cells with a normal karyotype.
  • Translocation: A small percentage of Down Syndrome cases are caused by a translocation of a portion of chromosome 21 onto another chromosome. This can be a result of a rearrangement involving chromosome 21 and another chromosome, typically chromosome 14 or 22.

Klinefelter syndrome

  • 47,XXY karyotype: Klinefelter syndrome is characterized by the presence of an extra X chromosome in males, resulting in a karyotype of 47,XXY.
  • Mosaicism: Some individuals with Klinefelter syndrome may have a mosaic karyotype, where there is a mixture of cells with the 47,XXY karyotype and cells with a normal 46,XY karyotype.
  • Variants: In addition to the 47,XXY karyotype, variants of Klinefelter syndrome can include 48,XXYY, 48,XXXY, and 49,XXXXY karyotypes, which involve additional X chromosomes.
  • Structural abnormalities: Rarely, individuals with Klinefelter syndrome may have structural abnormalities involving the X chromosome, such as deletions or rearrangements.

Primary villi

  • Thin, finger-like projections
  • Composed of cytotrophoblasts and syncytiotrophoblasts
  • Contain a core of mesenchymal cells
  • Develop from the chorionic sac
  • Involved in nutrient exchange between the maternal blood and fetal circulation

Secondary villi

  • Finger-like projections with a more complex structure compared to primary villi
  • Composed of an outer layer of syncytiotrophoblasts and an inner layer of cytotrophoblasts
  • Contain blood vessels derived from the embryonic circulation
  • Involved in increased surface area for nutrient and gas exchange between the maternal and fetal circulation
  • Associated with the formation of the placenta

Tertiary villi

  • Finger-like projections with further branching and complexity compared to secondary villi
  • Composed of an outer layer of syncytiotrophoblasts, an inner layer of cytotrophoblasts, and a central core of mesenchyme
  • Contain an extensive network of blood vessels derived from both the maternal and fetal circulations
  • Play a crucial role in the exchange of nutrients, gases, and waste products between the maternal and fetal circulation
  • Provide support and nourishment to the developing embryo/fetus

Pharyngela arch

  • A series of paired embryonic structures found in the developing embryo
  • Consists of six pairs of arches numbered from 1 to 6, located in the pharyngeal region
  • Each arch is composed of specific cartilage, muscles, nerves, and blood vessels
  • Plays a crucial role in the development of various structures in the head and neck region
  • Contributes to the formation of facial features, jawbones, middle ear structures, and certain neck muscles

Pharyngeal pouch

  • A series of paired endodermal outpocketings found in the developing embryo's pharyngeal region
  • Consists of four pairs of pouches numbered from 1 to 4
  • Develops between the pharyngeal arches
  • Plays a crucial role in the formation and development of various structures in the head and neck region
  • Contributes to the development of structures such as the middle ear, tonsils, parathyroid glands, and thymus

Cerebral cortex

  • Neocortex:
    • Outermost layer of the cerebral cortex
    • Highly developed in humans
    • Responsible for advanced cognitive functions such as perception, conscious thought, and language
  • Archicortex (Hippocampus):
    • Located in the medial temporal lobe
    • Involved in memory formation and spatial navigation
    • Consists of several subregions, including the dentate gyrus and the cornu ammonis (CA) fields
  • Paleocortex:
    • Found in the olfactory system
    • Plays a role in processing and interpreting smell
    • Less developed compared to neocortex
  • Layers:
    • Consists of six distinct layers, numbered from I to VI
    • Each layer has unique cellular organization and connectivity patterns
    • Layer IV, the granular layer, is particularly prominent in sensory areas and receives input from thalamic nuclei

Cerebellum

  • Molecular layer:
    • Outermost layer of the cerebellum
    • Contains a dense network of parallel fibers and stellate cells
    • Receives input from the granule cells of the internal granular layer
    • Consists of inhibitory interneurons called basket cells and stellate cells
  • Purkinje cell layer:
    • Located between the molecular layer and the granular layer
    • Contains large, flask-shaped Purkinje cells that are the principal neurons of the cerebellar cortex
    • Purkinje cells have extensive dendritic trees that receive input from parallel fibers and climbing fibers
    • They are involved in integrating and modulating motor signals
  • Granular layer:
    • Innermost layer of the cerebellar cortex
    • Composed of densely packed granule cells, the most numerous neurons in the brain
    • Granule cells send their axons (parallel fibers) into the molecular layer, where they synapse with Purkinje cells and molecular layer interneurons
    • Involved in receiving sensory information and providing excitatory input to Purkinje cells
