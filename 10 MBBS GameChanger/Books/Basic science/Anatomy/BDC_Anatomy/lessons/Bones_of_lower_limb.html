B. CLAVICLE

I. Introduction

  • The clavicle (Latin a small key) is a long bone.
  • It supports the shoulder so that the arm can swing clearly away from the trunk.
  • The clavicle transmits the weight of the limb to the sternum.
  • The bone has a curved part called the shaft, and two ends, lateral and medial.

II. Side determination

  • The side to which a clavicle belongs can be determined from the following characters.
    1. The lateral end is flat, and the medial end is large and quadrilateral.
    2. The shaft is slightly curved, so that it is convex forwards in its medial two-thirds, and concave forwards in its lateral one-third.
    3. The inferior surface is grooved longitudinally in its middle one-third.

III. Peculiarities of the Clavicle

  • It is the only long bone that lies horizontally.
  • It is subcutaneous throughout.
  • It is the only long bone which ossifies in membrane.
  • It is the first bone to start ossifying.
  • It is the only long bone which has two primary centres of ossification.
  • There is no medullary cavity.
  • It is occasionally pierced by the middle supraclavicular nerve.

  • Weight transmission

    It receives weight of upper limb via lateral one-third through coracoclavicular ligament and transmits weight of upper limb to the axial skeletal via medial two-thirds part (see Flow chart 1.1).

IV. Features

  • Shaft

    The shaft is divisible into the lateral one-third and the medial two-thirds.

    1. The lateral one-third of the shaft is flattened from above downwards.
      • It has two borders, anterior and posterior.
        1. The anterior border is concave forwards.
        2. The posterior border is convex backwards.
      • This part of the bone has two surfaces, superior and inferior.
        1. The superior surface is subcutaneous.
        2. The inferior surface presents an elevation called the conoid (Greek cone) tubercle and a ridge called the trapezoid ridge.
    2. The medial two-thirds of the shaft is rounded and is said to have four surfaces.
      • The anterior surface is convex forwards.
      • The posterior surface is smooth.
      • The superior surface is rough in its medial part.
      • The inferior surface
        1. has a rough oval impression at the medial end.
        2. The lateral half of this surface has a longitudinal subclavian groove.
        3. The nutrient foramen lies at the lateral end of the groove.

  • Lateral and Medial Ends

    1. The lateral or acromial (Greek peak of shoulder) end is flattened from above downwards.
      • It bears a facet that articulates with the acromion process of the scapula to form the acromioclavicular joint.
    2. The medial or sternal end is quadrangular and articulates with the clavicular notch of the manubrium sterni to form the sternoclavicular joint.
      • The articular surface extends to the inferior aspect, for articulation with the first costal cartilage.

VI. OSSIFICATION

  1. a. The clavicle is the first bone in the body to ossify (Fig. 2.3).

  2. b. Except for its medial end, it ossifies in membrane.

  3. c. It ossifies from two primary centres and one secondary centre.

    1. The two primary centres appear in the shaft between the fifth and sixth weeks of intrauterine life, and fuse about the 45th day.
    2. The secondary centre for the medial end appears during 15-17 years and fuses with the shaft during 21-22 years.
      • 1) Occasionally there may be a secondary centre for the acromial end.

VII. CLINICAL ANATOMY

  • a. The clavicle is commonly fractured by falling on the outstretched hand (indirect violence).

    • i. The most common site of fracture is the junction between the two curvatures of the bone, which is the weakest point.

    • ii. The lateral fragment is displaced downwards by the weight of the limb as trapezius muscle alone is unable to support the weight of the upper limb (Fig, 2.4).

  • b. The clavicles may be congenitally absent or imperfectly developed in a disease called cleidocranial dysostosis.

    • i. In this condition, the shoulders droop and can be approximated anteriorly in front of the chest (Fig.2.5a and b).

C. SCAPULA

I. Introduction

  • a. The scapula (Latin shoulder blade) is a thin bone placed on the posterolateral aspect of the thoracic cage.

  • b. The scapula has two surfaces, three borders, three angles, and three processes (Fig.2.6).

II. Side determination

  • a. The lateral or glenoid (Greek socket) angle is large and bears the glenoid cavity.

  • b. The dorsal surface is convex and is divided by the triangular spine into the supraspinous and infraspinous fossae.

    • i. The costal surface is occupied by the concave subscapular fossa to fit on the convex chest wall (Figs 2.6 and 2.7).

  • c. The thickest lateral border runs from the glenoid cavity above to the inferior angle below.

III. Features

a. Surfaces

  • i. The costal surface or subscapular fossa is concave and is directed medially and forwards.

    • i. It is marked by three longitudinal ridges.
    • ii. Another thick ridge adjoins the lateral border.
      • a) This part of the bone is almost rod-like.
      • b) It acts as a lever for the action of the serratus anterior in overhead abduction of the arm.

  • ii. The dorsal surface gives attachment to the spine of the scapula which divides the surface into a smaller supraspinous fossa and a larger infraspinous fossa.

    • i. The two fossae are connected by the spinoglenoid notch, situated lateral to the root of the spine.

b. Borders

  • i. The superior border is shortest.

    • i. Near the root of the coracoid process it presents the suprascapular notch.

  • ii. The lateral border is thick.

    • i. At the upper end, it presents the infraglenoid tubercle.

  • iii. The medial border is thin.

    • i. It extends from the superior angle to the inferior angle.

c. Angles

  • i. The superior angle is covered by the trapezius.

  • ii. The inferior angle is covered by the latissimus dorsi.

    • i. It moves forwards round the chest when the arm is abducted.

  • iii. The lateral or glenoid angle is broad and bears the glenoid cavity or fossa, which is directed forwards, laterally, and slightly upwards (Fig.2.7). A supraglenoid tubercle is present above the glenoid cavity.

d. Processes

  • i. The spine or spinous process is a triangular plate of bone with three borders and two surfaces.

    • i. It divides the dorsal surface of the scapula into the supraspinous and infraspinous fossae.
    • ii. Its posterior border is called the crest of the spine.
      • a) The crest has upper and lower lips.

  • ii. The acromion has two borders, medial and lateral; two surfaces, superior and inferior; and a facet for the clavicle (Fig. 2.7).

  • iii. The coracoid (Greek like a crow's beak) process is directed forwards and slightly laterally. It is bent and finger-like. It is an atavistic type of epiphysis.

e. Attachments

  • i. The multipennate subscapularis arises from the medial two-thirds of the subscapular fossa (Figs 2.8 and 6.4).

  • ii. The supraspinatus arises from the medial two-thirds of the supraspinous fossa, including the upper surface of the spine (Fig. 2.9).

  • iii. The infraspinatus arises from the medial two-thirds of the infraspinous fossa, including the lower surface of the spine (Fig. 2.9).

  • iv. The deltoid arises from the lower border of the crest of the spine and from the lateral border of the acromion (Fig. 2.10). The acromial fibers are multipennate.

  • v. The trapezius is inserted into the upper border of the crest of the spine and into the medial border of the acromion (Fig. 2.10).

  • vi. The serratus anterior is inserted along the medial border of the costal surface:

    • One digitation from the superior angle to the root of the spine,
    • two digitations to the medial border, and
    • five digitations to the inferior angle (Fig. 2.8).

  • vii. The long head of the biceps brachii arises from the supraglenoid tubercle;

    • the short head from the lateral part of the tip of the coracoid process (Fig. 2e).

  • viii. The coracobrachialis arises from the medial part of the tip of the coracoid process.

  • ix. The pectoralis minor is inserted into the medial border and superior surface of the coracoid process (Fig. 2.8).

  • x. The long head of the triceps brachii arises from the infraglenoid tubercle.

  • xi. The teres minor arises from the upper two-thirds of the rough strip on the dorsal surface along the lateral border (Fig.2.9).

  • xii. The teres major arises from the lower one-third of the rough strip on the dorsal aspect of the lateral border (Fig.2.9). Latissimus dorsi arises from the inferior angle.

  • xiii. The levator scapulae is inserted along the dorsal aspect of the medial border, from the superior angle up to the root of the spine (Fig. 2.9).

  • xiv. The rhomboid minor is inserted into the medial border (dorsal aspect) opposite the root of the spine (Fig. 2e).

  • xv. The rhomboid major is inserted into the medial border (dorsal aspect) between the root of the spine and the inferior angle.

  • xvi. The inferior belly of the omohyoid arises from the upper border near the suprascapular notch (Fig. 2.8).

  • xvii. The margin of the glenoid cavity gives attachment to the capsule of the shoulder joint and to the glenoidal labrum (Latin lip) (Fig.2.8).

  • xviii. The margin of the facet on the medial aspect of the acromion gives attachment to the capsule of the acromioclavicular joint (Fig. 2.10).

  • xix. The coracoid process gives attachments to the conoid and trapezoid ligaments (Fig. 2.10).

Humerus

Humerus

Humerus is the largest bone of upperlimb.

1. Introduction

The humerus is a long bone located in the upper arm, extending from the shoulder joint to the elbow joint. It is the largest bone of the upper limb and consists of various features and regions.

2. Side Determination

The humerus can be distinguished as either the left or right bone based on specific anatomical features. These features include the position of the greater tubercle, lesser tubercle, and the orientation of the trochlea.

3. Features

a. Upper End

The upper end of the humerus consists of the following structures:

  • Head: The rounded portion of the humerus that articulates with the glenoid fossa of the scapula, forming the shoulder joint.
  • Greater Tubercle: A prominent bony projection located on the lateral side of the humerus, serving as an attachment site for various muscles and tendons.
  • Lesser Tubercle: A smaller bony projection situated on the anterior surface of the humerus, also providing attachment for muscles and tendons.

b. Shaft

The shaft of the humerus is the long, cylindrical portion connecting the upper and lower ends. It contains various anatomical landmarks and regions, including:

  • Deltoid Tuberosity: A roughened area located on the lateral aspect of the humerus, serving as the attachment site for the deltoid muscle.
  • Radial Groove: A shallow groove on the posterior surface of the humerus, providing passage for the radial nerve.
  • Surgical Neck: A constricted region just below the greater and lesser tubercles, which is a common site for fractures.

c. Ossification

The humerus undergoes a process called ossification during development. It initially forms from several separate bones that eventually fuse together to form the mature bone.

4. Clinical Anatomy

The clinical anatomy of the humerus involves its relationship to various structures and its importance in medical procedures, such as fracture management, joint replacements, and surgical approaches.

Radius

Ulna and Radius

Ulna and Radius lies side by side.

1. Introduction

The radius is one of the two long bones located in the forearm, alongside the ulna. It extends from the elbow to the wrist and plays a crucial role in forearm movement and support.

2. Side Determination

The radius can be distinguished as either the left or right bone based on specific anatomical features, such as the orientation of the radial tuberosity and the styloid process.

3. Features

a. Upper End

The upper end of the radius consists of the following structures:

  • i. Head: The rounded proximal end of the radius that articulates with the capitulum of the humerus and the radial notch of the ulna.
  • ii. Neck: The narrowed region just below the head of the radius.
  • iii. Radial Tuberosity: A roughened projection located on the medial aspect of the radius, serving as the attachment site for the biceps muscle.

b. Shaft

The shaft of the radius is the long, slender portion connecting the upper and lower ends. It consists of specific borders and surfaces:

i. Borders

  1. Anterior Border: The front-facing edge of the radius.
  2. Posterior Border: The back-facing edge of the radius.
  3. Medial or Interosseous Border: The inner border of the radius that is in close proximity to the ulna.

ii. Surfaces

  1. Anterior Surface: The front-facing surface of the radius.
  2. Posterior Surface: The back-facing surface of the radius.
  3. Lateral Surface: The outer surface of the radius.

c. Lower End

The lower end of the radius includes multiple surfaces:

  1. Anterior Surface: The front-facing surface of the distal end of the radius.
  2. Posterior Surface: The back-facing surface of the distal end of the radius.
  3. Medial Surface: The inner surface of the radius that is in close proximity to the ulna.
  4. Lateral Surface: The outer surface of the distal end of the radius.
  5. Inferior Surface: The lower surface of the distal end of the radius.

4. Attachments

The radius serves as an attachment site for various muscles, tendons, and ligaments involved in forearm movement and stability.

5. Ossification

The radius undergoes a process called ossification during development. It forms through both primary and secondary centers of ossification.

a. Primary Center

The primary center of ossification is located in the midshaft of the radius and is responsible for the initial formation of the bone.

b. Secondary Center

The secondary centers of ossification are found at the ends of the radius, contributing to the growth and maturation of the bone.

6. Clinical Anatomy

a. Colles' Fracture

Colles' fracture is a common type of distal radius fracture where the bone breaks near the wrist joint, resulting in the displacement of the distal fragment.

b. Smith's Fracture

Smith's fracture, also known as a reverse Colles' fracture, occurs when the distal fragment of the radius is displaced in the opposite direction, towards the palm.

c. Pulled Elbow

Pulled elbow, also called nursemaid's elbow, is a condition commonly seen in children where the radius becomes partially dislocated from the elbow joint, typically due to a sudden pull or jerk.
