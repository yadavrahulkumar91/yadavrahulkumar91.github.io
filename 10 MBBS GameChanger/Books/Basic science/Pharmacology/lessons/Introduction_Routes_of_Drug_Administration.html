Introduction

Pharmacology

  • Pharmacology is the branch of science that studies how drugs interact with living organisms, their effects, and their applications.
  • It encompasses the study of drug action, absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.

Pharmacokinetics

  • What the body does to the drug?
  • Pharmacokinetics refers to the study of how the body processes drugs, including their absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.
  • It focuses on understanding how drugs move within the body and how their concentration changes over time.

Pharmacokinetics of Ibuprofen

  • Absorption: Ibuprofen is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract after oral administration.
  • Distribution: It has a moderate volume of distribution, meaning it can distribute throughout the body's tissues.
  • Metabolism: Ibuprofen undergoes extensive metabolism in the liver, primarily through oxidation and glucuronidation processes.
  • Elimination: The majority of ibuprofen and its metabolites are eliminated through the kidneys via urine.

Pharmacodynamics

  • What the drug does to the body?
  • Pharmacodynamics involves the study of how drugs exert their effects on the body at the molecular, cellular, and systemic levels.
  • It investigates the mechanisms of drug action, including receptor interactions, enzyme inhibition, and signal transduction pathways.

Pharmacodynamics of Ibuprofen

  • Ibuprofen inhibits the enzymes cyclooxygenase (COX-1 and COX-2) that are responsible for the production of prostaglandins.
  • By reducing the synthesis of prostaglandins, ibuprofen exerts its analgesic, anti-inflammatory, and antipyretic effects.
  • It alleviates pain by reducing the sensitivity of pain receptors and diminishing the inflammatory response.
  • Ibuprofen helps in reducing fever by acting on the hypothalamus, a part of the brain that regulates body temperature.

Drug

Definition of a Drug

  • The World Health Organization defines a drug as any substance or product that is used or intended to be used to modify or explore physiological systems or pathological states for the benefit of the recipient.

Sources of Drugs

Natural Sources

Plants:
  • Many drugs are derived from plants, such as opium from poppy plants or aspirin from willow bark.
Animals:
  • Certain drugs, like insulin, are obtained from animal sources, typically through extraction or synthesis.
Microorganisms:
  • Microbes, including bacteria and fungi, produce compounds with therapeutic properties, like antibiotics.
Minerals
  • Minerals such as zinc, iron, and calcium can be used as supplements or incorporated into medications for their therapeutic benefits.
  • For example, iron supplements are commonly used to treat iron deficiency anemia.

Synthetic Sources

Laboratory Synthesis:
  • Synthetic drugs are created through chemical processes in laboratories, allowing for precise control over their composition.
Semi-Synthetic Compounds:
  • Some drugs are partially derived from natural sources and then modified in the laboratory to enhance their efficacy or safety.
Biotechnology:
  • Advances in biotechnology have enabled the production of drugs using genetically engineered organisms or through the manipulation of genetic material.

Drug Nomenclature

Chemical Name:

The chemical name represents the exact molecular structure of a drug, providing information about its composition and arrangement of atoms. It is typically complex and not suitable for everyday use.

Example: The chemical name of Aspirin is 2-Acetoxybenzoic acid.

Generic Name:

The generic name is a simpler, non-proprietary name assigned to a drug. It is usually derived from the drug's chemical name and is universally accepted. Generic names are not subject to trademark restrictions and can be used by multiple manufacturers.

Example: The generic name of Aspirin is acetylsalicylic acid.

Brand Name:

Brand names, also known as trade names or proprietary names, are unique names given to drugs by specific pharmaceutical companies. These names are used for marketing purposes and are protected by trademarks. Brand names can differ across countries due to regional marketing strategies.

Example: Aspirin is a brand name for the drug with the generic name acetylsalicylic acid. Other brand names for the same drug include Bayer Aspirin and Ecotrin.

Essential Medicines (Drugs)

Essential Medicines (Drugs) are a fundamental concept in healthcare systems worldwide. These medicines are considered to be the most effective and safe options needed to meet the priority healthcare needs of populations. The concept of essential medicines was introduced by the World Health Organization (WHO) to ensure equitable access to quality medications at affordable prices.

WHO Definition:

The WHO defines essential medicines as "those medicines that satisfy the priority healthcare needs of the population."

WHO Criteria for Essential Medicines Selection:

When selecting medicines to be included in the essential medicines list, the WHO considers the following criteria:

  1. Disease Burden: The medicine should address the most significant health problems in the population and target conditions that cause a high burden of disease.
  2. Evidence of Efficacy and Safety: There should be robust scientific evidence demonstrating the medicine's efficacy and safety in treating the targeted conditions.
  3. Quality and Safety: The medicine should meet quality standards, ensuring consistency, purity, and reliability in its formulation.
  4. Clinical Guidelines and Expert Consensus: The medicine should be recommended in clinical guidelines and endorsed by experts in the field.
  5. Cost-Effectiveness: The medicine should provide value for money and be cost-effective compared to alternative treatment options.
  6. Access and Affordability: The medicine should be available and affordable to the population, taking into account socioeconomic factors and health system constraints.

Non-prescription, Prescription and Orphan Drugs

Below are the key points and examples illustrating the concepts of non-prescription drugs (over-the-counter), prescription drugs and orphan drugs:

Non-prescription Drugs (Over-the-Counter Drugs):

  • Non-prescription drugs can be purchased directly by consumers without a prescription.
  • They are typically used for self-diagnosis, self-treatment, or management of minor ailments and conditions.
  • Examples:
    • Pain relievers like Ibuprofen
    • Cough and cold medications like Benadryl
    • Antacids like Tums

Prescription Drugs:

  • Prescription drugs require a valid prescription from a licensed healthcare professional.
  • They are used to treat complex or serious medical conditions that necessitate professional supervision.
  • Examples:
    • Antibiotics like Amoxicillin
    • Pain relievers like Oxycodone
    • Antidepressants like Sertraline

Orphan Drugs:

  • Orphan drugs are developed to treat rare diseases or conditions affecting a small number of individuals.
  • They receive special designation and incentives to encourage their development due to limited market demand.
  • Examples:
    • Imatinib (Gleevec) for Chronic Myeloid Leukemia
    • Nitisinone (Orfadin) for Hereditary Tyrosinemia Type 1
    • Eculizumab (Soliris) for Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria

Dosage Forms of Drugs

Dosage forms of drugs can be classified into three broad categories based on their physical states: solid, liquid, and gas. Each category includes specific types of dosage forms. Here is a breakdown of these categories:

Solid Dosage Forms:

  • Tablets: Compressed solid formulations containing active ingredients and excipients. They are swallowed whole.
  • Capsules: Gelatin shells enclosing powders, granules, or liquids.
  • Granules: Small particles or grains containing the drug, often used for reconstitution into a liquid form.
  • Powders: Fine particles or mixtures of active ingredients and excipients, which can be dissolved or suspended in liquids or used for topical applications.
  • Suppositories: Solid formulations that melt when inserted into body cavities (rectum, vagina), releasing the drug.
  • Transdermal patches: Adhesive patches containing the drug, which is slowly released through the skin for systemic absorption.

Liquid Dosage Forms:

  • Solutions: Homogeneous mixtures of one or more drugs dissolved in a liquid medium.
  • Syrups: Concentrated solutions containing sugars, flavorings, and drugs, often used for oral administration.
  • Emulsions: Mixture of immiscible liquids, such as oil and water, stabilized with an emulsifying agent.
  • Suspensions: Heterogeneous mixtures in which solid particles are dispersed in a liquid medium.
  • Elixirs: Clear, sweetened hydroalcoholic solutions often used for oral administration.

Gas Dosage Forms:

  • Inhalers: Devices that deliver drugs in the form of a gas or aerosol for inhalation into the lungs.
  • Anesthetic gases: Gases used for anesthesia, typically administered through inhalation.

These various dosage forms cater to different patient needs, routes of administration, and desired drug release characteristics. They offer flexibility and convenience in drug delivery, ensuring effective treatment and patient compliance.

Routes of Drug Administration

Local Routes of Administration:

Local routes involve the application or delivery of drugs to a specific area of the body, targeting localized conditions or providing localized effects.

Topical:

Application of drugs directly onto the skin or mucous membranes.

  • Dermal: Application to the outer layers of the skin.
  • Ophthalmic: Application to the eyes (eye drops, ointments).
  • Otic: Application to the ears (ear drops).
  • Nasal: Application to the nasal cavity (nasal sprays).
  • Buccal: Placement of drugs in the cheek or gum for absorption.
  • Sublingual: Placement of drugs under the tongue for absorption.
  • Inhalation: Administration of drugs through inhalation into the lungs (aerosols, inhalers).
Rectal:

Administration of drugs into the rectum (suppositories, enemas).

Vaginal:

Administration of drugs into the vagina (suppositories, creams).

Local Injection:

Direct injection of drugs into specific body regions or tissues.

  • Intradermal: Injection into the skin layers.
  • Intramuscular: Injection into the muscle tissue.
  • Subcutaneous: Injection into the tissue layer beneath the skin.

Systemic Routes of Administration:

Systemic routes involve the absorption and distribution of drugs throughout the body, allowing them to reach target organs or tissues and exert systemic effects.

Oral:

Administration of drugs through the mouth, typically in the form of tablets, capsules, or liquids.

Parenteral:

Administration of drugs by bypassing the digestive system.

  • Intravenous (IV): Injection of drugs directly into the bloodstream.
  • Intramuscular (IM): Injection of drugs into the muscle tissue.
  • Subcutaneous (SC): Injection of drugs into the tissue layer beneath the skin.
  • Intrathecal: Injection into the spinal canal (commonly used for anesthesia or certain medications).
Transdermal:

Application of drugs onto the skin for absorption into the bloodstream.

Inhalation:

Administration of drugs through inhalation into the lungs for systemic absorption.

These different routes of drug administration offer flexibility in drug delivery, enabling targeted treatment for specific conditions or achieving systemic effects for widespread therapeutic benefits.

Therapies in Medicine

Medicine employs various therapies to prevent, treat, or manage diseases and improve overall health. Here are some different therapies used in medicine:

Pharmacotherapy

  • Pharmacotherapy involves the use of medications or drugs to treat diseases or alleviate symptoms.
  • It includes prescription drugs, over-the-counter medications, and other pharmacological interventions.

Physical Therapy

  • Physical therapy aims to restore, maintain, or enhance physical function and mobility.
  • It includes exercises, stretches, manual therapy, and other techniques to improve strength, flexibility, and range of motion.

Chemotherapy

  • Chemotherapy refers to the use of powerful drugs to destroy cancer cells or inhibit their growth and spread.
  • It can be administered orally, intravenously, or through other routes and may be used alone or in combination with other treatments.

Radiation Therapy

  • Radiation therapy utilizes high-energy radiation to target and destroy cancer cells or shrink tumors.
  • It is commonly used in the treatment of various cancers and may be delivered externally (external beam radiation) or internally (brachytherapy).

Psychotherapy

  • Psychotherapy, also known as talk therapy, is a form of mental health treatment that involves communication between a therapist and a patient.
  • It aims to address emotional and psychological issues, promote mental well-being, and improve coping strategies.

Surgical Therapy

  • Surgical therapy involves the use of invasive procedures to diagnose, treat, or manage medical conditions.
  • It includes operations performed by surgeons to remove or repair damaged tissues, organs, or body parts.
