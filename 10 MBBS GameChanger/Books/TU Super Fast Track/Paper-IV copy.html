Write short notes on Down syndrome?
Down syndrome, also known as trisomy 21, is a genetic disorder caused by the presence of all or part of a third copy of chromosome 21. This extra genetic material causes developmental delays and intellectual disabilities. Individuals with Down syndrome may also have physical characteristics such as a small stature, upward slanting eyes, and a single crease across the center of the palm. The severity of Down syndrome varies widely among individuals, but most people with Down syndrome can lead fulfilling lives with proper medical care and educational support.
How does trisomy occur? Explain the clinical features of trisomy 21.
Trisomy 21, also known as Down syndrome, occurs when there is an extra copy of chromosome 21 in every cell of the body. This extra genetic material causes developmental delays, intellectual disabilities, and physical characteristics such as a small stature, upward slanting eyes, and a single crease across the center of the palm. Trisomy 21 is caused by a random error during cell division in the early stages of fetal development, and is not usually inherited from parents. However, the risk of having a child with trisomy 21 increases with maternal age. Prenatal testing is available to detect trisomy 21 during pregnancy.

Describe the microscopic anatomy of bone.
The microscopic anatomy of bone consists of two main types of tissue: compact bone and cancellous bone. Compact bone is the dense outer layer of bone that gives bones their strength and protection. Cancellous bone, also known as spongy bone, is the inner layer of bone that contains small, interconnected spaces filled with bone marrow. Within compact bone, there are microscopic units called osteons that consist of concentric rings of bone tissue called lamellae, which surround a central canal that contains blood vessels and nerves. Between the osteons are small spaces called lacunae that house bone cells called osteocytes, which are connected to each other by tiny channels called canaliculi. These structures work together to maintain the strength and vitality of the bone.

Fertilization is the process by which a sperm cell from a male fuses with an egg cell from a female to form a zygote, which is the first cell of a new organism. During fertilization, the genetic material from the sperm and the egg combine to create a unique genetic makeup for the zygote. This genetic information determines the physical and biological characteristics of the organism that develops from the zygote. The result of fertilization is the formation of a zygote that undergoes cell division and eventually develops into an embryo.
Describe briefly on formation of blastocyst.
The blastocyst is a structure that forms during the early stages of embryonic development, typically around five to six days after fertilization. It is formed when the zygote undergoes multiple rounds of cell division to form a hollow ball of cells with an inner cell mass and an outer layer of cells called the trophoblast. The inner cell mass will eventually develop into the embryo, while the trophoblast will form the placenta and other supportive structures. The blastocyst is an important stage in embryonic development because it is the stage at which the embryo implants into the uterus and begins to receive nutrients from the mother.

The intraembryonic mesoderm also contributes to the formation of the body wall, limbs, and connective tissue.

The embryonic period, also known as the period of organogenesis, is the second period of prenatal development that lasts from the end of the germinal period to the end of the eighth week after fertilization. During this period, the major organs and organ systems of the body are formed, and the embryo takes on a recognizable human form. The embryonic period is characterized by rapid cell division, migration, and differentiation of cells to form the three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm) and their derivatives. The heart begins to beat, and the major organ systems including the nervous system, cardiovascular system, respiratory system, digestive system, and urinary system start to form. Teratogens, such as drugs, alcohol, and infections, can cause significant damage to developing organs and lead to congenital abnormalities.

Define equilibrium potential and explain the ionic basis of action potential of a neuron.
Equilibrium potential is the membrane potential at which there is no net flow of ions across the membrane, and it is determined by the concentration gradient and the electrical gradient of the ions. The ionic basis of action potential in a neuron is due to the movement of ions across the cell membrane, which results in a change in membrane potential. During the resting state, the inside of the neuron is negatively charged relative to the outside, with a resting potential of about -70 mV. When a stimulus is applied, sodium ions enter the cell, causing depolarization of the membrane potential. This depolarization triggers the opening of voltage-gated sodium channels, leading to a rapid influx of sodium ions into the cell, and a reversal of membrane potential. This is followed by the opening of voltage-gated potassium channels, leading to the efflux of potassium ions from the cell, and repolarization of the membrane potential to the resting state. The ionic basis of action potential ensures the transmission of electrical signals in neurons.

Define all or none law.
The all or none law states that when a neuron fires an action potential, it does so at full strength or not at all. If the stimulus is strong enough to reach the threshold potential, an action potential will be generated, but if the stimulus is too weak, the neuron will not fire.

Mention the differences between action potential and graded potential.
Action potential is a rapid and brief all-or-none depolarization of the membrane potential of excitable cells, including neurons and muscle cells, that travels along the length of the cell. It has a fixed amplitude, duration, and threshold, and is triggered by a depolarizing stimulus that reaches the threshold potential. Graded potential, on the other hand, is a slower and smaller change in the membrane potential that can be either depolarizing (excitatory) or hyperpolarizing (inhibitory), depending on the type and strength of the stimulus. It has a variable amplitude, duration, and threshold, and does not propagate along the cell.

Explain the clinical significance of plasma half life of a drug.
Plasma half life of a drug refers to the time it takes for the concentration of the drug in the plasma to decrease by 50%. This parameter is important because it determines the duration of action of the drug, and also influences the dosing frequency required to maintain therapeutic levels of the drug. A drug with a short half life will need to be administered more frequently than a drug with a long half life. Additionally, a long half life may increase the risk of drug accumulation and toxicity, while a short half life may lead to subtherapeutic levels of the drug. Therefore, understanding the plasma half life of a drug is important for optimizing its therapeutic use and avoiding adverse effects.

1. Calling for emergency medical services
2. Administering epinephrine injection immediately
3. Maintaining airway and breathing
4. Giving oxygen, intravenous fluids, and other supportive measures as needed
5. Monitoring the patient closely for recurrence of symptoms

    Differences between local potential and action potential.
    Local potentials are graded changes in membrane potential that occur in response to neurotransmitter binding or sensory stimuli. They can be either depolarizing (EPSP) or hyperpolarizing (IPSP) and are conducted decrementally, meaning they lose strength as they travel along the neuron. They can also summate, meaning multiple inputs can add together to reach the threshold for an action potential. In contrast, action potentials are all-or-nothing events that occur when the membrane potential reaches a certain threshold. They are conducted without decrement and have a fixed amplitude and duration. They also involve the opening and closing of voltage-gated ion channels and require a large influx of Na+ followed by an efflux of K+ to repolarize the membrane.

    A synapse is a specialized junction between two nerve cells or between a nerve cell and its target, where electrical or chemical signals are transmitted. There are four stages in synaptic transmission:
    1. Presynaptic events: Action potentials arrive at the presynaptic terminal, resulting in the opening of voltage-gated calcium channels and the influx of calcium ions into the terminal.
    2. Neurotransmitter release: The calcium influx triggers the fusion of synaptic vesicles with the presynaptic membrane, releasing neurotransmitter molecules into the synaptic cleft.
    3. Postsynaptic events: The neurotransmitter molecules diffuse across the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, leading to the opening or closing of ion channels and the generation of a postsynaptic potential.
    4. Termination: The neurotransmitter is either taken back up into the presynaptic terminal or broken down by enzymes in the synaptic cleft.

    Synaptic inhibition is a process by which the transmission of signals across a synapse is decreased. It can occur through various mechanisms, such as presynaptic inhibition, where the release of neurotransmitter from the presynaptic terminal is reduced, or postsynaptic inhibition, where the responsiveness of the postsynaptic cell to the neurotransmitter is decreased.

The virus is maintained in a cycle between mosquitoes and amplifying hosts, primarily pigs and wading birds, with humans being incidental hosts. The virus does not spread from person to person.

    Write short note on Japanese encephalitis virus.
    Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV) is a mosquito-borne virus that belongs to the family Flaviviridae. It is the leading cause of viral encephalitis in Asia, with an estimated 68,000 clinical cases reported annually. JEV is primarily transmitted by the mosquito species Culex tritaeniorhynchus, which breeds in paddy fields and feeds on pigs and birds, which serve as amplifying hosts.
    Life cycle of Japanese encephalitis virus
    The life cycle of Japanese encephalitis virus involves the following stages:
    1. Attachment: The virus attaches to specific receptors on the surface of host cells.
    2. Entry: The virus enters the host cell by endocytosis.
    3. Replication: The viral RNA is released and translated into viral proteins, which then replicate and assemble into new virus particles.
    4. Release: The new virus particles are released from the host cell by budding.
    5. Transmission: The virus is transmitted to a new host by the bite of an infected mosquito.
