Glenohumeral (Shoulder) Joint

  • The glenohumeral joint is a synovial multiaxial spheroidal joint between the roughly hemispherical head of the humerus and the shallow glenoid fossa of the scapula.
  • It is the most mobile joint in the body and the most frequently dislocated.
  • The anatomy of the glenohumeral joint is a compromise between the requirements for motion and stability.
  • Motion and stability depend on the surrounding muscular and soft tissue envelope more than on the shape and surface area of the articulating surfaces.

Joint Structure

  • The humeral head is held to the concave glenoid fossa by the compressive action of the rotator cuff muscles.
  • The stabilizing mechanism of the glenohumeral joint is one of concavity compression.
  • The rotator cuff muscles are attached to the proximal humerus through a musculotendinous envelope.
  • The deep surface of the musculotendinous envelope fuses with the lateral part of the articular fibrous capsule.
  • The humeral head is contained in a spherical space bounded medially by the glenoid fossa and elsewhere by the deep surface of the fibrous capsule.
  • The fibrous capsule acts as a form of osseofibrous acetabulum.

Anatomical Boundaries

  • The walls of the spherical space containing the glenohumeral joint consist of:
  • → Coracoid anteriorly
  • → Coraco-acromial ligament anterosuperiorly
  • → Acromion and spine of the scapula posteriorly, posterosuperiorly, and posterolaterally
  • → Deep surface of deltoid anterolaterally and laterally
  • This arrangement creates two gliding planes:
  • → Internal gliding plane (glenohumeral articular gliding plane)
  • → External gliding plane (subcoracoid-subacromial-subdeltoid plane)
  • The external gliding plane is continuous with the plane under the scapula (scapulothoracic gliding plane).

Gliding and Restrictions

  • Gliding in the glenohumeral joint is facilitated by the synovial lined cavities of the joint and the subcoracoid, subacromial, and subdeltoid bursae.
  • Infection, inflammation, and injury can cause adhesions and fibrosis in these gliding planes.
  • Adhesions and fibrosis lead to a restriction of motion specific to the site and extent of adhesion.
  • The surfaces of the gliding planes are extensive, and fibrosis can be very restricting.
