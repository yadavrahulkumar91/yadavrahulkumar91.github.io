Inflorescence

Inflorescences are the flamboyant displays of beauty and complexity that flowers use to attract pollinators and ensure their survival.

A. Introduction

Types of Inflorescences

There are several types of inflorescences based on the arrangement of flowers on the peduncle:

  1. Simple raceme
    • Peduncle is unbranched
    • Typical raceme:
      • Peduncle bears stalked or pedicellate flowers.
      • Example: Radish
    • Corymb:
      • Lower flower with long stalk, upper with short stalk so that all the flowers lie in same line.
      • Hence, longest pedicel is of most matured flower.
      • Example: Iberis amara (Candy tuft)
    • Corymbose raceme:
      • Mixture of both typical raceme and corymbose
      • Example: Mustard
    • Spike:
      • Sessile flowers in acropetal succession.
      • Most common type of inflorescence
      • Example: Bottle brush (Callistemon)
    • Catkin/ Amentum:
      • Unisexual spike and sessile and often hanging
      • If all male flower in catkin = Staminate catkin
      • If all female flowers in catkin = Pistillate catkin
      • Examples: Jackfruit, Pineapple, Mulberry, Salix, Betula
    • Umbel:
      • All the flowers arise from same point and have equal length of pedicel.
      • Peduncle is extremely reduced with an involucre.
    • Head/ Capitulum:
      • The peduncle is flattened to form a receptacle that bear small sessile flowers called florets.
      • The florets are arranged in centripetal fashion with ray floret at periphery and disc floret at the centre.
      • The inflorescence is surrounded by one or more whorls of bracts called Involucre.
      • Involucre is found in Capitulum, Cyathium and Umbel.
      • Examples: Tagetes (Marigold), Halianthus annus (Sunflower), Chrysanthemum

      III. Cymose

      1. Monochasial/ Uniparous cyme

        • Terminal branch stops to grow after giving single flower which push lateral branch.

          Growth of lateral branch is also stopped after giving single flower

          1. Helicoid

            • Flower borne in only one side.

            • Eg

              • Begonia
              • Drosera
              • Heliotropium

          2. Scorpoid

            • Flowers are alternately borne on both the sides i.e, zig-zag development of inflorescence axis.

            • Rhipidium is scorpoid cyme having all the flowers in one plane. Eg = Solanum nigrum

            • Eg

              • Freesia
              • Ranunculus
              • Cotton

        • Dichasial cyme/ Biparous (Common type)

          A terminal flower is subtended by two lateral branches, which also end in a flowers.

        • Multichasial cyme/ Polychasial cyme

          Cymose flower borne in more than two sides.

        • Scapigerous cyme umbel

          In onion a scape (a leafless peduncle shoot arising from terminal bud) from ground level bears an umbellate cyme covered by one or more spathes.

      C. Special Inflorescence

      I. Cyathium/ Cup-shaped inflorescence/ Flower like

      It has a cup-like involucre. It looks like a single flower. Single gynoecium represents female flower in the centre which is surrounded by many male flowers. All flowers are enclosed by Involucre or Bract. Ratio of female to male flower = 1:many or 1:5. An inflorescence having a number of achlamydous male flowers surrounding a single achlamydous female flower is Cyathium.

      Eg: Euphorbiaceae (Euphorbia)

      II. Hypanthodium/ Fruit like inflorescence

      Inflorescence axis becomes fleshy and pear-shaped with a hollow cavity inside. It has a flask or cup-shaped fleshy receptacle. After fertilization, it develops into Syconus fruit.

      Eg: Ficus (Fig)

      III. Verticellaster

      Biparous/ Dichasial cyme ending in uniparous cyme i.e., condensed cyme.

      Eg: Labiate family (Ocimum, Salvia, Mint)
