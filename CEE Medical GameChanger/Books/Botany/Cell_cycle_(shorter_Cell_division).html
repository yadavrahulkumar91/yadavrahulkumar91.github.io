Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the never-ending dance of life and death, as cells divide and renew themselves endlessly.

Reason for Cell Cycle/Cell division

  1. A cell divides in order to maintain balance between bulk of cytoplasm and nucleus.

  2. The ratio of nucleus upon the volume of the cytoplasm is called as Nucleocytoplasmic ratio.

    • Nucleocytoplasmic ratio = Vn/Vcyto = VN/(Vcell−VN)

    • In a cell going to divide, nucleocytoplasmic ratio (karyoplasmic ratio) and surface area per unit volume decreases.

    • The increase in nucleocytoplasmic ratio indicated increasing activity of cell division.

    • Smaller cells are metabolically more active than larger cell due to high nucleocytoplasmic ratio.

No. of Chromosomes Same as parent cell Half of parent cell Amount of DNA Same as parent cell Half of parent cell Chromatids per Chromosome 2 2 (before division), 1 (after division)

Every chromosome has 2 chromatids, except daughter chromosome.

Introduction

  • The cell cycle is defined as the sequence of events in which the cell accumulates large amount of nutrition and then divides into daughter cells.
  • The process of forming daughter cells from parent cell is called Cell division.
  • Note: Generation time/ cell cycle time is the total time taken by the parent cell to divide into daughter cell.
  • The sequence of events that occurs between two cell divisions is called Cell cycle.
  • Phases of Cell cycle:
  • Sequences of Cell cycle:
    1. G1 → S → G2 → M
    2. S → G2 → M → G1
    3. G2 → M → G1 → S
    4. M → G1 → S → G2
  • Duration of Cell cycle:
    1. A duration of 16 hours for a particular mammalian cell is distributed as follows.
    4. Special points
      • G1 is the most variable in cell cycle.
      • G2 is the shortest in Interphase. But the shortest phase in cell cycle is Mitotic phase (M-phase).
  • The events occur in cell cycle are:
    1. Replication of DNA
    2. Nuclear division (Karyokinesis)
    3. Cytoplasmic division (Cytokinesis)

Interphase / I-phase/ Non-dividing phase

  • Introduction
    1. Also known as:
      • Resting phase/
        • Though called a resting phase (due to non-dividing nucleus), it is metabolically or physiologically most active phase.
      • Metabolic phase/
      • Growth phase/
      • Preparative phase/
        • Nucleus is largest in size during Interphase and is prepared for division. [IOM 2001]
      • Invisible phase
        • As replicated chromosomes are not visible at this stage.
        • Longest phase of cell cycle.
        • Transcription occurs throughout interphase but the rate of transcription is highest during G1 phase.
        • Q. Transcription occurs during
          1. a. Interphase
          2. b. G1-phase
          3. c. S-phase
          4. d. Prophase

          Answer: b. G1-phase

        G1 phase/ Gap I/ Growth I/ Post mitotic phase/ Pre-DNA synthesis phase

        • Preparatory phase of DNA synthesis
        • Maximum growth occurs in G1 phase.
        • Synthesis of:
          • a) RNA (t-RNA, m-RNA, r-RNA)
            • a. Ribosomes
          • b) Histone protein is started but bulk of histone protein is synthesized during S-phase
          • c) Non-histone proteins i.e, Structural and functional protein occurs.
          • d) Enzymes:
            • a. DNA polymerase
            • b. RNA polymerase
        • G1 phase is absent in Amoeba, Slime moulds and Yeast.
        • Decision of cell division occurs in this phase.
          • a) Non-dividing cell remain in G1 phase.
          • b) Restriction point/ R-point/ Check point:
            • a. It is present in G1 phase.
            • b. When a cell has crossed this point or enter into S-phase, division of such cells become compulsion.

        Go phase/ Quiescent stage/ Quite stage

        • Stage of arrest of cell cycle and onset of differentiation (forming permanent tissue)
        • Cells of quiescent centre in root tip are in Go stage.
        • Skeletal muscle cells, Nerve cell, RBC are in Go phase.

        S Phase

        1. Metabolically most active stage.
        2. Synthesis
          1. Replication/synthesis of nuclear DNA and
            • DNA replication occurs with the help of DNA polymerase enzyme
            • DNA polymerase is active.
          2. Synthesis of histone protein
        3. Also called "Invisible phase" of mitosis as replicated chromosomes are not visible at this stage.
        4. After this phase a cell can enter into cell division without interruption.
        5. Each chromosome has 2 Chromatids.

        G2 Phase

        1. Also called Resting phase of I-phase.
        2. Replication of Cytoplasmic DNA occurs.
        3. Synthesis
          1. Synthesis of required organelle for M-phase.
          2. Synthesis of tubulin protein for spindle apparatus.
        4. Energy storage takes place.
        5. Centrioles are duplicated.
        6. During G2, a cell contains Double the amount of DNA (4n) as compared to original diploid cell (2n).
        7. Note:
          1. Division of Chloroplast and Mitochondria started during G2 phase and continued upto cytokinesis.
          2. In meiotic cell division, G2 phase is very short or almost absent.

        Q. Replication occurs during

        1. G1 phase
        2. S phase

        4. G2 and S phase

        Mitotic Phase/ M phase/ Dividing phase/ Cell division

        Introduction

        1. Shortest phase of cell cycle.
        2. It includes
          • Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)
          • Cytokinesis (Division of cytoplasm)
            • Cell plate: E.g- Plant
            • Constriction or Furrowing E.g: Animal

        3 types of Cell divisions

        1. Amitosis

          1. Also called
            • Direct cell division
              • Because division completes without forming spindle fibre.
            • Incipient cell division
            • Primitive cell division
          2. It is unequal cell division.
          3. It is the main method of division of prokaryotic cell but also occurs in some eukaryotic cell like Saccharomyces (yeast).
          4. A karyokinesis in which the nuclear membrane persists throughout the division cycle in called Amitosis.
          5. E.g: Budding, Fission

        2. Mitosis

          Introduction

          1. Also called
            • Somatic cell division
            • Indirect cell division
            • Homotype cell division
            • Equational cell division
              • Note
                On the basis of DNA, mitosis is Reductional cell division.
          2. Best material for study of mitosis is L.S. of onion root tip.
          3. Mitosis takes place in
            • Unicellular, Multicellular eukaryotes
              • Haploid, Diploid and Polyploid cell
              • Somatic or Reproductive cell
              • Q. Mitosis occurs in
                • Vegetative cells
                • Reproductive cells
                • Mature cells
                • All of these
          4. In mitosis single cell divides into two daughter cells.
          5. The cell which undergo mitosis is called Mitocyte.
          6. No. of mitosis required for the production of n-cells (Daughter cells) from single cells is (n-1).

            7. g) No. of daughter cells produced after 'n' turn of Division, 2n = given no.

            h) Types of Mitosis on the basis of formation of Spindle fibres.

            1. Astral/ Amphiastral mitosis:
              1. The mitosis occurs with the formation of spindle fibres.
              2. Is common in Animals.
            2. Anastral mitosis
              1. The mitosis occurs without the formation of spindle fibres.
              2. Is common in Plants.

            Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)

            • All the 4 stages occur continuously without any stoppage.

            • a) Prophase

              1. 1st and longest stage of mitosis.
              2. Metabolically most active stage of cell division.
                • Note: Interphase is metabolically most active stage of Cell cycle.
              3. Chromatin changes to chromosome i.e., chromosome become visible.
              4. The chromosomes of prophase are called Prochromosomes.
              5. Shows Spiralisation (Plectonemic coiling)
                • Note: Paranemic coiling is found in Meiotic prophase.
                • Question: In which phase of nuclear division is plectonemic coiling found?
                  Meiotic prophase
                  Mitotic prophase
                  Amitotic division
                  Crossing over
              6. Centromere and Chromatids are not visible.
              7. Cell becomes more viscous.
              8. Formation of spindle fibre is started during late prophase.
                • Spindle fibres are made of 97% tubulin protein and 3% RNA.
              9. Nuclear membrane, nucleoplasm and nucleolus disappear during late prophase.

            • b) Metaphase

              1. Chromosome are thickest with visible centromere and visible chromatids. So, it is the best stage to study chromosome. (Metaphase II > Metaphase I)
              2. Chromosomes are Shortest in metaphase.
              3. Chromosomes are arranged in single line at equator (Metakinesis). So, single metaphasic plate/ equatorial plate is formed.
              4. Structure, size and counting of number of chromosome is done at this stage.
              5. No. of chromatids in a chromosome at metaphase is 2 in mitosis and 2 in meiosis.
              6. Spindle fibre is well developed.
              7. Spindle fibre of opposite pole is attached to the kinetochore or primary constriction (of centromere) of chromosome.
              8. Idiogram/ Karyotype of chromosomes is prepared.

            • c) Anaphase/ Migratory phase

              1. Shortest stage, inert (less active stage of cell division).
              2. Shape of chromosome can be studied.

                3. The shape of chromosomes in anaphase are V of J or L or I shaped, based on position of centromere. Centromere is divided so chromatids are separated. Sister chromatid move towards opposite pole. No. of chromatid in a chromosome of anaphase is One. Poleward movement occus due to:

                • Spindle fibre
                • Centromere
                The main kinetics for poleward is generated by centromere.

                Note: The no. of chromosomes remains same but no. of chromatid is reduced to half in anaphase in comparison to metaphase.

                Telophase

                1. Also called Re-organisation phase.
                2. It is the reverse or Prophase.
                  • Nuclear membrane and Nucleous reappears.
                  • Chromosome become decondensed, uncoiled and reach to opposite poles.
                  • Spindle fibres disappears, chromosome reorganise and cell resumes its metabolic activity.
                3. It results in the formation of two daughter nuclei with same no. of chromosome and same amount of DNA.

                Calcium ion promote anaphase

                2) Cytokinesis (Cytoplasm division)

                a) By cell plate: (In Plant)

                1. During cell plate formation, spindle fibre aggregate at equator basically Golgi body initiate the cell plate formation which later converted into Phragmoplast and Ultimately cell wall.
                2. Plane of cell plate is determined by Microtubules.
                3. Growth of cell plate occurs in Centrifugal manner.

                b) Furrowing (In Animal)

                1. Plane of furrowing is determined by Microfilament.
                2. Growth of furrows occur in Centripetal manner.

                SPECIAL POINT:

                1. The daughter cells have same amount of DNA and Same no. of chromosome in mitosis.
                2. C-Mitosis (Colchicine treated mitosis)
                  1. Colchicine called 'mitotic poison' because it causes temporary cesation of mitotic division by inhibiting the formation of spindle fibres by breaking microtubule.
                  2. Thus, it produces polyploidy by arresting cell division in metaphase.
                3. Uncontrolled mitosis leads to Neoplasia.
                4. Questions
                  1. How many mitotic divisions will require to form 64 cells?

                    Solution: No. of divisions = n - 1 = 64 - 1 = 63 divisions

                  2. How many times or generations a single cell divides mitotically to form 32 cells.

                    Solution: 2^n = 32, or 2^6 = 32, n = 6 generations

                  3. A bacteria cell divides in every 20 minutes. How many bacteria will be formed after 2 hrs.

                    Solution: No. of 20 minutes in 2 hrs = (2 x 60) / 20 = 6, No. of bacteria after 6 mitotic division = 2^6 = 64

                Note:

                1. A bacteria cell divides in every 1 min. and takes one hour to fill a cup. Then the time taken to fill half of the cup is 59 minute.
                2. A bacteria cell divides in every 3 minute and takes one hour to fill a cup. The time taken to fill half of the cup is 57 min.

                Significance of Mitosis

                1. It is responsible for growth, maintenance ⛑ and repair of somatic cells.
                2. It is helpful in maintaining genetic stability.
                3. Avoids ageing by replacing old cells.
                4. Produce genetically identical cells resembling the zygote.
                5. Restores surface area volume ratio and nucleocytoplasmic ratio high by dividing mature cells into two smaller cells.

                Meiosis/ Reductional Cell Division

                Introduction

                1. Common in germ cells/ reproductive cells.
                2. Single cell divides to form 4 daughter cells.
                3. Occurs in pollen mother cell.
                4. The daughter cells produced at the end of meiosis when compared to the parent diploid cells will have
                  1. Same amount of DNA and
                  2. Half no. of chromosome
                5. Two divisions of nucleus and only one division of chromosome in meiosis.
                6. Best material for study of meiosis is Anthers of Onion or Tradescantia.
                7. Meiosis occurs at any stage of life-cycle.
                8. Cell involves in meiosis is called meiocyte cell (divides once in life times).
                9. Meiosis is never followed by meiosis.
                10. Meiosis can be followed by mitosis.
                11. In alternation of generation, meiosis is always followed by fusion (fertilization).
                12. Meiosis takes place in Diploid and Polypoids and never in haploid.
                13. A single primary spermatocyte (2n) and a single primary oocyte (2n) will respectively produce 4 sperms and 1 egg. But, a single secondary spermatocyte (n) and a single secondary oocyte (n) give 2 sperms and 1 egg 🥚 respectively.

                Two. Zygotene

                • a. Synapsis occurs.
                  • 1. Lengthwise pairing of homologous chromosomes in a very precise manner i.e., chromomere to chromomere is called as Synapsis or Syndesis or Zipping.
                  • 2. Synapsis occurs in zipper-like fashion and may begin at centromere or at ends of chromosome or at any point.
                  • 3. Synapsis is favoured by Synaptonemal complex (protein).
                    • 1. Synaptonemal complex is Tripartite.
                  • 4. Each pairing of homologous chromosomes is called bivalent which exist in the form of Dyad (2 stranded stage).
                • b. Thickening and Shortening of chromosome occur.
                • c. Note:
                  • 1. Bivalent is produced in diploid and allopolyploid.
                  • 2. Multivalent is produced in autopolyploid.
                  • 3. No. of bivalents =1/2 the no. of total chromosomes in a diploid cell.

                Three. Pachytene (Longest in Prophase-I)

                • a. Most important sub-stage of Prophase-I.
                • b. Thickest chromosome.
                • c. Exchange of genetic material/ chromatid segments/ DNA/ genes (i.e., Crossing over) takes place.
                • d. Crossing over occurs at 4-stranded stage.
                • e. Occurrence of single, double and triple crossing over is in the order.
                  Single > Double > Triple
                • f. Crossing over is favoured by 3 main enzymes.
                  • 1. Endonuclease: For breakdown (Nicking)
                  • 2. Unwindase: For separation
                  • 3. Ligase: For rejoining
                • g. Equal exchange of segments occurs between non-sister chromatids (homologous chromatids) of homologous chromosome during crossing over.
                  • Note: Crossing over is the major source of continuous variation in sexually reproducing organisms. But the ultimate source of variation is mutation, which produces discontinuous variation.
                • h. Synapsis and synaptonemal complex and bivalent becomes mature.
                • i. Sister chromatids become visible thus bivalents exists in the form of tetrad i.e., Tetrad formation occurs in Pachytene.
                  • 1. Tetrad contains 2 homologous chromosome, 4 chromatids, 2 centromere and 4 kinetochore.
                • j. Morphological details of the chromosome are best studied.

                b. Metaphase-I

                1. Paired chromosomes arrange themselves at equatorial plane (congression).
                2. Chromosomes arranged in two distinct lines (i.e., Double metaphase plate is formed).
                3. Appearance of Spindle fibres.
                4. Chromosome at Metaphase-I consists of 2 chromatids and 1 centromere.

                c. Anaphase-I

                1. It is the stage of separation of maternal and paternal chromosomes of homologous pair also called disjunction and the separated chromosomes are called univalents or dyads.
                2. Centromere is not divide.
                3. No. of chromosomes is reduced to half but no. of chromatids remain same in anaphase-I in comparison to metaphase-I.

                d. Telophase-I

                1. Similar to mitotic telophase.

                b) Cytokinesis (Cytoplasm division)

                Interkinesis:

                1. It is brief intrameiotic interphase, but without S-phase (DNA synthesis does not occur).
                2. Time period between meiosis-I and miosis-II is called Interkinesis.

                2) Meiosis-II

                • Similar to that of mitosis and so called meiotic mitosis.

                • a) Karyokinesis (Nuclear division)

                  1. Prophase-II
                  2. Metaphase-II
                  3. Anaphase-II
                  4. Telophase-II

                • b) Cytokinesis (Cytoplasm division)

                  1. Successive type:

                    Both meiosis I and miosis II are followed by wall formation and lead to formation of isobilateral tetrads.

                  2. Simultaneous type:

                    Meiosis I is not followed by wall formation but wall formation occurs simultaneously after meiosis II and leads to formation of tetrahedral tetrads.

                • Significance of Meiosis

                • Maintains the no. of chromosome constant in sexually reproducing organisms.
                • Recombination occurs as a result of crossing over which have role in evolution.
                • Meiosis shows basic relationship among the organism.

                Note:
