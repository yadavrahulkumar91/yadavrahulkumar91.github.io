Stomata

Water relations are crucial to plant survival, as they balance the competing demands of hydration and photosynthesis.

Stomata

Stomata are minute pores found on the epidermis of leaves. The number of stomata per cm2 of a common leaf is about 1000.

Structure of Stomata

Stomata consist of:

  • Guard cells: These are specialized epidermal cells that are kidney-shaped in dicots and dumb-bell-shaped in monocots. The thickest wall among the inner, outer, and lateral walls of guard cells is the inner wall.
  • Subsidiary cells: These are surrounding cells that support the movement of guard cells.

Note:

  1. Chloroplasts are present in the guard cells but are absent in other epidermal cells. However, the chloroplasts in guard cells are capable of poor photosynthesis because there is no RUBP-carboxylase enzyme. Both PS-I and PS-II are present in the chloroplasts of guard cells.
  2. In the crucifer type of stomata, there are three subsidiary cells.
  3. Sunken stomata reduce the rate of transpiration.

Types of Stomata

Stomata can be classified based on their distribution and light response:

  • Based on distribution:
    • Apple or Mulberry type: Stomata are found only on the lower/dorsal surface. Example: Trees
    • Potato type: Stomata are more common on the lower surface than the upper surface. This is the most common type of stomata and is found in dorsiventral leaves.
    • Oat type: Stomata are equally distributed on both surfaces. Example: Isobilateral leaves, grasses
    • Water lily type: Stomata are found only on the upper surface. Example: Free-floating hydrophytes
    • Potamogeton type: Stomata are absent or vestigial. Example: Submerged hydrophytes
  • Based on light response:
    • Photoactive: Stomata open during the daytime. Example: Usual plants
    • Scotoactive: Stomata open during the nighttime. Example: Succulent xerophytes (CAM plants) such as Opunita and Bryophyllum
Opening and Closing of Stomata
  1. Stomata open → Guard cell becomes turgid.
    • Note: Stomata open when guard cells show increase in both osmotic and turgor pressures.
  2. Stomata close → Guard cell becomes flaccid.
  3. Theories
    • Starch-Sugar hypothesis
      • Proposed by Lloyd and elaborated by Sayre and Steward.
      • Accordingly, stomatal opening and closing mainly depends on activity of enzyme Starch phosphorylase in different pH.
        • Starch (Stomata close) (⇄┴(pH High)┬(pH low) Sugar (Stomata open)
      • Conversion of starch to organic acids is required for stomatal opening.
    • Potassium malate theory/ Active K+ exchange mechanism
      • Most accepted theory on mechanism of opening and closing of stomata.
      • Given by Fujino and later modified by Levitt.
      • Opening of stomata during day time
      • Special point:
        • Influx of K+ into guard cell → Stomata opens
        • Outflux of K+ from guard cell → Stomata closes
        • Opening of stomata is active process. But closing of stomata is a passive process.
        • Transpiration in old stems and fruits occur through lenticels.

A. Mineral nutrition in Plants:

  1. Green plants prepare their organic food from inorganic substances which they take from soil in the form of minerals, known as mineral elements or mineral nutrients and this nutrition is called mineral nutrition.
  2. Analysis of plant ash shows that about 92 mineral elements are present in different plants. Out of these, 30 elements are present in each and every plant. Out of these 30, 16 elements are necessary for plants and are called essential elements, which are as: C, H, O, N, P, S, K, Mg, Ca, Fe, Cu, B, Zn, Mn, Mo and Cl.
  3. Essential elements are divided into 2 main types:
    • a. Macro elements/ Major elements:
      • These are required by plants in larger amounts.
      • Eg: H, C, N, O, P, S, K, Mg, Ca
    • b. Micro elements/ Minor elements/ Trace elements:
      • These are required by plants in very small amounts, i.e., in traces
      • Eg: Fe, Cu, B, Zn, Mn, Mo, Cl.
      • [The elements having atom no>20 are micro elements except Boron and Chlorine].
  4. C, H and O are the non-mineral elements. Plants obtain C in the form of atmospheric CO2, H mainly from H2O and O is obtained from air or H2O.
  5. Tracer elements: These are radioactive isotopes of elements which are used to detect various metabolic pathways in plants. Eg: C14,N15, P32,S35, etc
  6. Critical elements: Critical elements are the elements in which soil is generally deficient. Eg: N, P "and" K. These are given in the form of chemical fertilizer.
  7. C, H, O(Framework elemetns): They constitute the carbohydrates which form the cell wall.
  8. Protoplasmic elements: N, P, S form part of protoplasm along with C, H, O.
  9. Balancing elements: Ca, Mg and K counteracts the toxic effect of other minerals by ion balancing.
  10. In addition to 16 essential elements, some plants requires some more essential elements. These are
    • a. Silica - Found in grasses and diatoms.
    • b. Sodium - Found in algae and microbes.
    • c. Aluminium - Found in ferns and Lycopodium.
    • d. Iodine - Found in marine algae.
  11. Special points:
    • a. Molybdenum is the least required micronutrient for the plants.
    • b. Nitrogen is derived from both mineral and non-minerals sources.

    Specific Roles of Different Elements

    1. Nitrogen (N):
      • Chief source of nitrogen for plants is nitrates of Ca and K.
      • Constituent of proteins, nucleic acids, vitamins, hormones, coenzymes, ATP, etc.
      • Deficiency causes: Stunted growth, Lower respiration rate, Chlorosis of older leaves, Premature leaf fall.
    2. Sulphur (S):
      • It is absorbed as sulphates.
      • Constituents of certain proteins, vitamins (thiamine, biotin, CoA) and ferrodoxin.
      • Deficiency causes: Chlorosis of younger leaves.
    3. Phosphorus (P):
      • It is absorbed as phosphate.
      • Important constituent of nucleic acid, cell membrane, certain protein and nucleotides required for phosphorylation reactions.
      • Deficiency causes: Premature leaf fall.
    4. Calcium (Ca):
      • It is absorbed in the form of its nitrates and sulphates.
      • Involved in selective permeability of cell membranes, activates certain enzymes, required for development of stem and root apex and as calcium pectate in the middle lamella of the cell wall.
      • Deficiency causes: Disintegration of growing meristem.
    5. Magnesium (Mg):
      • It is important constituent of Chlorophyll.
      • Note: In chlorophyll magnesium is in the form of Mg2+.
      • It helps in binding of ribosomal particles where protein synthesis occurs.
      • Deficiency causes: Chlorosis.
    6. Potassium:
      • It is absorbed as its nitrate and chloride.
      • It is essential for process of photosynthesis and respiration.
      • It plays important role in stomatal movement.
      • Deficiency causes: Mottled chlorosis of leaves, plant growth inhibition.
    7. Iron (Fe):
      • It is absorbed by plant mostly in ferrous form.
      • It is a part of cytochrome, hence play important role in ETS, Photosynthesis and Respiration. It is also essential for chlorophyll synthesis.
    8. Boron (B):
      • It is absorbed as borate.
      • It helps in translocation of food in plants.
      • Deficiency causes: Death of shoot tip, Suppression of flower formation, Stunted root growth, Brown heart disease.
    9. 9) Manganese (Mn):
      • It is absorbed as its oxide. It is activator of several enzymes of Kreb's cycle like malic dehydrogenase.
      • It is responsible for photolysis of water during photosynthesis, synthesis of chlorophyll and IAA.
      • iii. ETS reactions are dependent n Mn.
      • iv. Deficiency causes: Grey spots in leaves
      • 10) Zinc (Zn):
      • It is essential for synthesis of Tryptophan amino acids (which form IAA).
      • Deficiency causes: Chlorosis of older leaves
      • Khaira disease of rice and white bud of maize is due to Zinc deficiency. Zinc is absorbed by Foliage.

        • Essential Elements

        1. Copper (Cu):
          1. Its deficiency causes Necrosis of tip of new leaves.
        2. Molybdenum (Mo):
          1. It is responsible for modulation in legumes.
          2. Deficiency causes inhibition of fruit formation.
          3. Whiptail disease of cauliflower is caused by Mo deficiency.
        3. Chlorine (Cl):
          1. It has important role in photosynthesis, specially in light reaction, i.e., helps in photolysis of water.
          2. With Na+ and K+ helps in maintaining solute concentraiton and ionic balance in cells.
          3. Deficiency: Wilting of leaf tip followed by chlorosis, necrosis (death of tissues).

        Liebig's Law of Minimum

        It states that the yield of the crop plant is determined by the amount of that essential elements, which is present in minimum quantity, in proportion to demand of the plants.
