Error Types

One of the ways to quantify the uncertainty in a measurement is by calculating the error. The formula for calculating the value of X is:

X = (xayb)/zc

where a, b and c are constants, and x, y, and z are the measured quantities.

The relative error for X can be calculated using the following formula:

((Î”X)/X) = a((Î”x)/x) + b((Î”y)/y) + c((Î”z)/z)

where Î”x, Î”y, and Î”z are the uncertainties in the measurements of x, y, and z, respectively.

There are three types of errors:

  1. Random error: This type of error is caused by unpredictable variations in the measurement process, such as noise or fluctuations in the measurement equipment.
  2. Systematic error: This type of error is caused by a consistent bias in the measurement process. It can be caused by factors such as a miscalibrated instrument or an incorrect measurement technique.
  3. Gross error: This type of error is caused by a mistake or a malfunction in the measurement process, such as a reading that is clearly out of range or a misread instrument.

Systematic errors are further classified into:

  1. Instrumental error: This type of error is caused by a flaw in the measuring instrument or equipment. For example, an instrument that is not calibrated correctly may produce a systematic error in the measurements.
  2. Method error: This type of error is caused by a flaw in the measurement method or technique. For example, if a measurement requires a certain amount of time to stabilize, but the measurement is taken too quickly, a systematic error may occur.
  3. Environmental error: This type of error is caused by external factors that affect the measurement process. For example, changes in temperature or humidity may affect the measurement and cause a systematic error.
