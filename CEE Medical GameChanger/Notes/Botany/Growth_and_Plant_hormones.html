Growth and Plant hormones

Growth and plant hormones control every aspect of plant development, from the roots to the flowers and beyond.

Growth

I. Sequential process involved in growth:
  § Cell division → Cell enlargement → Cell differentiation

II. Maximum cell elongation occurs in region behind the root/shoot.

III. Growth is Quantitative change.
  1. But, Development is Qualitative + Quantitative

IV. Growth is primarily affected by two climatic factors which are light and temperature.

V. During growth, there is an increase in dry weight, permanent change in size, volume, the number of cells.

VI. Growth curve → 'S' shaped sigmoid curve.
  1. Plant growth
  2. Bacterial growth in the lab

VII. Growth is measured by Auxanometer, Horizontal microscope, Crescograph.

VIII. The classical experiments in growth were performed by Boysen-Jensen and Darwin.

IX. Phases of Growth = 4
  1. Lag phase → Initiating phase, Slow cell division.
  2. Log phase (exponential) → Maximum growth, Fast cell division.
  3. Deaccelerating phase → Decreased in growth rate.
  4. Stationary phase → Constant growth

A. Growth promoter phytohormones

  • Growth regulators are the organic substances which are required for the growth and are called Hormones.
  • The term hormone was given by "Starling".
  • The existence of growth promoting substance in plant was given by Darwin â†’ Observed in Canary grass (Phalaris canariensis)
  • Nature of Hormone:
    • Naturally produced organic substance.
    • Active in minute amount.
    • Acts at a site remote from its production i.e., they are produced in one part and transferred to some other part where their physiological effects are observed.
    • May have promotory or Inhibitory role.
    • Hormones are used up after biochemical reaction while enzymes are unchanged after biochemical reaction.
    • Hormones are synthesized in meristematic region of plants.
  • Hormones were originally measure with bioassays but now most hormones are measured by Gas chromatography and Immunoassay technique.
  • All three growth promoter hormones are required for tissue culture.

Examples and Structures

  1. ABA is mildly acidic hormone formed from Xanthophyll and Mevalonic acid.

    1. Produced in Mature stem, Leaf, Fruit and Seed.

    2. Most abundantly formed in chloroplast and are transported through all the parts of the plants by diffusion. It can be produced from Isolated chloroplast.

  • Synthesized from amino acid Methionine (precursor molecule). It is the only Gaseous hormone.

    1. Synthesis is promoted by O2 and Inhibited by CO2.

    2. Also known as Ripening hormone. Synthesized in large amount by tissues undergoing senescence and ripening fruits.

    3. Produced at root and shoot meristem.

    4. Ethylene is connected with Climacteric respiration.

    Functions
    • ABA is a natural growth inhibitor.
    • It hastens the process of senescence in leaves and leads to falling of leaves (Abscission).
    • Maintains dormancy of buds, seeds.
    • Causes closing of stomata, so also called Anti-transpirant.
    • It inhibits the conversion of starch to sugar by blocking the action of Gibberellins.
      • Functionally antagonistic to Gibberellins.
    • It inhibits the activity of cambium.
    • Protect plants from frost (i.e., low temperature), injury and desiccation.
