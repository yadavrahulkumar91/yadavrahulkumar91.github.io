Plant Movement

Growth and plant hormones control every aspect of plant development, from the roots to the flowers and beyond.

Plant Hormones and their Functions

  1. Caline/ Formative hormone

    Hormone responsible for root, shoot and leaf formation is called Caline/ Formative hormone.

    • Rhizocaline = Hormone responsible for Root formation.
    • Caulocaline = Hormone responsible for Shot formation.
    • Phyllocaline = Hormone responsible for Leaf formation.

  2. Traumatic acid/ Wound hormone

    Causes callus formation on injured part.

  3. Florigen

    Hormone responsible for flowering is Florigen.

    Florigen is produced in the region of leaves.

  4. Vernalin

    Hormone responsible for flowering under cold condition is called Vernalin.

Some terms

  1. Vernalization

    • Low temp. (0-4°C)
    • Chilling treatment for early flowering in some winter annuals like wheat.
    • Cold stimulus for vernalization → Perceived by shoot meristem
    • Light stimulus for Photoperiodism → Perceived by leaf

  2. Senescence

    It is the process of ageing which is caused by increased entropy, cellular breakdown, reduced homeostasis, increased metabolic failure and errors of replication as well as transcription.

  3. Etiolation

    A green plant will turn yellow if placed in the dark. This is called Etiolation.

  4. Phytochrome system

    • Photomorphogenetic pigment in plants is Phytochrome system.
    • Detects the light.

  5. Phytotron

    Device by which plants are grown in controlled condition/ environment.

  1. Living plants have the characteristic of showing some changes in their position due to changes in the environment or due to some endogenous causes. Such changes are called movements.

  2. These movements are due to sensitivity or irritability of protoplasm.

  3. Stimulus, may be defined as any change in the external environment which produces a reaction (response) in the plant or plant organ.

    1. The minimum stimulus intensity required to induce a response is known as threshold intensity.

    2. According to the nature of the stimulus, the movement may be

      1. autonomous (spontaneous), due to internal stimuli or

      2. paratonic (induced), due to external stimuli like light, temperature and chemical.

        1. Stimuli Prefix

          1. Light 1. Photo

          2. Temperature 2. Thermo

          3. Water 3. Hydro

          4. Chemical 4. Chemo

          5. Water current 5. Rheo

          6. Gravity 6. Geo

          7. Touch/ Contact 7. Thigmo/Hapto [IOM, 2005, MOE 2003]

          8. Shock 8. Seismo

  4. The visible change or reaction of a plant or plant organ as a result of stimuli is called response.

  5. The interval between the presentation of the stimulus and the actual development of response is known as reaction time/presentation time.

  6. Movement is not confined to the organism as a whole but occurs down to the cellular level.

    1. E.g.: The cytoplasm of living cells constantly flows like a river in the cells, this streaming motion is called cyclosis.

  7. Hygroscopic movement:

    • Found in bryophytes and pteridophytes.

    • E.g.: Twisting movement of elater, Sporangial wall of fern.

Autonomic/ Self Movement

  1. Amoeboid:

    • Movement due to pseudopodia like structure.
    • Eg: Synchytrium (Slime mould)

  2. Ciliary:

    • Movement due to cilia, flagella.
    • Eg: Chlamydomonas, Volvox

  3. Cyclosis/ Streaming Movement:

    • Protoplasmic streaming movements are referred as autonomic movements of locomotion.
    • Chloroplasts moving by cytoplasmic streaming in the cells of the aquatic plant Elodea

      1. Rotation

        • Movement either in clockwise or anticlockwise direction i.e., movement in single direction.
        • Eg: Leaf of Hydrilla and Vallisnaria

      2. Circular

        • Movement both in clockwise and anticlockwise direction i.e., movement in all possible direction.
        • Eg: Staminal hairs of Tradescantia

Paratonic/ Induced Movement

Also called Tactic/ Taxis movement.

  1. Phototactic:

    • Stimulus: Light
    • Eg: Zoospores of Chlamydomonas and Volvox.
    • Moves from dark to diffused light.
    • Also moves from intense to diffused light (or vice versa).

  2. Thermotactic:

    • Stimulus: Temperature
    • Moves from cold water to warm water and vice-versa.
    • Eg: Chlamydomonas and bacteria

  3. Chemotactic:

    • Stimulus: Chemicals (Malic acid)
    • Eg: Movement of antherozoid from antheridium to archegonium in Bryophytes and Pteridophytes.

  4. Rheotactic:

    • Stimulus: Water current
    • Eg: Aquatic algae

Change in position without growth

I. Autonomic:

  • Eg: Leaves of Desmodium gyrans (Indian telegraph plant)
  • The Telegraph Plant
  • Lateral leaflets always moves up and down.

II. Paratonic/ Induced/ Nastic movement:

  1. Photonasty:
    • Eg: Head of sunflower 🌻 → Helionasty
  2. Thermonastic:
    • Stimulus: Temperature
    • Eg: Tulip 🌷 (Flowering occurs at high temperature), Crocus flower.
  3. Nictynastic (Sleep movement):
    • Light and temperature both.
    • Sleep movement of leaves in these plants (Photonasty) is due to decrease in the intensity of light.
    • Eg:
      • Oxalis and Albizzia flowers are opened in day and closed at night.
      • Oenothera open at night and closed in day.
  4. Seismonastic/ Osmotic/ Turgor movement:
    • a) Eg: Leaves of Mimosa pudica (Touch me not plant)
    • Leaves of Mimosa droops down on touching because of loss of water from cells to intercellular spaces in pulvinus and pulvinules.
  5. Thigmonastic/ Haptonastic:
    • Stimulus: Touch or Contact
    • Eg: Insectivorous plants (movement of hairs in Drosera).

Plant Tropisms

3) Hydrotropic:

  • Stimulus → Water, important for young roots
  • Roots → +ve hydrotropic
  • Shoots → -ve hydrotropic

4) Thigmotropic/ Haptotropic:

  • Stimulus → Touch or Contact
  • Eg:
    • Tendrils bending on a support
    • Haustoria in Cuscuta

5) Chemotropic:

  • Stimulus → Chemical (Malic acid)
  • Eg: Growth of pollen tube towards direction of embryo sac
