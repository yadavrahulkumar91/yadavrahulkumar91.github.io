Mutation and Polyploidy

Mutation and polyploidy drive the evolution of life, as new traits emerge and old ones disappear over time.

    Mutation and Variation

    1. Allelic Variation

      Allelic genes are produced by mutation.

    2. Root Cause of Variation

      Mutation is the ultimate cause of variation.

    3. Major Cause of Variation

      Sexual reproduction is the major cause of variation.

    4. Mutation as the Basic Cause of Evolution

      Mutation is the basic cause of evolution.

    5. Mutation as Natural or Induced

      Mutation can be natural or induced (by using mutagens).

    6. Mutagens

      1. Mutagen → which causes mutation.

      2. Physical Mutagens

        1. Temperature
        2. Radiation
          • Ionizing with high penetration power
            • X-rays
            • γ-rays
          • Non-ionizing with low penetration power
            • UV-rays

      3. Chemical Mutagens

        1. Mustard gas
        2. Acridine dye
        3. Colchicine
        4. Nitric oxide (NO)
        5. Maleic hydrazide
        6. Hydroxylamine
        7. Ethyl methane sulfonate [EMS]
          • EMS is used as mutagen in crop plants.
      4. H. J. Muller 1st produced induced mutation in Drosophila by using X-ray.
      5. H.J. Muller is called as father of actinobiology. (= study of effect of rays on organism)
      6. X-rays, β-rays used as mutagens in seeds, stem cutting etc. (i.e. where nucleus is deep seated)
      7. UV rays used in micro-organisms, pollen, etc. (i.e. where nucleus is superficially seated).
      8. First chemical mutagen was made by C. Auerbach.

    7. Frequency of Natural Mutation

      The frequency of natural mutation is very low (1 × 10-5 - 1 × 10-6).

      8. Mutation and Mutational Studies

      1. Mutation is forward - if wild type changes to new.
      2. Mutation is reverse - if mutated type changes to wild.
      3. Best material to study mutation is haploid.
      4. Organisms frequently used for mutational studies are:
        • Drosophila (fruit fly)
        • Neurospora → fungi belongs to Ascomycetes.
        • E. coli and bacteriophage.
      5. Ascospores are haploid.
      6. Drosophila is diploid (2n = 8).
      7. Neurospora is also called pink mould.
      8. Neurospora is also called Drosophila of plant kingdom.
      9. Note: Beadle and Tatum discovered one gene one enzyme hypothesis while working on Neurospora. They were awarded Nobel prize in 1958 for this work.
      10. One gene one polypeptide theory was given by Yanofsky.

      B. Type of Mutation

      I. Gene mutation/ Point mutation

      1. 1. Frame shift mutation

        When maximum or all codons of a gene are changed, it is called as Frameshift mutation. Such mutation occurs when deletion or insertion occurs at initial point of gene or in internal position of gene. If addition occurs at the terminal point of gene, it causes no change. Most dangerous type since it causes change in all successive polypeptide chain.

        • a. Deletion
        • b. Addition/ Insertion

      2. 2. Substitution mutation

        Due to replacement of nucleotides.

        • a. Transition
          • Purine by purine/ Pyridine by pyridine
        • b. Transversion
          • Purine by pyridine or vice-versa
          • Cause of point mutation for the origin of gene of sickle-shaped anaemia is Transversion.

      II. Chromosomal mutation

      1. 1. Change in structure of Chromosome

        • a. Deletion - Loss of a part of chromosome (loss of no. of gene).
        • b. Inversion - During inversion deleted part of chromosome is reinserted in reverse order. Angle of twist during inversion is 180°.
        • c. Duplication - Deleted part of chromosome is joined to the homologous chromosome. Duplication is always possible between homologous chromosome.
        • d. Translocation - Deleted chromosome is joined to non-homologous chromosome.

      2. Change in no. of chromosome

      a. Aneuploidy

      • Change in Individual no. of chromosome

        • i. Hypoaneuploidy

          • a) Nullisomic (2n–2)

            • Loss of one pair of homologous chromosome from 2n.
            • Nullisomics don't survive.

          • b) Double monosomic (2n–1–1)

            • Loss of two extra dissimilar chromosomes in 2n.

          • c) Monosomic (2n–1)

            • Loss of one chromosome from 2n.
            • Turner's syndrome (44+XO)
              • Loss of one X-chromosome in female results Turner's syndrome.

            • Q. If no. of chromosomes in 2n body is 14. How many monosomics are possible?

              • Ans: 7; Possible monosomic are always 'n' no. of chromosome.

        • ii. Hyperaneuploidy

          • a) Trisomy (2n+1)

            • Patau's syndrome - Trisomy of 13th chromosome
            • Edward's syndrome - Trisomy of 18th chromosome
            • Down's syndrome/ Mongolism - Trisomy of 21th chromosome

          • ® Sex chromosomal trisomy

            • Super female(Sterility) (XXX)
            • Klinefelter's syndrome (XXY)
            • Super male/ Male criminal (XYY)
              • i) Double trisomy (2n+1+1)

          • b) Tetrasomy (2n+2)

            • Super female (XXXX)

      Euploidy

      Change occurs in multiples of haploid no. i.e., in entire set of chromosome.

      1. Homoploidy (n)
      2. Polyploidy
        • Autopolyploid(AAAA)/(BBB)/(CCCCC)
          • Multiple sets are from the same genome (n)
          • Autotriploid (AAA)
            • Banana
            • Grapes
            • Doob grass
          • Autotetraploid (AAAA)
            • Barseem
        • Allopolyploids(AAA'A')/(AABB)
          • Multiple sets are from different species.
          • Allotetraploid(AAA'A')
            • Raphano brassica
          • Allohexaploid(AAA'A'A"A")
            • Triticum spelta (Wheat)
        • Autoallopolyploids(AAAAA'A')/(AAABBB)

      Note:

      • Autotriploids are always sterile.
      • Triticale is the first man-made cereal crop.
      • Autopolyploids form multivalent during zygotene by synapsis.
      • Allopolyploids form bivalents.
