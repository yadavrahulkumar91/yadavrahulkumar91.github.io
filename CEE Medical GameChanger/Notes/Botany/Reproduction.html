D. Pollination

Types

  • i. Geitenogamy = Flower of same plant
  • ii. Xenogamy = Flowers of different plant
  • iii. Hybridization = Flowers of different species plant.

Features

  1. Maintains purity of race.
  2. Less wastage of pollen grains.
  3. Progeny of offsprings are healthier.
  4. New varieties may be produced.
  5. Adaptability is better.

Contrivances

  1. Bisexual/Monocliny
  2. Cleistogamy (Closed flower)
    • a) Arachis hypogea (Groundnut)
    • b) Pisum sativum (Pea)
    • c) Oxalis
    • d) Commelina benghalensis has both cleistogamy and chasmogamy flower.
    • Cleistogamy is accompanied by Geocarpy.
  3. Homogamy
    • Male and female part develop at same time.
  4. Unisexual/Dicliny
    • Monoecious = Same plant
    • Dioecious = Different plant
    • E.g. a. Mango a. Carica papaya (Papaya)
    • b. Maize b. Cannabinus sativa (Ganga)
    • c. Cucurbit c. Vallisneria
    • d. Castor
  5. Chasmogamy (Open flower)
  6. Dichogamy
    • Male and female part develop at different time.
    • a) Protandry = Male develops first
    • b) Protogyny = Female develops first
  7. Heterostyly
    • Stamens, style and stigma are at different height.
  8. Herkogamy
    • Natural barrier between anthers and stigma.
    • E.g. a. Gynostegium disc in Calotropis.
    • i. Best herkogamy is seen in Calotropis.
    • ii. In Calotropis, pollen grains occur in sacs called pollinea.
    • iii. The union of stigma with pollinea is called gynostegium.
  9. Self sterility/Self incompatibility
    • Pollen fails to germinate on the stigma of same flower due to some physiological or genetical reasons.
    • 7) Pollen pre-potency
    • Pollen gains of one flower germinate on the stigma of another flower move rapidly than the same one.

    E. Fertilization

    a. The process of fusion of male and female gamete to form zygote is called as fertilization.
    b. Before, the male gamates are carried to embryo sac by pollen tube (siphonogamous)

    • The pollen tube enter the ovule through:
      1. Chalaza: Chalazogamy or Basigamy/Eg. Casuarina.
      2. Integument Mesogamy/Eg: Cucurbita
      3. Micropyle: Porogamy.
    • Most common one is porogamy.
    c. Most active part of ovule is chalaza.
    d. The pollen tube invariably enters the embryo sac at micropylar end.
    e. Pollen tube secretes pectinases and other hydrolytic enzymes to create a passage for its entry to style.
    f. Movement of pollen tube is chemotactic.
    g. Pollen tube is attracted by Synergids though filliform apparatus.
    h. Pollen tube releases its two male gametes and tube nucleus (degenerating) into the synergid.
    i. Double fertilization is unique characteristic of angiosperm.
    j. In all, 5 nuclei are involved in double fertilization. But the gametes taking part are 3.
    k. Double fertilization and Triple fusion
    • Double fertilization discovered by Nawaschian in Fritilaria and Lilium
    • Among two nucleus formed by small generative nucleus one fuses with secondary to form endosperm and other with egg cell to form zygote. This is character of Angiosperm and is called double fertilization and triple fusion.
    l. Endosperm
    • Endosperm is a characteristic storage tissue, which supplies nutrition to the developing embryo.
    • Being storage tissue, the cells of endosperm are isodiametric, thin-walled and generally without pits.
    • Development of endosperm is from Primary Endosperm Nucleus (i.e., result of triple fusion).
    • Proteinaceous part of maize (most of the cereals) endosperm is aleurone layer.
    • Aleurone layer occurs in the peripheral region of endosperm. [BPKIHS]
    • The endosperm in gymnosperms is pre-fertilization tissue and is equivalent to female gametophyte, hence haploid in nature.
    • But, in angiosperms, it is post-fertilization tissue and is generally triploid in nature.
    • The endosperm in angiosperms is (eg. maize) triploid and formed after fertilization while in case gymnosperms, it is haploid and formed before fertilization. [OM 2003, 2004]

      • Types of Endosperm in Angiosperms

        • Found in Magnoliaceae.

        • It is found in mostly monocots.

      Note: Endosperm is haploid in gymnosperms not in monocots.

      F. Embryo development (Embryogenesis)

      1. a. Fertilized egg is known as zygote (2n), which on division and differentiation gives rise to embryo.
      2. b. This process of development of embryo is called embryogenesis.
      3. c. The main axis of the embryo is called tigellum.
      4. d. An embryo has the capacity to develop into a complete plant (Thus, embryo is future miny or tiny plant inside the seed)
      5. e. Hofmeister proposed the origin of embryo from existing cell of embryo sac not from pollen tube.
        • Embryo development in Dicot
        • Embryo development in Monocot
          • A typical dicot embryo (common type is onagrad type or ranunculaceous type) is having an embryonal axis and 2 cotyledons attached to it laterally.
          • A monocot embryo differs from a dicot embryo mainly in having a single cotyledon (instead of two).
          • The portion of embryonal axis above the point of attachment of cotyledons is called epicotyl and the portion below the point of attachment of cotyledons is called hypocotyl.
          • Terminal cells forms the embryo.
          • Below the point of attachment of cotyledons is called hypocotyl.
          • In grasses (monocots), structure and development of embryo is different from other monocots.
          • At the terminal end of epicotyl is plumule (embryonic shoot) and the hypocotyl at its end bears radicle (embryonic root).
          • A grass embryo (e.g. Triticum or wheat) is having single cotyledon, which is called "Scutellum".
          • The embryonal axis below the attachment of scutellum is called hypocotyl, which at its tip is having radicle, bearing apical meristem and root cap at its lower end.

      Nutrition of Embryo

      Main nutritive tissues for embryo are:

      1. Endosperm
      2. Perisperm: Remnant of nucellus or persistent nucellus surrounding the embryo.
      3. Chalazosperm: Nucellar cells in chalasal region of ovule is called Chalazosperm, which stores fats and starch and is substitue for endosperm. Chalazoperm is found in Cyanastrum.

      Polyembryony

      Process of developing more than one embryo in the same seed.

      Discovered by Leeuwenhoek in citrus.

      Very common in gymnosperms.

      1. Simple polyembryony is presence of more than one embryo sac. Eg: Brassica.
      2. Mixed polyembryony is due to entry of more than one pollen tube in an embryo sac and fusion of extra male gametes with synergids or antipodals Eg: Allium.
      3. Cleavage polyembryony is due to cleavage of the embryo to produce two or more embryos. Eg: orchids.
      4. Adventive polyembryony is due to growth of diploid nucellar or integument cells into embryos. Eg: Citrus.
