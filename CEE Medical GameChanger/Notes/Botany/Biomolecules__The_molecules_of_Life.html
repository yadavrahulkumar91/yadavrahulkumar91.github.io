Biomolecules

Biomolecules are the building blocks of life, forming the intricate machinery that powers every living cell.

A. Introduction

a. Molecules (organic/ inorganic) present in living organisms is known as Biomolecules.

b. Collection of biomolecules in the cells is called Cellular pool.

  • i. Cellular pool is crystallocolloid in nature.
  • ii. Cellular pool contains both Organic (Carbohydrate, Lipid, Protein) and Inorganic (Water, Mineral, Salts, etc) compound.

B. Organic Compounds

a. Carbohydrate

  • i. Introduction
    1. Compounds that contain C, H and O and represented as Cn(H2O)n are carbohydrates i.e., hydrates of carbon.
    2. Carbohydrates contain carbon contained with hydrogen and oxygen often in the ratio as in water (1C:2H:1O).
    3. Carbohydrates are nature's most abundant organic substance.
    4. Nowadays, carbohydrates are defined as optically active polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones which yield monosaccharides on hydrolysis.
    5. Carbohydrates, especially monosaccharides like glucose, fructose, etc. are the most important source of energy for living organisms.
    6. The storage of glucose in liver or muscle as glycogen is known as Glycogenesis.
    7. The regeneration of glucose from glycogen in emergency condition (e.g., starvation, diabetes mellitus) is known as Glycogenolysis.
    8. Generation of glucose from non-carbohydrate source is called as Glucogenesis/ Gluconeogenesis.

b. Lipid

i. Introduction

  1. Lipids are biological molecules insoluble in water, but readily soluble in non-polar organic solvents like chloroform and benzene.
  2. Lipids are not polymers.
  3. They form a heterogenous group but all are made of Carbon, Hydrogen and Oxygen.
  4. The number of oxygen atoms is very small compared to carbon atoms.
  5. True lipids are esters of fatty acids and an alcohol.
    1. Fatty acids: Fatty acids are large molecules containing an acidic group. The general formula is R−COOH.

    Protein

    Introduction

    Proteins are highly complex, natural compounds composed of large number of different α-amino acids joined together with peptide linkage.

    • Amino acids
      • Introduction
        1. Amino acids are amino substituted acids containing both amino(−NH2) and carboxyl(−COOH) groups.
        2. Amino acids are amphoteric in nature and slightly acidic in pH.
        3. Each amino acid contains a centrally located carbon atom called α-carbon to which 4 groups are attached;
          1. An acidic amino group(−NH3+ ),
          2. A basic carboxylic group (−COO− ),
          3. A hydrogen atom, and
          4. A group of varying chemical structures called a side chain (−R).
        4. Zwitter ions:
          1. When protons from the carboxyl group are migrated to the amino group and a dipolar ion of amino acid is formed called Zwitter ions.
          2. A dipolar ion containing both +ve and -ve charge is formed.
        5. Isoelectric point: The pH at which an amino acid will not migrate into an electric field is known as its Isoelectric point.
          1. At isoelectric point, amino acid has no net charge i.e., the amount of negative charge on the amino acid exactly balanced by the amount of +ve charge.
          2. An isoelectric point of each amino acid is constant.
          3. An amino acid has the minimum solubility at its isoelectric point because here concentration of dipolar ion is maximum.

    Proteins

    Protein is the basis of structure and function of life. It is a complex nitrogenous organic compound found in the protoplasm. Animals have relatively more proteins than plants. Proteins are hydrophilic high polymer colloids. All proteins are optically active because of the presence of chiral centres at α-position of the amino acid residue.

    Colour Reactions for Protein Characterization

    Proteins are easily characterized by the following colour reactions:

    1. Biuret Test:

      Protein+10% NaOH+Few drops of CuSO4 → Reddish violet colour

      • Colour depends upon the number of peptide bond present in the protein.

      This test is characteristic of all compounds having a peptide linkage and indicates the presence of peptide linkage in proteins.

    2. Million's Reaction:

      A white ppt is obtained when a protein is treated with Million's reagent(HgNO3 in HNO3 containing a little HNO2) and turns brick red when heated in boiling water.

      • This test is shown by proteins having phenol (e.g. Those having Tyrosine unit).

    3. Ninhydrin Reaction:

      Proteins when treated with pyridine solution of ninhydrin give colour ranging from deep blue to violet pink.

      • Proteins+Pyridine solution of Ninhydrin → Deep blue to Violet pink

    4. Xanthoprotic Test:

      Protein+HNO3 → △ Yellow colour.

    Denaturation of Proteins

    On heating, exposing to ultraviolet radiations or treating with a number of solvents or reagents the proteins are denatured. These changes may be reversible or irreversible and are referred to as denaturation. Denaturation occurs most readily near the isoelectric point. The denatured protein samples may contain an intact primary structure, but their three-dimensional structure is destroyed. Reversal of denaturation is called Renaturation or Refolding. Boiling of an egg is an example of irreversible denaturation. Chemical change of globular protein into fibrous protein takes place.

    Classification of Proteins

    1. On the basis of
      1. Simple proteins:
        • Simple proteins are those which yield only α-amino acids on hydrolysis.
        • Examples:
          • albumins
          • albuminoids
          • globulins
          • glutelins
      2. Conjugated proteins:
        • Conjugated proteins on hydrolysis yield α-amino acids and other non-proteinous substance in addition to α-amino acids.
        • Examples:
          • nucleoproteins
          • glycoproteins
          • phosphoproteins
          • hemoglobins
      3. Derived proteins:
        • These are the products formed by the action of physical, chemical or enzymatic agents of natural proteins.
        • Derived proteins are thus intermediate hydrolysis product of proteins.
        • Examples:
          • peptides
          • proteoses
          • peptones

    Structure of Protein
    Level of Structure Description Bonds/Interactions involved
    Primary structure
    1. Refers to linear sequence of amino acids in a protein molecule.
    2. A protein's primary structure determines the type of function to be carried out.
    		 Peptide bonds
    Secondary structure
    1. Deals with the shape of the individual polypeptide chains present in a protein molecule.
    2. Secondary structure depends on hydrogen bonds between −C=O and −NH group.
    3. Secondary structure of protein molecules involves:
      • α-helices:
        • In α-helix, a single amino acid chain is coiled spirally by establishing hydrogen bonds between first and fourth amino acids.
      • β-sheets:
        • In β-pleated arrangement, a sheet is produced by holding of two or more polypeptide chains in parallel or antiparallel manner.
    		 Hydrogen bonds
    Tertiary structure
    1. When the polypeptide chains of protein molecule bend and fold in order to assume a more compact three-dimensional shape, a tertiary level of structure is formed.
    2. Tertiary structure is due to a variety of bonds and interactions between amino acid chains like:
      • Hydrogen bonds
      • Van der Waal's interactions
      • Disulfide bonds
      • Electrostatic bonds
      • Hydrophobic effect
    		 Hydrogen bonds, Van der Waal's interactions, Disulfide bonds, Electrostatic bonds, and Hydrophobic effect
    Quaternary structure
    1. Quaternary structure of protein is inter-relation between polypeptide chains having more than two peptide chains.
    		 Primarily due to interactions involved in secondary and tertiary structure of protein
