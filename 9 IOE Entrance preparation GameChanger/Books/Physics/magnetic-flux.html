Introduction

Magnetic flux is a concept in electromagnetism that measures the quantity of magnetic field passing through a given surface. It plays a crucial role in understanding electromagnetic phenomena and has practical applications in various areas.

Definition of Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux, denoted by the symbol Φ, is defined as the product of the magnetic field strength (B) and the area (A) perpendicular to the magnetic field:

Φ = B × A

The SI unit of magnetic flux is the Weber (Wb), which is equivalent to tesla-square meter (T·m²).

Flux Through a Surface

The magnetic flux passing through a surface can be calculated using the following formula:

Φ = ∫ B · dA

where B is the magnetic field vector, dA is an infinitesimal area vector, and the integral is taken over the entire surface.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

One of the significant applications of magnetic flux is in Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction. According to this law, a change in magnetic flux through a coil of wire induces an electromotive force (emf) across the coil. The magnitude of the induced emf is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux:

ε = -dΦ/dt

where ε is the induced emf, dΦ/dt is the rate of change of magnetic flux.
