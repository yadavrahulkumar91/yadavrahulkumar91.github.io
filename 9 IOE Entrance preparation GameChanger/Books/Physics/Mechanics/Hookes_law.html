Introduction

Hooke's Law is a fundamental principle in physics that describes the relationship between the force applied to a spring and the resulting displacement of the spring from its equilibrium position. It is named after the 17th-century physicist Robert Hooke.

Statement of Hooke's Law

Hooke's Law can be stated as follows:

"The force exerted by a spring is directly proportional to the displacement of the spring from its equilibrium position, as long as the spring does not exceed its elastic limit."

Mathematical Formulation

Hooke's Law can be mathematically expressed as:

$F = -kx$

where:

  • $F$ is the force applied to the spring
  • $k$ is the spring constant, a measure of the stiffness of the spring
  • $x$ is the displacement of the spring from its equilibrium position

Spring Constant

The spring constant ($k$) determines the stiffness of a spring. It represents the amount of force required to stretch or compress the spring by a certain amount. Springs with higher spring constants are stiffer and require more force to produce the same displacement compared to springs with lower spring constants.

Applications of Hooke's Law

Hooke's Law has various applications in different fields, including:

  • Spring systems, such as in suspension systems and mattresses
  • Elastic materials, such as rubber bands and bungee cords
  • Force measurement devices, such as strain gauges and load cells

Example

Let's work through an example to understand the concept of Hooke's Law:

Example: A spring with a spring constant of 100 N/m is stretched by 0.2 meters. Calculate the force exerted by the spring.

Using Hooke's Law formula:

$F = -kx$

Substituting the given values:

$F = -100 \, \text{N/m} \times 0.2 \, \text{m} = -20 \, \text{N}$

Therefore, the force exerted by the spring is 20 N.
