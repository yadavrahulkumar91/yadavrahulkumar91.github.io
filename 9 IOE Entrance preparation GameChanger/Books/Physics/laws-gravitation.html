Laws of Gravitation

Introduction

In this chapter, we will study the fundamental laws of gravitation that describe the interactions between objects with mass. These laws were first formulated by Sir Isaac Newton in the 17th century and have since been widely accepted in the field of physics.

Law of Universal Gravitation

The law of universal gravitation states that every particle of matter in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

$$F = G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}$$

Where:

  • $F$ is the gravitational force between two objects
  • $G$ is the gravitational constant
  • $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses of the two objects
  • $r$ is the distance between the centers of the two objects

Gravitational Field

The gravitational field is a concept that helps us understand how the presence of a massive object affects the space around it. It is defined as the force experienced by a unit mass placed at a particular point in space. The gravitational field intensity, denoted by $$\vec{g}$$, is given by:

$$\vec{g} = \frac{\vec{F}}{m}$$

Where:

  • $\vec{F}$ is the gravitational force acting on the unit mass
  • $m$ is the mass of the unit mass

MCQs and Answers

  1. Which scientist formulated the law of universal gravitation?
    1. Isaac Newton
    2. Albert Einstein
    3. Nikola Tesla
    4. Galileo Galilei

    Answer: A

  2. What does the law of universal gravitation state?
    1. The force of gravity depends only on the mass of the object
    2. The force of gravity depends only on the distance between objects
    3. The force of gravity depends on both the mass and distance between objects
    4. The force of gravity is independent of mass and distance

    Answer: C

Tricks

  • Trick 1: Remember that the gravitational force is always attractive, pulling objects towards each other.
  • Trick 2: The strength of the gravitational force decreases rapidly as the distance between objects increases, following an inverse square relationship.
