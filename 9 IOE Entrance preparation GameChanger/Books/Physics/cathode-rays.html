Introduction

Cathode rays are streams of electrons that are emitted from the cathode (negative electrode) of a high-vacuum discharge tube. They were first discovered and studied by J.J. Thomson in the late 19th century, and their properties played a significant role in the development of modern physics.

Properties of Cathode Rays

Cathode rays exhibit several interesting properties:

  • Nature: Cathode rays consist of negatively charged particles called electrons.
  • Charge-to-Mass Ratio: Cathode rays have a specific charge-to-mass ratio, which was determined by Thomson to be e/m = -1.76 × 10^8 C/kg.
  • Deflection in Electric and Magnetic Fields: Cathode rays can be deflected by electric and magnetic fields, indicating their charged nature.
  • Particle-like Behavior: Cathode rays travel in straight lines, collide with objects in their path, and produce shadow images.
  • Heat and Luminescence: When cathode rays strike a material, they can generate heat and cause certain substances to emit light (phosphorescence).

Applications of Cathode Rays

Cathode rays have found applications in various areas, including:

  • Cathode Ray Tubes (CRTs): Cathode ray tubes have been used in early television sets and computer monitors to display images.
  • Particle Accelerators: Cathode rays, specifically electrons, are utilized in particle accelerators for research in nuclear and particle physics.
  • Cathode Ray Oscilloscopes (CROs): CROs are electronic devices that utilize cathode rays to display and analyze electrical waveforms.
  • X-ray Production: When cathode rays strike a metal target, they can generate X-rays, which have various applications in medicine and industry.
