Introduction

Ohm's Law is a fundamental principle in electricity that relates the current flowing through a conductor to the voltage across it and the resistance of the conductor. It provides a simple mathematical relationship between these electrical quantities.

Statement of Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law states that the current (I) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage (V) across it and inversely proportional to the resistance (R) of the conductor. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

I = V / R

Units and Notation

In the International System of Units (SI), the unit of current is the Ampere (A), the unit of voltage is the Volt (V), and the unit of resistance is the Ohm (Ω). Ohm's Law can also be expressed using the following notation:

V = I * R

R = V / I

Applications of Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is widely used in various applications in electrical circuits:

  • Circuit Analysis: Ohm's Law allows us to calculate the current flowing through a circuit component or the voltage across it when the resistance is known. It is a fundamental tool for analyzing and designing electrical circuits.
  • Power Calculation: Ohm's Law can be used to calculate the power dissipated in a resistor. The power (P) can be calculated using the formula:

P = I2 * R = V2 / R

  • Voltage Divider: Ohm's Law is used to understand and design voltage divider circuits, which can divide a voltage into smaller values using resistors.
  • Ohmic Devices: Ohm's Law is applicable to ohmic devices, which are devices that obey Ohm's Law and have a linear relationship between current and voltage. Examples include resistors, certain types of conductors, and some semiconductor devices.

Answer Questions

  1. Question 1: What does Ohm's Law relate?

    Answer: Ohm's Law relates the current flowing through a conductor to the voltage across it and the resistance of the conductor.

  2. Question 2: What is the mathematical expression of Ohm's Law?

    Answer: Ohm's Law can be expressed as I = V / R, where I is the current, V is the voltage, and R is the resistance.

  3. Question 3: What are the applications of Ohm's Law?

    Answer: Ohm's Law is used in circuit analysis, power calculation, voltage divider circuits, and for understanding ohmic devices.
